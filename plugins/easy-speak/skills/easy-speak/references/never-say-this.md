# Never say this

Load this whenever the user asks you *about* communication — how to present, how to write, how people learn, what the research says.

This field is unusually polluted. The most quotable claims in it are the ones that died. Each entry below was killed by adversarial verification against the primary source.

**The pattern to internalize:** most of these are not lies. They are **one step of overreach on a real finding**. Flattening them into "true" or "false" loses exactly the thing you needed to know — so each entry says what survives *and* what does not.

---

## The three famous myths

### "Communication is 7% words, 38% tone, 55% body language"

**The numbers were never measured.** They appear exactly once in the primary literature — as a *suggested* extrapolation in the final Discussion paragraph of Mehrabian & Ferris (1967), combining results across two separate studies, **neither of which compared all three channels.** The paper's own words: "It is suggested that the combined effect... is a weighted sum of their independent effects — with the coefficients of .07, .38, and .55" and "analytic procedures... can presently be employed to **test** this proposed model."

The actual experiment held the verbal channel constant at the single neutral word *"maybe"*, and "body language" was **head-only still photographs**. Twenty subjects produced the weights.

**Mehrabian's own standing caveat:** the equations apply *only* to communications of **feelings and attitudes**, *and* only where the channels are **inconsistent**. He calls the popular reading absurd — outside that narrow case, "the verbal part is nearly 100% of the message."

When all three channels were finally measured together (2025), the shares came out roughly **48 / 31 / 21** — only the rank order replicated, never the magnitudes.

**Do not replace it with a different channel-weighting constant.** None is supported.

### "The average attention span is 8 seconds — shorter than a goldfish"

Trace the citation chain and it terminates in a void:

1. Microsoft Canada's 2015 consumer report presents the figure on an infographic whose only attribution is *"Source: Statistic Brain."*
2. Statistic Brain's page names no study, no author, no method, no sample. The number is **not even stable across archived snapshots** of the same page.
3. BBC *More or Less* contacted every organisation Statistic Brain listed. None had any record of the research. Statistic Brain never replied.

The only real study anywhere in the chain measured **web-page dwell time**, not attention: median stay on search-result pages was 8.0 seconds. Its authors warned it is an upper bound. That is almost certainly where "8 seconds" came from.

Researchers reject the premise, not just the number: **there is no such quantity as an "average attention span."** Attention is task-dependent; psychologists would not try to express it as a fixed scalar.

**Do not substitute a different number of seconds.** The defensible construct is the **vigilance decrement** — detection of rare signals degrades over **tens of minutes to hours**, not seconds.

### "Match the explanation to their learning style"

Pashler et al. specify exactly what would validate this — a **crossover interaction**: group learners by style, randomly assign each to different instructional methods, and show that each style group does best under its matched method. Scouring a literature they call "enormous," they found essentially **zero** studies meeting the bar, and found sound studies **contradicting** it.

**Be scrupulous about the scope, because almost every retelling botches it.** They do *not* claim style preferences are fictitious, and they do not claim people are cognitively identical. People do reliably report preferring one presentation mode. **What fails is the *meshing hypothesis*** — the inference that matching instruction to that preference improves learning.

Do not say "learning styles don't exist." Say: adding a diagram is fine; *justifying* it by their style is not, and *withholding* text because of their style is not.

## Claims from the real literature that did not survive

### Effect sizes and magic numbers

**Any Mayer box-score effect size** ("23 of 23 tests, d = 0.86"; "22 of 22, d = 1.10"; "16 of 16"; "9 of 9, d = 1.22"). These are **vote-counts from a self-review by the theory's own author** — no random-effects pooling, no heterogeneity test, no publication-bias correction, no confidence intervals, and unstable across editions of the same chapter. The "23 of 23 supported" tally even contains a d = −0.17 entry favouring the *embellished* lesson.

Use the independent meta-analyses instead:

| Principle | Real pooled effect |
|---|---|
| All multimedia design principles | **g = 0.38** [0.27, 0.49] |
| Spatial contiguity | **g = 0.63** [0.55, 0.71], k = 58 |
| Signaling | **g+ = 0.53** retention / **0.33** transfer, k = 103 |
| Verbal redundancy | **g = 0.15** |

**"Working memory holds N slots, so use N bullets."** Three independent reasons this is wrong. The ~4 figure is a *pure-capacity* estimate observable only under lab conditions that deliberately block rehearsal, recoding and long-term-memory grouping — conditions no listener in a real conversation is in. The slot-vs-resource mechanism is contested. And capacity is over **knowledge-relative chunks**, so the number of presentable things is a function of the audience, not a constant. Miller himself called "seven" a *"pernicious, Pythagorean coincidence."*

Correcting 7 to 4 while keeping the habit of budgeting by raw item count reproduces the original error with a smaller number.

**"Teach-back has an effect size of d = ..."** citing the 20-study review. Fabrication — that review states verbatim that "given the heterogeneity of outcome measures, it was not possible to conduct a meta-analysis." What you may say: **19 of 20 studies, 9 RCTs, five outcome domains.**

### Mechanisms you cannot narrate

**"This reduces cognitive load."** No standardized instrument backs that sentence. Cognitive Load Theory's intrinsic/extraneous/germane split is not cleanly separable — the extraneous-vs-germane definition is tautological by its own critical literature, and load is routinely inferred *backwards* from test scores. For spatial split-attention specifically, a review of **41 comparisons** found that load measures **"largely do not support the extraneous cognitive load explanation."**

The behavioural effects are real. The story everyone tells about *why* is not established. Say what you did to the chunk structure instead.

*(Do not flatten this into "load theory is dead" — load* was *significantly reduced for signaling.)*

**"The expert hears the full orchestration in their head."** Newton never measured vividness, and her own informed-observer condition contradicts the story.

**"Experts are literally unable to imagine not knowing."** The paper always cited for this **explicitly rejects** its own pure-bias hypothesis. The finding is *insufficient adjustment*, not incapacity. Say "systematically and persistently miscalibrated."

### Structure and writing rules

**"The Federal Plain Language Guidelines mandate BLUF."** They *recommend* it. The document self-describes as **advice**; the words "binding" and "mandatory" appear zero times in 118 pages; and the Plain Writing Act expressly forecloses judicial review.

**"Paragraphs should be 150 words / never over 250 / one idea per sentence."** The FPLG does state these — but **attributes them to third parties** ("*Writing experts recommend*..."), sourced to two legal-writing style manuals with **no empirical study**. And they ship with hedges that are always dropped when quoted: an explicit warning that uniform paragraph lengths are "choppy," and "there is nothing wrong with an occasional one-sentence paragraph."

**These are English-only artifacts.** "Word" is not a cross-linguistically stable unit, and Korean, Japanese and Turkish encode multiple ideas per orthographic sentence as the unmarked norm.

**"Users don't read, they scan"** — and its cousin, *"screen readers scan."* The second is a **category error** that would encode a false accessibility claim: screen readers *linearize*. The scanning statistics themselves (58% / 47% / 27% / 124%) come from a non-peer-reviewed 1997 vendor report with roughly **10 participants per cell**, one fictitious website, never replicated — and the largest effect **reversed** in an independent test.

**"Marketese degrades comprehension and destroys trust."** In the source study, the *only* significant difference was **subjective satisfaction**. Task time, errors and memory all failed to reach significance, and **trust was never measured**. Say "readers report preferring objective phrasing." Nothing more.

**"Kieras proved conclusion-first doesn't work."** Also refuted — his "bad" versions *deleted* the conclusion rather than *moving* it. You get no valid evidence for BLUF and none against it.

### Comprehension checking

**"People cannot self-assess comprehension."** Restricted to **explanatory/mechanistic** knowledge. The source's own abstract: the illusion "is far stronger for explanatory knowledge than many other kinds of knowledge, such as that for facts, procedures or narratives." There was **no drop** for procedures or narratives.

**"Preparing to teach wears off after a week."** Refuted by a 28-study meta-analysis that names the claim and rejects it: the *delayed* effect (g = 0.53) was **larger** than the immediate one (g = 0.28).

**"Never use a multiple-choice comprehension check."** Contradicted by the very paper cited for it: "post-hoc tests found no significant differences among them."

**"Open-ended checks beat closed-ended ones."** The closed Yes/No scored *numerically higher* than the open directive tell-back. The lever is **collaborative framing**, not open-endedness.

### Readability

**"This reads at grade X, therefore it is understandable."** No readability score — traditional, machine-learning, commercial, or LLM-produced — is validated as evidence of clarity, and all are English artifacts by construction.

**But do not overreach in the other direction either.** They do not have *zero* predictive validity, and "LLMs cannot assess readability" is broader than the evidence supports. The surviving claim is narrow and sufficient: **readability scores may never be a target metric or proof of clarity.**

### Pragmatics

**Gricean maxims as a style checklist.** Grice himself excludes "be polite" from the conversational maxims and calls his own scheme "too narrow." The maxims describe **rational cooperation**, and they are *flouted* to generate implicature — they are expectations hearers exploit, not instructions writers follow.

Also: paraphrasing them as "be clear, be brief, be honest, be relevant" silently deletes the second maxim of Quantity — **do not be *more* informative than required.**

And their universality is contested: primary ethnographic fieldwork (Malagasy) and Wierzbicka's cross-cultural pragmatics both charge the scheme with Anglocentrism.

## The meta-rule

**Never let a claim inherit more confidence than its source.**

The academic layer here is strong — primary, peer-reviewed, quote-verified. The practitioner layer is weak: the two most-cited practitioner sources in this field both collapsed under verification.

When you cannot cite it, do not assert it. When it is a heuristic, say the word "heuristic."
