# Explaining: analogy, examples, and the cost of jargon

> **What this is.** The constructive half of the skill: how to *build* an explanation — pick an analogy that actually explains, supply enough examples that the point transfers, and price the technical terms you were about to use anyway.
> **Evidence base.** 5 confirmed claims (four unanimous 3-0, one split 2-1) and 2 refuted claims from the adversarial verification pass on the explaining dossier, plus supporting findings from the pass-1 synthesis. The two refuted claims are the most valuable content in this file: both are real findings that were *overreached*, and the coaching rules people usually derive from them are wrong in specific, nameable ways.
> **Load this when.** The coaching task involves constructing an explanation — choosing or auditing an analogy, deciding how many examples to give, deciding whether to keep a colorful aside "for engagement," or deciding what to do with a technical term.

---

## 1. The operational core

Five rules. Each is stated first, then evidenced. Nothing here is asserted that is not traceable to the dossier.

| # | Rule | Status of the evidence |
|---|---|---|
| R1 | An analogy must map **relations**, not looks. Discard surface attributes on purpose. | CONFIRMED 3-0, primary source |
| R2 | Run the **relational-skeleton test** on every analogy. If nothing survives the strip, it is decoration, not explanation. | CONFIRMED 3-0, primary source |
| R3 | **Signal the mapping explicitly.** An analogy placed nearby and left implicit is mostly wasted. | CONFIRMED 3-0, primary source |
| R4 | Give **two** structurally-matched, surface-different examples and ask what they share. One example plus a statement of the principle does not reliably work. | CONFIRMED 3-0, primary source |
| R5 | **Cut the interesting-but-irrelevant material.** You cannot compensate for it with emphasis. | CONFIRMED 2-1 — direction holds, magnitude and universality are contested; see §6 |

And two things you were probably about to assert that the evidence does not support at the strength usually claimed: **concreteness fading** (§5) and **"defining jargon doesn't help"** (§7).

## 2. R1 — An analogy maps relations and discards attributes

Gentner's mapping rules are *literally ordered* in the source, and the order is the instruction:

1. **Discard attributes of objects.**
2. **Try to preserve relations between objects.**
3. **Prefer systems of relations** (the Systematicity Principle).

> "An analogy is a comparison in which relational predicates, but few or no object attributes, can be mapped from base to target."
> — (Gentner 1983, [confirmed 3-0](https://groups.psych.northwestern.edu/gentner/papers/Gentner83.2b.pdf))

The verifier retrieved the author-hosted PDF directly and confirmed the numbered mapping rules appear on the page in exactly that order, and that the worked example below is **Gentner's own**, not a later gloss:

> "EXAMPLE 2. The hydrogen atom is like our solar system. INTERPRETATION: Intended inferences concern chiefly the relational structure: e.g., 'The electron REVOLVES AROUND the nucleus, just as the planets REVOLVE AROUND the sun,' but not 'The nucleus is YELLOW, MASSIVE, etc., like the sun.'"
> — (Gentner 1983, [confirmed 3-0](https://groups.psych.northwestern.edu/gentner/papers/Gentner83.2b.pdf))

**Coaching form.** When she proposes an analogy, ask what *does what to what* in the base, and whether the same *does-what-to-what* holds in the target. Do not ask whether the two things feel alike. Looks, size, color, and "domain feel" are precisely what rule (1) tells you to throw away.

---

## 3. R2 — The named failure mode: the "mere appearance match"

This is the mechanically checkable test, and it has a name in the source.

> "Such a match is considered a mere appearance match. Unlike the comparisons considered so far, these matches do not involve relational mappings. Although they can be appealing and locally useful, their explanatory power is sharply limited."
> — (Gentner 1983, [confirmed 3-0](https://groups.psych.northwestern.edu/gentner/papers/Gentner83.2b.pdf))

Keep the hedge: Gentner says such matches **"can be appealing and locally useful"** — she does not say they are worthless, she says their *explanatory power* is sharply limited. A mere appearance match is allowed to be a mnemonic. It is not allowed to be your explanation.

Gentner's own examples of the category: **"A sunflower looks like the sun."** and **"The symbol for infinity is a sidewise 8."** Both have strong overlap in object attributes and zero overlap in relations.

### The test, as a procedure

Strip the candidate analogy down to its relational skeleton — *X causes Y, which constrains Z*. If nothing survives the strip, you have decoration.

| Candidate | Strip to the relational skeleton | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| "The hydrogen atom is like our solar system." | REVOLVES-AROUND(electron, nucleus) ← REVOLVES-AROUND(planet, sun). Survives. Discarded on the way: YELLOW(sun), MASSIVE(sun). | Explanatory analogy |
| "A sunflower looks like the sun." | Nothing. Only attribute overlap (round, yellow, radiating). | Mere appearance match — memorable, not explanatory |

This is the single cheapest audit in the file: it is a yes/no question with a written artifact (the skeleton), and it can be run on an analogy in about ten seconds. **Does the analogy share RELATIONS, or just LOOKS?**

The danger is that a mere appearance match *feels apt*. Aptness of feel is not the criterion, and the coached speaker's own sense of aptness is not admissible evidence — that is exactly the failure mode the test exists to catch.

## 4. R3 + R4 — Transfer is near-catastrophically bad, and one example does not fix it

### R3: an implicit analogy is mostly wasted

In Gick & Holyoak's paradigm, subjects read a fortress/army story and then faced Duncker's radiation problem — structurally the same problem.

> "Under these conditions only about 30% of our subjects produced the convergence solution, as opposed to about 75% who produced it when a hint was given."
> — (Gick & Holyoak 1983, [confirmed 3-0](https://gwern.net/doc/psychology/1983-gick.pdf))

The authors' own next sentence, quoted by the verifier from the primary PDF: assuming ~10% would solve it with no analogy at all, "only a third or less of the subjects who could potentially apply the analogy spontaneously noticed it. We found this striking gap between noticing and application quite surprising."

**Coaching form.** Do not place an analogy near the point and hope. Say the mapping out loud: *"the same thing is happening here: X plays the role of Y."* People who had the analogy sitting in their heads, having just read it, mostly failed to reach for it.

### R4: two examples, not one

Gick & Holyoak's Part I threw everything at a *single* analog — summarization instructions, an explicit verbal statement of the underlying principle, and a diagram of it:

> "The studies in Part I attempted to foster the abstraction of a problem schema from a single story analog by means of summarization instructions, a verbal statement of the underlying principle, or a diagrammatic representation of it. None of these devices achieved a notable degree of sucess [sic]. In contrast, the experiments in Part II demonstrated that if two prior analogs were given, subjects often derived a problem schema as an incidental product of describing the similarities of the analogs. The quality of the induced schema was highly predictive of subsequent transfer performance."
> — (Gick & Holyoak 1983, [confirmed 3-0](https://gwern.net/doc/psychology/1983-gick.pdf))

The verifier pulled the corroborating body text: "These all failed to facilitate transfer from a single analog... even though the verbal statement amounted to an explicit statement of such a schema." And the decisive reversal — the verbal statements and diagrams that failed with one analog **"proved highly beneficial when paired with two."**

The paper's own summary of the comparison: **"It was clearly the case that two analogs were better than one."**

**This is the actionable rule, and it is unusually concrete:** give at least **two** structurally-matched, surface-*different* examples, and explicitly ask what they share. The schema is induced as a side effect of the comparison — not by you announcing the principle. Announcing the principle over one example is exactly the intervention that was tested and failed.

## 6. Concreteness fading — what people believe vs. what the evidence actually says

**REFUTED (0-3).** Unanimous. This is a real, correctly-quoted recommendation that gets promoted, in transit, from *"we recommend"* to *"the evidence shows."*

**What people believe (and what the skill was tempted to assert):** the concrete-vs-abstract debate is a false dichotomy; the evidence-backed recommendation is a sequence — start concrete, then explicitly fade to the abstract; neither pole alone wins; Fyfe et al. name four *mechanisms* by which it works.

**What the source actually says** — quoted verbatim, and verified word-for-word by both refuters against ERIC and the publisher:

> "Specifically, we recommend beginning with concrete materials and then explicitly and gradually fading to the more abstract. Theoretical benefits of this 'concreteness fading' technique for mathematics and science instruction include (1) helping learners interpret ambiguous or opaque abstract symbols in terms of well-understood concrete objects, (2) providing embodied perceptual and physical experiences that can ground abstract thinking, (3) enabling learners to build up a store of memorable images that can be used when abstract symbols lose meaning, and (4) guiding learners to strip away extraneous concrete properties and distill the generic, generalizable properties."
> — (Fyfe, McNeil, Son & Goldstone 2014, [refuted 0-3](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ1036777))

**What survives:**

- The recommendation exists and is correctly attributed: **"we recommend"** beginning concrete and **explicitly and gradually** fading to the abstract. The word "explicitly" is the operationally useful part — a fade that is never announced leaves the learner stranded in the example.
- Benefit (4) — fading "guiding learners to strip away extraneous concrete properties and distill the generic, generalizable properties" — is a coherent rationale, and it is *the same shape* as Gentner's mapping rule (1) and as the two-example schema-induction result. Those two findings are separately confirmed and can carry the weight on their own.

**What does NOT survive:**

1. **The hedge was stripped.** The authors' own words are **"THEORETICAL benefits"** and **"we argue for"** / **"we recommend."** They are not demonstrated mechanisms. Calling them "mechanisms" or the recommendation "evidence-backed" misreports the source. Fyfe & Nathan themselves later (Educational Review 2019, 71(4), 403–422, doi:10.1080/00131911.2018.1424116) concede concreteness fading is an **underspecified theory of instruction with "critical gaps,"** and wrote that paper specifically to supply "testable hypotheses" — five years after the review, the authors did not consider the mechanism set settled.
2. **"Neither pole alone wins" is contradicted by later experiments.** Jaakkola & Veermans (2018, *Instructional Science*, doi:10.1007/s11251-017-9428-y, N=127 elementary students, electric circuits): "learning with constantly concrete representations either took less time or resulted in better learning compared to concreteness fading," and "the concrete condition succeeded at least as well as the fading condition on transfer tasks." They explicitly write that their results question the effectiveness of concreteness fading.
3. **Domain generality is contested.** Kokkonen & Schalk, "One Instructional Sequence Fits all? A Conceptual Analysis of the Applicability of Concreteness Fading in Mathematics, Physics, Chemistry, and Biology Education" (*Educational Psychology Review*) is cited by both refuters as a direct challenge to treating the sequence as a universal instructional recipe.
4. One further counter-citation (Kaminski et al.) is named by a refuter but the dossier text is truncated at that point; it is therefore **not** reproduced here as a claim.

**Coaching form.** You may still say: *"start with the concrete case, then say out loud what the general rule is — do not leave the abstraction implicit."* Say it as **a recommendation with a plausible rationale**, not as a proven sequence, and never claim "neither pole alone wins." If she wants a rule with actual experimental support behind the same goal, use the two-example rule (§4) — that one has the data.

## 7. Seductive details — cut the fun bit, and you cannot buy it back with emphasis

**CONFIRMED, but 2-1 (split).** The direction holds; the *magnitude* and *universality* are what the dissenting verifier attacked, and those attacks are legitimate. State the rule; carry the qualifications.

> "In 4 experiments, students who read expository passages with seductive details (i.e., interesting but irrelevant adjuncts) recalled significantly fewer main ideas and generated significantly fewer problem-solving transfer solutions than those who read passages without seductive details. In Experiments 1, 2, and 3, revising the passage to include either highlighting of the main ideas, a statement of learning objectives, or signaling, respectively, did not reduce the seductive details effect."
> — (Harp & Mayer 1998, [confirmed 2-1](https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-0663.90.3.414))

Verified independently: **N = 357 undergraduates** (ERIC record EJ576496), materials were an illustrated English text on the process of lightning formation.

**The part that kills the usual workaround.** The instinct is "keep the fun anecdote, but flag the important part harder." That exact fix was tested three separate ways — **highlighting the main ideas, stating learning objectives, and signaling** — and all three **failed to reduce the effect**. Only removing the detail, or moving it to the very end, helped. (Experiment 4: beginning placement *exacerbated* the effect; end placement *reduced* it — the source says reduced, not eliminated.)

**The proposed mechanism** — and keep the hedge, the source says *suggest*, so we say *proposed* — is **diversion**: seductive details "interfere with learning by priming inappropriate schemas around which readers organize the material, rather than by distracting the reader or by disrupting the coherence of the passage." The reader builds the wrong frame and then hangs the whole passage on it.

**The dissent (the 1 vote against), which you must carry:**

- **The effect is not unconditional.** Rey (2012, *Educational Research Review* 7:216–237, doi:10.1016/j.edurev.2012.05.003), meta-analysis of 39 experimental effects: the pooled effect is significant but **small-to-medium** — retention d=0.30 (99% CI 0.20–0.39), transfer d=0.48 (99% CI 0.34–0.61) — a magnitude the strong form of the claim never states. **11 of 39 studies supported the effect; 15 did not.**
- **A failed replication on the identical material.** Kuhl et al. (2019) failed to observe the seductive detail effect using the same well-traveled lightning content that produced it in Harp & Mayer (1997, 1998) — reported in Tislar & Steelman (2021), *Brain and Behavior*, doi:10.1002/brb3.2322.
- **Moderators.** Rey finds the effect moderated by time limits, detail type, and domain. Sundararajan & Adesope (2020) found **LANGUAGE itself moderated effect sizes**.

**Coaching form.** *Cut the entertaining tangent.* That instruction is directionally supported by a primary study and a meta-analysis and survives the dissent. What does **not** survive is selling it as a large, universal, language-invariant law. If she pushes back — "but it keeps them engaged" — the honest answer is: the engagement fix was tested and did not rescue comprehension; the tangent is not free; and if you truly cannot cut it, the only placement with evidence behind it is **the very end**.

## 8. Jargon — the narrow finding that survives, and the overreach that does not

**REFUTED (1-2), as an overreach.** This is the most delicate entry in the file. The numbers are genuine, verified verbatim from the author-hosted PDF by all three verifiers. What failed was the *rule* built on top of them.

### The claim as usually stated (and as the skill was tempted to ship it)

> "Defining jargon does not rescue it. Jargon depresses processing fluency; providing definitions has no effect and no interaction. The damage is metacognitive: jargon makes reading FEEL hard, that feeling is misattributed to the topic, and it drives motivated resistance, higher risk perception and lower support. **Skill rule: replace the jargon with plain wording; a parenthetical gloss does not buy back the cost.**"

### What the source actually reports (verbatim, triple-verified)

> "As predicted, there was a significant main effect for jargon, F(1, 636) = 76.03, p < .001, η² = .11, such that those in the jargon condition (M = 4.57, SD = 1.11) reported significantly lower processing fluency than those in the no-jargon condition (M = 5.27, SD = 0.90). In addition, consistent with expectations, there was not a significant main effect for definition condition, F(1, 636) = 0.37, p = .543, η² = .0005, nor a significant interaction effect, F(1, 636) = 0.17, p = .678, η² = .0002."
> — (Bullock, Colón Amill, Shulman & Dixon 2019, *Public Understanding of Science* 28(7), 845–853, [refuted-as-overreached 1-2](https://comm.osu.edu/sites/comm.osu.edu/files/PUS%202019-%20Bullock%20et%20al..pdf))

Design confirmed: **N = 650**, Qualtrics online **general-population panel in the United States**, 2 (jargon vs. no-jargon) × 2 (definitions vs. no-definitions) between-subjects. Same dataset as Shulman, Dixon, Bullock & Colón Amill (2020, *JLSP* 39(5–6), 579–597).

### THE NARROW FINDING THAT SURVIVES — state this and no more

> In one English-language experiment (N=650, U.S. general-population panel), text containing jargon produced **significantly lower self-reported processing fluency** than the same text without jargon (η² = .11 — a substantial effect). In that experiment, **making definitions available did not restore processing fluency**: no main effect, no interaction, both null with effect sizes at the fourth decimal place.

That is the whole surviving finding. It is about **self-reported processing fluency** — how hard the text *felt* — under **one specific definitions manipulation**.

### What does NOT survive (the refuters' reasoning, reproduced)

1. **The definitions manipulation was NOT a parenthetical gloss.** From the paper's own Method section: *"Definitions were provided using a mouseover text feature. In this condition, participants were told they could scroll over underlined [terms]..."* — i.e. definitions were **optional, opt-in, hover-to-reveal**. So the skill rule "a parenthetical gloss does not buy back the cost" **misdescribes the manipulation that was actually run**. This experiment tested whether *making a definition available on demand* rescues fluency. It did not test an inline, unavoidable, plain-wording gloss. You cannot conclude the latter from the former.
2. **The null is specific to the processing-fluency measure.** The refuters point to the same paper's Results (p. 6), where the definition condition **was** found to be significant on at least one other outcome. The dossier's quotation is truncated at that point, so this document deliberately does **not** state which outcome or in which direction — only that a blanket "definitions do nothing" is contradicted by the source's own data. *If the skill ever needs to make a positive claim about what definitions DO help, that must be re-verified against the primary PDF first.*
3. **Mechanism mismatch.** The "damage is metacognitive → misattributed to the topic" story was challenged as not established by this study. (The dossier's refutation text is truncated here; the supporting verifier separately confirmed the paper *does* report downstream indirect effects — motivated resistance to persuasion B = −.21 [−.28, −.15]; risk perceptions B = −.11 [−.15, −.07]; support B = .08 [.06, .12].) **Report the indirect effects as the paper's reported findings; do not narrate the misattribution mechanism as an established fact.**

### What the skill should actually say about jargon

- **Prefer plain wording over a technical term the listener does not already own.** This is supported, but the load argument — not the definitions argument — is what carries it: working-memory capacity is limited over **chunks grounded in the receiver's long-term knowledge**, so the same message costs a novice many chunks and an expert one; this is the mechanism by which jargon inflates load (Cowan 2001, [confirmed 3-0](https://www.cambridge.org/core/services/aop-cambridge-core/content/view/44023F1147D4A1D44BDC0AD226838496/S0140525X01003922a.pdf/the-magical-number-4-in-short-term-memory-a-reconsideration-of-mental-storage-capacity.pdf), via the pass-1 synthesis). A term the audience does not hold is a new chunk to be carried; a plain phrase they already hold is free.
- **Do not tell her "definitions are useless."** Tell her the narrow thing: *in the one experiment we have, hover-to-reveal definitions did not make jargon-laden text feel any easier to read.* Feeling easier is not the same as being understood, and an on-demand definition is not the same as plain wording.
- **Jargon is audience-relative, not a word list.** Plain language is audience-relative, not universal simplification — "write for your audience... Take your audience's current level of knowledge, expertise, and interest into account. Don't write for an 8th-grade class if your readers are PhD candidates" (U.S. Federal Plain Language Guidelines, [confirmed 3-0](https://digital.gov/guides/plain-language/principles)). A term that is jargon for one audience is a free, pre-grounded chunk for another.

## 9. Language scope — what generalizes and what does not

- **The jargon finding carries an explicit English-specific artifact risk, flagged in the dossier.** Stimuli were English paragraphs; jargon was operationalized as English technical/scientific terms **plus acronyms**, and the no-jargon condition replaced acronyms with their full form. Processing fluency was measured with items like *"A lot of the terms felt familiar to me."* English scientific vocabulary is largely opaque Greco-Latinate borrowing, so an English jargon term is phonologically and morphologically alien to a lay reader. **Do not port the magnitude (η² = .11) to languages whose technical vocabulary is built from transparent native morphemes.** The *direction* — an unowned term costs the listener — is defensible via the chunk argument, which is language-neutral. The *size* is not.
- **The seductive-details effect is not a language-invariant constant.** All Harp & Mayer materials were English expository science texts read by U.S. undergraduates, and **Sundararajan & Adesope (2020) found language itself moderated effect sizes.** The dossier's instruction is explicit: *do not present the effect as a language-invariant constant.*
- **The dossier does not adjudicate the cross-linguistic scope of structure-mapping or of schema induction from two examples.** Do not claim they are universal; do not claim they are English-specific. Both are stated at the level of cognitive structure (relations, schemas), not at the level of surface linguistic form, but that is an argument, not a verified finding, and this document will not assert it as one.
- **Never use a readability score as the jargon check.** Readability formulas — and frontier-LLM readability judgments — are poor predictors of real reading ease, and the finding itself was validated only in English ([confirmed 3-0](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.11150)). A readability score may be a rough smell-test. It is never proof of clarity and never a target metric.

## What this does NOT license

Each item below is a defensible claim that becomes a false one if pushed one step further. Do not push.

1. **"Defining jargon does not help."** REFUTED as an overreach. What was tested was *optional mouseover definitions*, and what was measured was *self-reported processing fluency*. The paper's own results report the definition condition as significant on at least one other outcome. Say: *"in the one experiment available, hover-to-reveal definitions did not restore how easy the text felt"* — and stop there.
2. **"A parenthetical gloss doesn't buy back the cost."** Not tested. The study did not run an inline parenthetical gloss. Asserting this is inventing a result.
3. **"Jargon makes it feel hard, and readers misattribute that feeling to the topic."** The mechanism narration did not survive verification at the strength claimed. Report the reported indirect effects if you must; do not narrate the mechanism as established.
4. **"Concreteness fading is evidence-backed."** REFUTED 0-3. The authors say *theoretical* benefits and *we recommend*. A later experiment (Jaakkola & Veermans 2018) found constantly-concrete representations matched or beat fading, including on transfer. The authors themselves later called the theory underspecified with "critical gaps."
5. **"Neither pole alone wins — you must fade."** Directly contradicted. Do not say it.
6. **"Seductive details are catastrophic / removing them is a large universal win."** The pooled effect is **small-to-medium** (retention d=0.30; transfer d=0.48), 11 of 39 studies supported it and 15 did not, there is a failed replication on the identical lightning material, and language moderates the effect size. Keep the *rule* (cut the tangent — the workaround of highlighting/objectives/signaling demonstrably fails). Drop the *drama*.
7. **"Mere appearance matches are worthless."** Gentner's own hedge: they **"can be appealing and locally useful."** Their *explanatory* power is sharply limited. A vivid surface match may still be a fine mnemonic; it may not be your explanation.
8. **"One good example plus a clear statement of the principle is enough."** This is the exact intervention Gick & Holyoak tested — three ways — and it failed. Two structurally-matched, surface-different examples, then ask what they share.
9. **"Put the analogy in and the reader will apply it."** ~30% did without a hint, versus ~75% with one. Signal the mapping explicitly or expect it to be wasted.
10. **"Working memory holds ~4 items, so give at most 4 things."** Do not derive this from the chunking argument used in §8. The ~4 figure is a pure-capacity estimate valid only under Cowan's boundary conditions (rehearsal and recoding blocked), the underlying mechanism is contested, and capacity is over **knowledge-relative chunks**. The correct move is to reduce the chunk count *for this audience* (pre-teach labels, group by familiar structure), never to enforce a magic number.
11. **A readability score as evidence that the jargon is gone.** Never a target metric.
