# Plain language, and the readability trap

> **What this is.** The standards layer of plain language (what ISO and the U.S. Federal Plain Language Guidelines actually say, hedges included, with page numbers), a hard prohibition on readability formulas as a target metric, and the one difficulty signal that is mechanically checkable *and* cross-linguistically supported.
> **Evidence base.** 17 confirmed claims, 12 refuted claims, from the adversarial verification pass, plus 10 unverified claims quarantined at the end.
> **Load this when.** The coaching task involves written text, a plain-language rewrite, a "make this simpler" request, a request to score or certify a text's clarity, or any moment when a user reaches for a grade-level / Flesch-Kincaid / Lexile number as evidence that a rewrite worked.

---

## Part 1 — Standards

### 1.1 The governing rule: plain language is audience-relative, not universal simplification

Plain language does not mean "make it simple." It means "make it match this audience." The Federal Plain Language Guidelines (FPLG) put the audience rule first and derive everything else from it (Federal Plain Language Guidelines, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://wid.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/FederalPLGuidelines.pdf)):

> "The first rule of plain language is: write for your audience. Use language your audience knows and feels comfortable with. Take your audience's current level of knowledge into account."
> — FPLG, March 2011, Rev. 1 May 2011, printed p.1 (PDF p.7), Section I "Think about your audience"

**Operational rule.** Before rewriting anything, fix *who* is reading. There is no audience-independent "plain" version of a text. A fixed readability-grade target is a misapplication of the standard, not an implementation of it (FPLG, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://digital.gov/guides/plain-language/principles)).

### 1.2 Correcting the "FPLG says 8th-grade level is a myth" claim

This is the single most-repeated line about the FPLG, and it is **partially false as usually stated**.

| What people say | What the FPLG actually designates as the myth |
|---|---|
| "The FPLG says writing to an 8th-grade level is a myth." | The FPLG names the **universal dumbing-down** claim as the myth: that you must dumb content down "so that everyone everywhere can read it." |
| Implied: grade-level targeting is itself rejected. | Grade-level targeting is made strictly **audience-conditional** — *required* when the audience genuinely is 8th graders, *wrong* when it is not. |

Verbatim (FPLG printed p.1 / PDF p.7, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://wid.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/FederalPLGuidelines.pdf)):

> "One of the most popular plain language myths is that you have to 'dumb down' your content so that everyone everywhere can read it. That's not true. The first rule of plain language is: write for your audience... Don't write for an 8th grade class if your audience is composed of PhD candidates, small business owners, working parents, or immigrants. **Only write for 8th graders if your audience is, in fact, an 8th grade class.**"

So the FPLG rejects a *fixed universal grade-level target*, not grade-level targeting as such. The rule it substitutes is "write for your audience." Note that the dossier's own earlier claim ([CONFIRMED 3-0](https://plainlanguage.gov/guidelines/), phrased as "explicitly designate the blanket 'write to an 8th-grade level' prescription a myth") carries the same imprecision the later, page-level verification corrects. Use the corrected form.

**Citation hygiene (mandatory).** `https://plainlanguage.gov/guidelines/` now 301-redirects to a digital.gov landing page that does **not** contain the quoted text. Cite either the archived PDF (`https://wid.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/FederalPLGuidelines.pdf`, or the Dept. of Education mirror `https://ies.ed.gov/sites/default/files/rel-central/document/2024/10/Federal%20Plain%20Language%20Guidelines.pdf`) or the live successor page (`https://digital.gov/guides/plain-language/principles`).

### 1.3 ISO 24495-1:2023 exists — and concedes its examples are English-only

Plain-language guidance is standards-backed, not style-guide folklore (ISO catalogue, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://www.iso.org/standard/78907.html)):

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Standard | ISO 24495-1:2023, *Plain language — Part 1: Governing principles and guidelines* |
| Technical committee | ISO/TC 37 |
| Edition | 1 |
| Pages | 14 |
| Published | 2023-06-20 |
| Stage | 60.60 (International Standard published) |
| Abstract | "This document establishes governing principles and guidelines for developing plain language documents. The guidelines detail how the principles are interpreted and applied." |

The language-scope sentence is the load-bearing one for any multilingual skill (ISO 24495-1:2023 abstract, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://www.iso.org/standard/78907.html)):

> "This document applies to most, if not all, written languages, **but it provides examples only in English**."

**Operational rule.** Generalize ISO's *principles* across languages. Do **not** generalize its *worked examples*, or any English-specific artifact inside them. ISO itself is the source of this restriction, so honoring it is conservative, not skeptical.

Two attribution notes the skill must carry:
- The stronger, unhedged "language neutral" framing comes from the International Plain Language Federation, not from ISO's own hedged text ([source](https://www.iplfederation.org/iso-standard/)). Attribute accordingly.
- The ISO 24495-1 working group **declined to endorse readability formulas** for assessing clarity (Government of Canada Language Portal, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://www.noslangues-ourlanguages.gc.ca/en/blogue-blog/readability-formulas-eng)). This matters for Part 2.

### 1.4 What the FPLG actually says about sentences and paragraphs — with the hedges attached

Every rule below is verbatim from the archived PDF. The right-hand columns are what gets stripped when these rules are quoted, and stripping them is a misquote.

| Rule (verbatim) | Printed page | Whose voice | Adjacent qualifier that travels with it |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Express only one idea in each sentence." | p.50 (PDF p.56) | FPLG's own | Rationale is anti-hedging, not hedged: "Long, complicated sentences often mean that you aren't sure about what you want to say." **No numeric word limit for sentences appears anywhere in the section** — unlike the paragraph section. |
| "Writing experts recommend paragraphs of no more than 150 words in three to eight sentences." | p.66 (PDF p.72) | **Third parties** — "Writing experts recommend" | The FPLG does not assert the 150-word figure in its own voice. |
| "Paragraphs should never be longer than 250 words." | p.66 (PDF p.72) | FPLG's own (bare imperative) | The only paragraph number stated as a direct imperative. |
| "Vary the lengths of your paragraphs to make them more interesting. As with sentence length, if all paragraphs are the same size your writing will be choppy." | p.66 (PDF p.72) | FPLG's own | Sits **immediately after** the 150/250 numbers. Uniform paragraph length is explicitly a defect. |
| "There is nothing wrong with an occasional one-sentence paragraph." | p.66 (PDF p.72) | FPLG's own | Its own standalone line. **Explicitly permits a paragraph below the document's own "three to eight sentences" band.** |
| "Limit each paragraph or section to one topic to make it easier for your audience to understand your information." | p.68 (PDF p.74) | FPLG's own | Scoped to "each paragraph **or section**", not paragraphs alone. |
| "Each paragraph should start with a topic sentence that captures the essence of everything in the paragraph." | p.68 (PDF p.74) | FPLG's own | The usually-omitted second half of the one-topic rule. |

Sources: FPLG printed p.50 ([CONFIRMED 3-0](https://wid.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/FederalPLGuidelines.pdf)), p.66 ([CONFIRMED 3-0](https://wid.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/FederalPLGuidelines.pdf)), p.66 hedges ([CONFIRMED 3-0](https://wid.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/FederalPLGuidelines.pdf)), p.68 ([CONFIRMED 3-0](https://wid.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/FederalPLGuidelines.pdf)). The parent section header at printed p.62 states both paragraph rules together: "Write short paragraphs and include only one topic in each paragraph."

**Where the 150/250 numbers come from.** The FPLG's source list for that section (printed p.67) cites exactly two items, both prescriptive legal-writing style manuals, and **no empirical readability study**: Garner, *Legal Writing in Plain English* (2001), pp. 72–73; Murawski, *Writing Readable Regulations* (1999), pp. 24–25 ([CONFIRMED 3-0](https://wid.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/FederalPLGuidelines.pdf)). Treat 150/250 as inherited style-manual convention, not measured thresholds.

#### Quote fidelity: before and after

**Misquote (as usually circulated):**
> "The Federal Plain Language Guidelines require paragraphs of 150 words in three to eight sentences, with a hard ceiling of 250."

Three defects: it promotes an attributed recommendation to a requirement; it drops the "choppy"/vary-your-lengths warning; and it drops the explicit one-sentence-paragraph exception, which the document permits *below its own stated band*.

**Faithful quote:**
> "The FPLG reports that 'writing experts recommend paragraphs of no more than 150 words in three to eight sentences' and states in its own voice that 'paragraphs should never be longer than 250 words' (printed p.66). It immediately adds that uniform paragraph lengths make writing 'choppy' and that 'there is nothing wrong with an occasional one-sentence paragraph.' Its cited sources are two legal-writing style manuals, not empirical research."

### 1.5 The FPLG is advice, not a binding standard

The FPLG self-describes as advice from a voluntary community of federal employees (PLAIN), not a rule issued under authority (FPLG Introduction, printed p.i / PDF p.2, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://wid.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/FederalPLGuidelines.pdf)):

> "We first developed this document in the mid-90s. We continue to revise it every few years to provide **updated advice** on clear communication. **We hope** you find this document useful... When we first wrote this document, we were primarily interested in regulations. We've broadened our coverage, but the document **still bears the stamp of its origin**."

Falsifiable and checked across all 118 pages: the strings "Plain Writing Act", "Act of 2010", "binding", and "mandatory" occur **zero times**. The March 2011 FPLG never invokes the statute it is popularly assumed to implement.

### 1.6 Refuted — what the FPLG does *not* establish

**What people believe:** the FPLG mandates BLUF / answer-first ordering as a normative rule in a legally-backed federal standard.

**What the evidence actually says:** [REFUTED 0-3](https://wid.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/FederalPLGuidelines.pdf). This is an overreach on a real quote. What survives: the FPLG *does recommend* answer-first ordering. Section II "Organize", printed p.5 (PDF p.11), verbatim: "Organization is key. Start by stating the document's purpose and its bottom line. Eliminate filler and unnecessary content. Put the most important information at the beginning and include background information (when necessary) toward the end." What does not survive: "mandate", "normative rule", "legally-backed standard." The FPLG uses "We hope", never "must"/"shall", for this rule (see 1.5), and the Plain Writing Act of 2010 expressly disclaims legal force — §6: "There shall be no judicial review of compliance or noncompliance with any provision of this Act... No provision of this Act shall be construed to create any right or benefit, substantive or procedural, enforceable by any administrative or judicial action" ([Pub. L. 111-274](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/PLAW-111publ274/pdf/PLAW-111publ274.pdf)).

A related scoping claim was also refuted: that the ordering instruction is *document-scoped only* and says nothing at the sentence level ([REFUTED 1-2](https://wid.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/FederalPLGuidelines.pdf)). The same document gives main-idea-first guidance at sentence level too — printed p.56, §III.b.4 "Place the main idea before exceptions and conditions." Also false in that claim: "the whole of Section II is four sentences long" — only the section's divider page is; Section II spans printed pp.5–16.

**What people believe:** the FPLG's structural limits are "directly enforceable by an automated checker."

**What the evidence actually says:** [REFUTED 0-3](https://wid.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/FederalPLGuidelines.pdf). The quotes are genuine (see the table in 1.4). The enforceability inference is not, and it is false for half the items. Paragraph word counts (≤150 / ≤250) and sentence counts are mechanically checkable. "Express only one idea in each sentence" and "Limit each paragraph to one topic" are **not**: the guidelines supply no operational definition of "idea" or "topic", and judging whether a topic sentence "captures the essence" is an irreducibly semantic judgment. A checker can proxy these with sentence length or clause count — but that is a *different rule* than the one cited.

**Language caveat (binding).** "One idea per sentence" is an English-prose heuristic developed for U.S. federal regulatory English. Languages with heavy clause-chaining or converb morphology (Korean, Japanese, Turkish), or that routinely subordinate via participial constructions, encode multiple "ideas" per orthographic sentence as the unmarked norm. Do not transplant it as a sentence-segmentation target for those languages. Likewise, word-count thresholds are English-specific artifacts: "word" is not a cross-linguistically stable unit, and agglutinative/compounding languages (Korean, Turkish, Finnish, German) pack into one orthographic word what English spreads over several. A 150-word English paragraph is not information-equivalent to a 150-word Korean or German one.

## Part 2 — The readability trap (hard prohibition)

### 2.1 The prohibition

**Readability scores may be used as a rough smell-test. They must NEVER be used as a target metric, as a success criterion, or as proof that a rewrite is clearer.** This applies to Flesch Reading Ease, Flesch-Kincaid, Dale-Chall, Gunning Fog, ARI, Coleman-Liau, Lexile, TextEvaluator, and to an LLM asked "how readable is this?"

### 2.2 The evidence

**The surface features are not a sound basis.** Bailin & Grafstein (2001), *Language & Communication* 21(3):285–301, ask the question directly and answer it in the negative ([CONFIRMED 3-0](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ629584)):

> "Do these assumptions really enable readability formulae to offer a sound, scientific way of evaluating the difficulty of texts?" ... "the criteria commonly used in readability formulae do not constitute a satisfactory basis for assessing reading difficulty."

**Institutional entrenchment is not evidence of validity.** The same authors, restating their argument ([CONFIRMED 3-0](https://www.academia.edu/197100/The_linguistic_assumptions_underlying_readability_formulae_a_critique)): "**despite their widespread use**, readability formulas were based on assumptions about linguistic complexity... that simply were not supported." The formulae are entrenched in schools and publishing houses; that is a fact about adoption, not about validity.

**Convergent institutional position:** the ISO 24495-1 working group declined to endorse readability formulas; the Government of Canada Language Portal states readability formulas cannot match material to readers at targeted grade levels ([source](https://www.noslangues-ourlanguages.gc.ca/en/blogue-blog/readability-formulas-eng)); the Center for Plain Language calls most readability formulas "outdated and overrated", with reader testing — not formula scores — as the gold standard ([source](https://centerforplainlanguage.org/most-readability-formulas-outdated/)).

**Asking an LLM instead does not fix it.** Gruteke Klein, Frenkel, Shubi & Berzak (arXiv 2502.11150) evaluated 6 traditional formulas, modern ML systems, commercial education systems (Lexile, TextEvaluator) **and 6 frontier LLMs** against eye-tracking data (OneStop corpus: 360 English L1 adults, original vs. human-simplified Guardian paragraphs; total fixation duration, skip rate, regression rate) ([CONFIRMED 3-0](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.11150)):

> "Applying this evaluation to prominent traditional readability formulas, modern machine learning systems, frontier Large Language Models and commercial systems used in education, suggests that they are all poor predictors of reading ease **in English**."

The paper reports "low and, in many cases, non-significant correlations", and "we do not observe a pattern of improvement in readability scoring method performance over time." The authors' own normative conclusion: "These results not only call for more caution in the use of existing readability assessment methods and LLMs, but also for new, more cognitively driven methodologies for readability scoring."

**Operational rule.** An LLM-generated readability judgment is not evidence that a rewrite is easier to read. It cannot be the sole evidence, and it cannot close the loop. The skill must not report "readability improved" as a result.

### 2.3 The refuted overreach — say precisely what survives

Two seductive strong forms of this prohibition were killed on merit. Both are overreaches on a real finding. The skill must state the narrow version.

**Overreach A — "No reader-independent text score can ever predict whether a given audience will understand a given text."**
[REFUTED 0-3](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ629584). This does **not** survive.
- It is not what Bailin & Grafstein say. Their claim is that formulas rest on a false reader-homogeneity assumption and measure the *wrong* text properties. "Unsatisfactory basis" is not "cannot predict." Their own project is explicitly "an inquiry into what properties of TEXTS help or hinder communication" — i.e. they are arguing for *better* text-side measures, not for the impossibility of any.
- It is empirically contradicted by the strongest anti-formula study available. Cunningham, Hiebert & Mesmer (2018), *Reading and Writing* 31(4), tested Lexile and Flesch-Kincaid against Bormuth's mean-cloze comprehension criterion over 330 passages: **Flesch-Kincaid r = −.76, r² = .57; Lexile r = −.70, r² = .49** ([source](https://textproject.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/07/Cunningham-Hiebert-Mesmer-2018.pdf)). A score explaining ~50% of variance in a comprehension criterion is not a score with zero predictive validity.

| Counter-evidence to the "zero predictive validity" overreach | r | r² | k |
|---|---|---|---|
| Flesch-Kincaid vs. Bormuth mean-cloze (Cunningham, Hiebert & Mesmer 2018) | −.76 | .57 | 330 passages |
| Lexile vs. Bormuth mean-cloze (same study) | −.70 | .49 | 330 passages |

**Overreach B — "Every major class of readability method cannot be treated as a valid proxy for how easily a reader processes text."**
[REFUTED 0-3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.11150). The blanket universal does not survive; the narrow finding does.
- The source is an **unrefereed arXiv preprint** (v1 Feb 2025, v4 Nov 2025), one English corpus, no journal venue at time of verification. That is not enough to carry a universal negative about the construct validity of readability scoring.
- Its own abstract restricts the finding to English ("in English"). The overreach silently strips that.
- It is contradicted on the same measure by peer-reviewed eye-tracking work: Nahatame (2021), *Language Learning* 71(4):1004–1043, found traditional formulas **did** show reliable performance in predicting eye-movement measures in L2 reading ([source](https://doi.org/10.1111/lang.12455)).

**What survives (use exactly this wording).** Readability scores — traditional, ML, commercial, and LLM-produced — are poor predictors of *real-time reading ease*, as measured by eye-tracking, **in English**, and their linguistic criteria are not a satisfactory basis for assessing reading difficulty. Therefore they may not be used as a target metric or as proof of clarity. They are not thereby proven to have zero correlation with comprehension outcomes.

**Language caveat (binding).** Readability formulas are English-specific by construction — they count syllables and word lengths and carry English-tuned regression constants. Bailin & Grafstein argue syllable count does not necessarily affect word difficulty and that neither syntactic complexity nor sentence length is by itself a readability issue. Do not port an English-derived syllable/sentence-length heuristic to another language, and do not silently generalize the *negative* result to other languages either — it was demonstrated in English.

## Part 3 — What can be measured instead: surprisal

Surprisal is the one mechanically-checkable, cross-linguistically-supported signal of processing difficulty available to this skill. Four claims, in order of what they buy you.

| # | Source | What it establishes | Verdict | Language scope |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Hale (2001), *A Probabilistic Earley Parser as a Psycholinguistic Model*, NAACL ([PDF](https://aclanthology.org/N01-1021.pdf)) | **Definition.** Per-word cognitive load = surprisal = the negative log-probability of word *w<sub>i</sub>* given its prefix, under a probabilistic language model. No syllable counting, no word-length proxy, no English-tuned regression constants. It needs only a probability distribution over next words — which any autoregressive LM supplies for any language. | CONFIRMED 3-0 | Definition is language-neutral; **Hale's own simulations are English-only** (garden paths, subject/object relative asymmetry). Do not cite Hale alone as cross-linguistic support. |
| 2 | Levy (2008), *Expectation-based syntactic comprehension*, *Cognition* 106:1126–1177 ([PDF](https://www.mit.edu/~rplevy/papers/levy-2008-cognition.pdf)) | **Derivation.** If processing difficulty is the KL-divergence "work" of updating a belief distribution over parses at each word, then "regardless of the form of complete structures T or the preference distribution P<sub>T</sub>, the predicted difficulty of the ith word, w<sub>i</sub>, is precisely equal to the surprisal of w<sub>i</sub>." The proof holds for *any* stochastic generative process — n-gram, HMM, PCFG, and by extension an LLM. | CONFIRMED 2-1 | Representation-agnosticism is a **mathematical property of the derivation, not empirical proof** that surprisal predicts reading time in every language. The dissenting verifier's point stands and must be carried: the proof presupposes a fixed word segmentation, so it does not by itself license cross-typological validity. Levy's empirical case studies are English/German (plus Hindi, Japanese). |
| 3 | Smith & Levy (2013), *The effect of word predictability on reading time is logarithmic*, *Cognition* 128:302–319 ([PDF](https://vorpus.org/papers/smith-levy-2013-predictability-logarithmic.pdf)) | **Shape.** The predictability→reading-time relationship "is logarithmic over six orders of magnitude in estimated predictability" — equivalently, **linear in surprisal**. That linearity is what licenses averaging per-token surprisal to score a passage rather than needing a nonlinear transform. | CONFIRMED, but on a **1-1 split** | **English-only evidence.** Both behavioral datasets are English readers reading English (Dundee eye-tracking; self-paced reading, 35 UCSD native speakers, Brown corpus). On its own it licenses nothing outside English. The dissent cites Brothers & Kuperberg (2021), *JML* 116, titled in direct opposition ("Word predictability effects are linear, not logarithmic"), with far larger samples. Treat the *shape* as contested. |
| 4 | Wilcox, Pimentel, Meister, Cotterell & Levy (2023), *Testing the Predictions of Surprisal Theory in 11 Languages*, TACL 11:1451–1470 ([full text](https://arxiv.org/html/2307.03667v4)) | **The decisive cross-linguistic warrant.** On the MECO eye-tracking corpus (same article content translated into each language): "We find that, **in all languages tested**, regression models that include surprisal are significantly better predictors of reading times over baselines which do not include surprisal, confirming the surprisal hypothesis." 11 languages, 5 families: Korean (Koreanic), Turkish (Turkic), Hebrew (Semitic), Finnish (Uralic), Dutch, English, German, Greek, Italian, Russian, Spanish (Indo-European). | CONFIRMED 3-0 | This is the **only** claim in this document that carries genuine cross-linguistic support for a difficulty signal. |

**Wilcox et al.'s own falsifiable limits — state them, do not smooth them over:**
- The sample is still biased toward Indo-European (7 of 11). No African, Austronesian, or signed languages.
- **Predictive power varies by language** (highest Russian/Dutch; lowest Spanish/English/Hebrew), and the variation is *not* explained by training-data size.
- The unit is the orthographic "word", which is not commensurable across Korean *eojeol*, Turkish agglutination, or Chinese (no spaces); the authors sum sub-word units to get there.

**Operational rule.** Surprisal is a **scoring measure**, computed the same way in any language, and it is the best-supported mechanical difficulty signal the skill has. Use it to *locate* likely trouble spots. Do not use it as a rewrite target without human confirmation — see the refutations below.

### 3.1 Refuted — the four ways this gets overreached

**Overreach A — "Surprisal, word frequency and word length are concrete rewrite levers: make words shorter, more frequent, more predictable."**
[REFUTED 1-2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.11150). The *descriptive* half survives: the psycholinguistic "big three" (word length, frequency, and **especially surprisal**) outperform readability formulas and LLM judgments at predicting reading times, and "the best predictor overall tends to be surprisal." The *prescriptive* half fails on two grounds:
1. **Measurement → intervention leap.** The paper proposes surprisal as an unsupervised *scoring measure*. It never tests whether *rewriting* text to be shorter / more frequent / lower-surprisal improves anything. Turning a correlational eye-tracking measurement into a causal writing prescription is exactly the Goodhart failure that discredited readability formulas as writing targets in the first place — the same critique this paper leans on.
2. **Construct swap.** Reading time is not understanding. The paper's own premise is that it measures *reading ease*, not comprehension.
Note also that the claim as usually stated silently omits **word entropy**, which the paper lists alongside the big three.

**Overreach B — "The linear surprisal→reading-time link survives cross-linguistically, so mean per-token surprisal is the defensible aggregate in any language."**
[REFUTED 0-3](https://arxiv.org/html/2307.03667v4). The quote is real ("models constrained to a linear relationship between surprisal and reading times are just as good as those that can express more complex relationships"), but: (a) it is a **null result** — Wilcox et al. state "Our tests do not support the alternative hypothesis for an alpha=0.05 for any of the models or languages", and failure to reject nonlinearity is not proof of linearity; (b) the wider literature is openly split, with published conclusions of sublinear (Brothers & Kuperberg 2021), linear (Shain et al. 2022) and superlinear (Meister et al. 2021; Hoover et al. 2022); (c) it is directly contradicted on overlapping languages by Xu, Chon, Liu & Futrell (2023), *Findings of EMNLP*, "The Linearity of the Effect of Surprisal on Reading Times across Languages", which finds evidence for **superlinearity** across seven languages ([source](https://aclanthology.org/2023.findings-emnlp.1052/)). Wilcox et al. also warn that very large LMs become slightly *worse* predictors of human reading (citing Shain et al. 2022; Oh & Schuler 2023, [TACL](https://doi.org/10.1162/tacl_a_00548)) — so a coach reading a frontier LLM's own logprobs may be using a systematically mis-calibrated estimator.

**Overreach C — "Dependency length is a language-neutral, probability-free structural difficulty signal."**
[REFUTED 0-3](https://sites.socsci.uci.edu/~rfutrell/papers/futrell2015largescale.pdf). Futrell, Mahowald & Gibson (2015), PNAS 112(33):10336–10341, genuinely show that across 37 parsed languages from 10 families, real sentences have shorter dependency lengths than random baselines. But the paper itself kills the "language-neutral" gloss: "the head-final languages such as Japanese, Korean, and Turkish show much less minimization than more head-initial languages such as Italian, Indonesian, and Irish, which are apparently highly optimized" — roughly a 6× spread in effect size. A signal whose effect size varies systematically with head-directionality supports **no fixed cross-language threshold**. It is also corpus evidence about what speakers *produce*, not a demonstrated comprehension effect.

**Overreach D — "Shorten your dependencies" as a coaching rule, falsified by German anti-locality.**
[REFUTED 1-2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3928089/). Levy & Keller (2013), *JML* 68(2):199–222, did report anti-locality in German verb-final main clauses (adding a preverbal dependent makes the final verb *faster*). But this cannot be promoted to a "crucial falsifier": Vasishth, Mertzen, Jäger & Gelman (2018), *JML* 103:151–175, ran **seven direct replication attempts (268 participants)** of exactly those effects and concluded "the published claims are so noisy that even non-significant results are fully compatible with them" ([source](https://www.ling.uni-potsdam.de/~jaeger/publications/VasishthMertzenJaegerGelman2018.pdf)). The honest position: locality/anti-locality is head-direction-sensitive and unsettled — Husain, Vasishth & Srinivasan (2014), *PLoS ONE* 9(7):e100986, report that "expectation-based accounts do well for German, Hindi and Japanese, but languages like English and Russian do not conform to the predictions of the expectation-based account." Do not ship "shorten your dependencies" as a universal rule, and do not ship the German counter-example as a settled refutation of it either.

## Part 4 — The NN/g web-writing numbers (58% / 47% / 27% / 124%)

These are the most-quoted numbers in web-writing advice. Report them **only** with their caveats.

**The numbers.** Morkes & Nielsen (1997), Study 3 ([CONFIRMED 2-1](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/concise-scannable-and-objective-how-to-write-for-the-web/)):

> "A study of five different writing styles found that a sample Web site scored 58% higher in measured usability when it was written concisely, 47% higher when the text was scannable, and 27% higher when it was written in an objective style instead of the promotional style used in the control condition... Combining these three changes into a single site that was concise, scannable, and objective at the same time resulted in 124% higher measured usability."

| Condition | Overall usability index (control = 100) | Reported improvement |
|---|---|---|
| Control (promotional) | 100 | — |
| Concise | 158 | +58% |
| Scannable | 147 | +47% |
| Objective | 127 | +27% |
| Combined | 224 | +124% |

Additivity check (the claim-writer's gloss, checkable from the source's own table): 58 + 47 + 27 = 132, against an observed 124 — **approximately additive, not multiplicative** (multiplicative would be 1.58 × 1.47 × 1.27 = +195%).

**The caveats that must travel with the numbers, every time:**

| Caveat | Detail |
|---|---|
| Design | 5-condition **between**-subjects, **n = 51 total (~10 per cell)**, one fictitious 7-page site ("Travel Nebraska"), one-tailed t-tests. Very low power. |
| What "measured usability" is | A **geometric mean of five normalized measures** (task time, task errors, memory, sitemap-drawing time, subjective satisfaction), control set to 100. Not a direct comprehension measure, and the composite index was never itself significance-tested. |
| Peer review | None. Self-published Sun Microsystems / useit.com vendor report. The companion 159% figure (Morkes & Nielsen 1998, CHI'98, n=21) is by the same authors and is **not** independent corroboration. |
| Replication | **Never independently replicated.** |
| Independent tests went the *other* way | Elgin, Jones, Anders & Farris (2001, HFES, n=80): "fewer idea units were correctly recalled for the concise writing style than the scannable, objective, and combined writing styles" — the **opposite direction for Nielsen's single largest effect** ([source](https://doi.org/10.1177/154193120104501513)). Jones, Elgin, Johnson & Farris (2004, HFES): writing style did not affect memory for web content ([source](https://doi.org/10.1177/154193120404801315)). |
| Era and language | 1997 dial-up-era web with a marketese-heavy control; English-language site only. Do not project the magnitude onto modern content or other languages. |

### 4.1 Refuted — the three famous claims built on this study

**What people believe: "Users scan, they don't read."**
**What the evidence actually says:** [REFUTED 0-3](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/concise-scannable-and-objective-how-to-write-for-the-web/), as stated. Two separate defects:
1. *Category error in the common restatement.* The NN/g sentence is "**users** do not read on the Web; instead they scan the pages." It is routinely restated as "**screen readers** do not read linearly; they scan" — which is factually backwards. "Screen reader" is a term of art for assistive technology (JAWS, NVDA, VoiceOver), and screen readers do the opposite: they **linearize** the page. WebAIM: "audio interfaces are linearized versions of web content" ([source](https://webaim.org/techniques/screenreader/)); Thatcher: "this screen-reading process is converting a two dimensional page to a one-dimensional text string... aptly called linearizing the page" ([source](https://jimthatcher.com/book2/chapter05.html)). W3C WCAG SC 1.3.2 *Meaningful Sequence* exists precisely because assistive tech reads in a programmatically-determined **linear** order ([source](https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG20/Understanding/meaningful-sequence)). Morkes & Nielsen never studied screen readers or blind users. Propagating this sentence would encode a false accessibility claim.
2. *Overstatement disowned by NN/g's own later research.* Even read charitably as "sighted users on screens", the absolute form ("do not read... they scan, extracting fragments") is contradicted by NN/g's own eye-tracking follow-ups ([text-scanning patterns](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/text-scanning-patterns-eyetracking/); [F-shaped pattern, reconsidered](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/f-shaped-pattern-reading-web-content/)), which document multiple patterns (F, layer-cake, spotted, commitment) — including full-commitment reading — and which describe the F-pattern as *bad for users* rather than as a law of reading.
**What survives:** scanning is a common and important reading mode online. Design for it. Do not assert that reading does not happen, and never say "screen readers scan."

**What people believe: "Promotional 'marketese' degrades comprehension and trust."**
**What the evidence actually says:** [REFUTED 0-3](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/concise-scannable-and-objective-how-to-write-for-the-web/). The quote supports a **preference** finding only — "users detest anything that seems like marketing fluff or overly hyped language ('marketese') and prefer factual information." The escalation to comprehension and trust fails against the study's own data:
- In Study 3, for the objective-vs-promotional contrast, the **only statistically significant difference was subjective satisfaction** (6.9 vs 5.7, p<.05). Task time (280 vs 359), task errors (0.50 vs 0.82), memory (0.47 vs 0.41) and sitemap time (159 vs 185) **all failed to reach significance**. The authors' own words: "We did not predict (nor did we find) significant differences between objective users' and control users' measures for task time, task errors, memory, or sitemap time... **The differences are not significant**, but they all point in the same direction."
- **Trust was never measured.** The subjective-satisfaction index did not contain a trust construct.
- The comprehension half is contradicted by independent replication (Jones et al. 2004: writing style did not affect memory for web content).
**What survives:** users *say* they dislike hyped, promotional phrasing, and *report* higher satisfaction with objective phrasing. That is a preference/satisfaction finding — a legitimate reason to strip boosterism. It is not a demonstrated comprehension or trust effect. Say "readers report preferring it", not "it degrades comprehension."

**What people believe: "Readers read only the first sentence of each paragraph, so the first sentence must state the paragraph's single main point."**
**What the evidence actually says:** [REFUTED 0-3](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/concise-scannable-and-objective-how-to-write-for-the-web/). The observation is real but is an **unquantified qualitative anecdote** from an exploratory arm: "Several participants, while scanning text, would read only the first sentence of each paragraph." The study's own hedge is "This **suggests** that topic sentences are important" — and it sits in a think-aloud study whose authors concede they "got fewer results than desired relating to actual reading of content", with a handful of participants out of ~11. Topic sentences and one-idea-per-paragraph were **never isolated or experimentally manipulated** in the quantitative arm; scannability was manipulated as a *bundle* (bulleted lists, highlighted keywords, subheads, roughly half the word count). NN/g's own later eye-tracking further undercuts the mechanism: readers fixate on the first few words of each **line**, not neatly on the first sentence of each paragraph ([source](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/f-shaped-pattern-reading-web-content/)).
**What survives:** topic sentences are a reasonable convention with independent support from the FPLG's own guidance (printed p.68, see 1.4). They do **not** have the experimental validation this claim asserts, and "a paragraph with two topics loses the second one entirely" is not a measured finding.

These were extracted but never adjudicated in the verification pass. They may be true. They may not be cited as established.

- **ISO 24495-1 scope is text-only.** Quote: "It does not cover all types of communication. It applies only to printed or digital information that is primarily in the form of text. NOTE 1 However, creators of other types of communications, such as podcasts and videos, can find this document useful." If true, ISO cannot be cited as authority for presentation-delivery or conversational-speech guidance. UNVERIFIED ([source](https://www.iso.org/standard/78907.html)).
- **ISO 24495-1 covers technical writing.** Quote: "it is also applicable, for example, to technical writing, legislative drafting or using controlled languages." UNVERIFIED ([source](https://www.iso.org/standard/78907.html)).
- **ISO 24495-1 excludes accessibility**, deferring to WCAG and EN 301 549 — so plain-language conformance does not entail accessibility conformance. UNVERIFIED ([source](https://www.iso.org/standard/78907.html)).
- **FPLG prescribes active voice, present tense, and elimination of hidden verbs (nominalizations)**, with surface detection heuristics. If used: the heuristics are **English-morphology artifacts** (a form of "to be" plus an "-ed" participle; "-ment/-tion/-sion/-ance" endings) and do not transfer to other morphologies, though "name the actor, use the strongest direct verb form" does generalize. UNVERIFIED ([source](https://plainlanguage.gov/guidelines/), a URL which no longer resolves to the quoted text).
- **The readability-scoring failure is robust across L1/L2 readers, reading regimes, and text-unit lengths** (OneStop L1&L2, 611 adults: 360 L1, 251 L2 from 10 language backgrounds). UNVERIFIED ([source](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.11150)).
- **The readability-scoring failure is explicitly scoped to English.** UNVERIFIED as a standalone claim ([source](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.11150)) — though the same restriction is carried by the CONFIRMED claims above and should be honored regardless.
- **No improvement trajectory in readability scoring:** "We do not observe a pattern of improvement in readability scoring method performance over time" — i.e. recency of a readability tool is not evidence of its validity. UNVERIFIED ([source](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.11150v4)).
- **Bailin & Grafstein (2001) is a mainstream, heavily-cited position** (~148–280 citations), not a fringe objection. UNVERIFIED ([source](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ629584)).
- **Formula variables are language-particular:** the authors distinguish syntactic complexity from sentence length and argue neither is by itself a readability issue, and that syllable count does not necessarily affect word difficulty. UNVERIFIED ([source](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ629584)).
- **The NN/g study totals 81 users across three studies** (11 directed-task, 19 exploratory, 51 measurement), all in one lab in summer 1997 on one travel site, with two of three studies explicitly qualitative/exploratory. UNVERIFIED ([source](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/concise-scannable-and-objective-how-to-write-for-the-web/)).

## What this does NOT license

1. **Do not treat any readability score as a target.** Not Flesch-Kincaid, not Lexile, not a grade level, not an LLM's "this reads at grade 9." A score may be a smell-test that prompts a look. It is never the success criterion, and "the score improved" is never the report.
2. **Do not claim readability scores have zero predictive validity.** That overreach was refuted 0-3. Flesch-Kincaid and Lexile explain roughly half the variance in a cloze-comprehension criterion over 330 passages. The defensible claim is narrower: their linguistic criteria are not a *satisfactory basis* for assessing difficulty, and they are poor predictors of *real-time reading ease in English* — so they cannot be a target or a proof.
3. **Do not claim "LLMs cannot assess readability" in the broad form.** The surviving claim is only that LLM readability judgments are not validated against real-time processing ease, and were poor predictors of it in the one English eye-tracking evaluation available (an unrefereed preprint).
4. **Do not quote the FPLG's paragraph rule without its hedges.** "150 words in three to eight sentences" is attributed by the FPLG to "writing experts", not asserted in its own voice; it ships with a warning that uniform paragraph lengths are "choppy" and with the explicit permission that "there is nothing wrong with an occasional one-sentence paragraph." A rule quoted without its adjacent qualifier is a misquote.
5. **Do not present the FPLG as binding.** It self-describes as advice from a voluntary employee network, never uses "must"/"shall" for these rules, and the words "binding" and "mandatory" appear zero times in its 118 pages. The Plain Writing Act of 2010 expressly forecloses judicial review and enforceability.
6. **Do not claim BLUF/answer-first is a federal mandate.** The FPLG *recommends* it (printed p.5). That is all.
7. **Do not claim the FPLG's structural rules are automatable end-to-end.** Word and sentence counts are countable. "One idea per sentence" and "one topic per paragraph" are semantic judgments with no operational definition in the source. A length proxy is a different rule than the one you are citing.
8. **Do not port English structural numbers to other languages.** Word counts, syllable counts, "one idea per sentence" as a segmentation target, and every readability formula are English artifacts. "Word" is not a cross-linguistically stable unit. Korean, Japanese, Turkish, Finnish and German violate these assumptions structurally, not stylistically.
9. **Do not generalize ISO's worked examples.** ISO says its principles apply to most or all written languages but that its examples are English-only. Generalize the principles; leave the examples behind. Do not cite ISO 24495-1 as authority for spoken delivery (its own scope statement appears to exclude it — see Unverified).
10. **Do not convert surprisal from a measurement into a rewrite prescription.** No study in this dossier tested whether *rewriting to lower surprisal* improves anything. Lower reading time is also not the same construct as better understanding. Surprisal locates suspicion; a human confirms.
11. **Do not assume the surprisal→reading-time link function is settled.** Linear, sublinear and superlinear conclusions all exist in the published literature, including across languages. And very large LMs are reported to be slightly *worse* predictors of human reading, so a frontier model's own logprobs may be mis-calibrated as a coaching signal.
12. **Do not use dependency length as a cross-language difficulty threshold.** Its effect size varies roughly 6× with head-directionality (Japanese/Korean/Turkish show much less minimization than Italian/Indonesian/Irish). Any use requires a per-language corpus baseline, never an absolute number — and it is production evidence, not comprehension evidence.
13. **Do not say "screen readers scan."** They linearize. The NN/g claim was about sighted *users*, and even that claim's absolute form is disowned by NN/g's own later eye-tracking.
14. **Do not say marketese "degrades comprehension" or "destroys trust."** In the source study, every performance and memory measure for the objective-vs-promotional contrast was non-significant, and trust was never measured. Say readers *report* preferring objective phrasing.
15. **Do not sell the 58/47/27/124 numbers as effect sizes.** ~10 participants per cell, one fictitious site, a composite index that was never significance-tested, no peer review, no replication — and the largest of the four effects (concise) was **reversed** in an independent HFES test. They are directional folklore, not measurements.
16. **Do not claim first-sentence scanning "validates" the topic-sentence rule.** That was an unquantified anecdote from an exploratory arm of ~11 participants, hedged by its own authors, never isolated in the quantitative arm.
