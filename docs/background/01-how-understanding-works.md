# How understanding works: memory, chunks, and load

> **What this is.** The mechanism layer of `easy_speak`: why the same sentence is cheap for an expert and ruinous for a novice, what the real working-memory capacity number is (and what it is emphatically not), what Cognitive Load Theory does and does not license, and why verbosity costs a listener something even when every word is true.
>
> **Evidence base.** 8 confirmed claims (all 3-0 unanimous), 4 refuted claims, and 7 unverified claims quarantined at the bottom — from the adversarial verification pass. Every number below is traceable to a source in the dossier; nothing is supplemented from memory.
>
> **Load this when.** The coaching task involves deciding how much to say, whether a term must be explained before it is used, whether to cut material, how to structure a list or a slide, how to calibrate for a specific audience's expertise — or whenever someone invokes "7±2", "four slots", "the rule of three", or "reduce cognitive load" as if it were a design constant.

## 1. The mechanism: capacity is over chunks, and chunks live in the receiver's head — not in your message

This is the single load-bearing fact in this document. Everything else is a consequence or a caveat.

**The capacity limit applies to chunks — meaningful units grounded in the receiver's prior knowledge — not to raw surface items. The same message therefore costs different amounts of working memory to different audiences.** (Cowan 2015, "George Miller's Magical Number of Immediate Memory in Retrospect", *Psychological Review* — [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4486516/))

> VERBATIM: "the limit of memory was about seven familiar chunks. For example, the letter string FBICIAUSA can be remembered without much difficulty if it is parsed into 3 chunks"

The verification pass established two things about this quote that matter operationally:

1. Cowan's full sentence continues "…each of which is an acronym representing an American agency **if you know it**: FBI, CIA, and USA." The **audience-dependence is Cowan's own clause**, not an embellishment added by this knowledge base.
2. The claim survives precisely *because* it asserts no numeric capacity. Weaker versions that pin a number to the chunk count are where this line of reasoning dies (see §3).

**LANGUAGE CAVEAT (binding).** The FBICIAUSA illustration is an **English-specific artifact** and must not be used as the explanatory device outside English. The *mechanism* — chunk-relative capacity — is flagged in the dossier as language-neutral. When coaching in another language, rebuild the illustration from that language's own familiar abbreviations or compounds; do not transliterate FBI/CIA/USA and expect it to land.

### The same point in Cognitive Load Theory's vocabulary

CLT encodes the identical mechanism as **element interactivity**: the word "Dog" is 3 elements for a beginning reader and 1 element for a fluent one (element interactivity, as reported in the pass-1 synthesis [F1, confirmed 3-0](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-023-09782-w)). Two different research traditions, one mechanism: **the unit of cost is defined by what the receiver already knows.**

### What this makes jargon

Jargon is not expensive because it is ugly, or long, or unfriendly. It is expensive because **an unexplained term that the receiver has not chunked consumes multiple slots instead of one**, and it does so silently — the term looks like one word on the page to the person who wrote it. This is the mechanism, and it is the reason a term can be free for you and ruinous for them in the same sentence.

**Operational rules (each followed by its evidence):**

- **Count chunks-for-this-audience, not items.** Before asking "is this too long?", ask "how many units of this are already in their long-term memory?" *Evidence: the capacity limit is over chunks grounded in prior knowledge (Cowan 2015, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4486516/)).*
- **Every unexplained term is a chunk you are asking them to build at runtime, while also tracking your argument.** Pre-teach the label, or replace it. *Evidence: same claim; plus element interactivity ([F1, 3-0](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-023-09782-w)).*
- **Optimize chunk structure rather than trimming item count.** Grouping material into units the audience already owns raises how much lands, without deleting content. *Evidence: Miller's substantive contribution was chunking — "the ability to increase the effective storage capacity through the use of intelligent grouping" (Cowan 2001 on Miller 1956, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://www.cambridge.org/core/services/aop-cambridge-core/content/view/44023F1147D4A1D44BDC0AD226838496/S0140525X01003922a.pdf/the-magical-number-4-in-short-term-memory-a-reconsideration-of-mental-storage-capacity.pdf)).*

**Do not overstate the discount.** Chunking is not a free pass. The dossier's counter-evidence (Thalmann, Souza & Oberauer 2019, *JEP:LMC*, [cited as counter-source in the R7 verification](https://doi.org/10.1037/xlm0000578)) states: *"WM capacity is not limited to a fixed number of chunks regardless of their size."* The synthesis's reading: **chunking discounts load substantially, but a large familiar chunk is not perfectly free.** Coaching that says "just name it and it costs nothing" is over-claiming.

## 2. The number: about 4 (range 3–5), not 7 — and Miller himself called seven a coincidence

**Pure short-term storage capacity is about four chunks (three to five). Miller's 7±2 was a rough estimate and a rhetorical device, not a measured capacity limit.** (Cowan 2001, "The magical number 4 in short-term memory", *Behavioral and Brain Sciences* 24(1):87–114 — [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://www.cambridge.org/core/services/aop-cambridge-core/content/view/44023F1147D4A1D44BDC0AD226838496/S0140525X01003922a.pdf/the-magical-number-4-in-short-term-memory-a-reconsideration-of-mental-storage-capacity.pdf))

> VERBATIM: "Miller (1956) summarized evidence that people can remember about seven chunks in short-term memory (STM) tasks. However, that number was meant more as a rough estimate and a rhetorical device than as a real capacity limit. Others have since suggested that there is a more precise capacity limit, but that it is only three to five chunks."

**Miller himself treated "seven" as a coincidence; his real contribution was chunking.** (Cowan 2001, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://www.cambridge.org/core/services/aop-cambridge-core/content/view/44023F1147D4A1D44BDC0AD226838496/S0140525X01003922a.pdf/the-magical-number-4-in-short-term-memory-a-reconsideration-of-mental-storage-capacity.pdf))

> VERBATIM: "Miller's reference to a magical number, however, was probably a rhetorical device. A more central focus of his article was the ability to increase the effective storage capacity through the use of intelligent grouping or 'chunking' of items. He ultimately suggested that the specific limit of seven probably emerged as a coincidence."

The verification pass went to the primary text (Miller 1956, *Psychological Review* 63:81–97, full text at [psychclassics.yorku.ca/Miller](https://psychclassics.yorku.ca/Miller/)) and confirmed Miller's own closing line verbatim: **"But I suspect that it is only a pernicious, Pythagorean coincidence."** And on chunking: *"recoding is an extremely powerful weapon for increasing the amount of information that we can deal with."*

So when a person cites 7±2 at you, the correct correction is not merely "the number is 4." It is: **the number was never the point, and the man who coined it said so.**

### The boundary conditions — this is the part everyone drops

**The ~4-chunk limit is only observable under specific boundary conditions: when rehearsal, recoding into larger chunks, and long-term-memory-based grouping are blocked. Outside those conditions, processing strategies increase how much is recalled.** (Cowan 2001, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://www.cambridge.org/core/services/aop-cambridge-core/content/view/44023F1147D4A1D44BDC0AD226838496/S0140525X01003922a.pdf/the-magical-number-4-in-short-term-memory-a-reconsideration-of-mental-storage-capacity.pdf))

> VERBATIM: "Four basic conditions in which chunks can be identified and capacity limits can accordingly be observed are: (1) when information overload limits chunks to individual stimulus items, (2) when other steps are taken specifically to block the recoding of stimulus items into larger chunks, (3) in performance discontinuities caused by the capacity limit, and (4) in various indirect effects of the capacity limit. Under these conditions, rehearsal and long-term memory cannot be used to combine stimulus items into chunks of an unknown size; nor can storage mechanisms that are not capacity-limited, such as sensory memory, allow the capacity-limited storage mechanism to be refilled during recall."

A precision note the verifier insisted on: conditions (1) and (2) are *blocking manipulations*; conditions (3) and (4) are *observational contexts*, not blocking manipulations. Cowan's own follow-on sentence asserts that rehearsal and LTM chunking cannot inflate estimates under all four, so compressing them into "when rehearsal/recoding/LTM grouping are blocked" is defensible — but do not present the four conditions as four kinds of blocking.

**Why this matters more than the number itself:** ~4 is a *pure* capacity estimate, measured under laboratory conditions engineered to switch off exactly the mechanisms that real communication runs on — prior knowledge, grouping, rehearsal, re-reading. Ordinary speaking and writing violate every one of those boundary conditions. That is why the next section exists.

**Also honestly contested:** whether capacity is a fixed set of discrete slots at all is an open theoretical question. The R4 verification records flexible-resource models disputing a discrete slot limit in visual working memory (Bays & Husain 2009, ["we have found no evidence to support a fixed upper limit on the number of visual items that can be held in working memory"](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3118422/); Ma, Husain & Bays 2014, *Nature Neuroscience*, [continuous-resource account](https://www.nature.com/articles/nn.3655)). The verifier's judgment: this challenges the *mechanism and discreteness*, **not** the demotion of 7±2, and it does not restore seven.

## 3. The capacity number is NOT a bullet-point budget — three refuted design constants

These are the seductive, skill-ready numbers. They are exactly the ones that failed verification. They must be actively debunked whenever they surface, because they are the most natural (and most wrong) thing to derive from §2.

### REFUTED: "the usable working-memory budget is ~3–4 units, so design for 3–4"

*(REFUTED, vote 1-2 — [source: Cowan 2015](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4486516/))*

**What people believe.** Once you strip out long-term memory, rehearsal and grouping, pure immediate-memory capacity is roughly 3–4 items — Miller himself said running memory span was only about 4, and Herbert Simon favored 3. Therefore: budget your communication at 3–4 units.

**What survives.** The history and the quotes are real and primary. Cowan (2015) verbatim records Miller: *"the running memory span was only about 4, and my introspections convinced me that the 7 that was standard on the intelligence tests at that time must have been a hooking together of a rehearsed initial segment with a final segment"* — and Simon: *"George had the right idea, but the wrong number. I think Herb favored 3."* The narrative that 7±2 is inflated by rehearsal, grouping and LTM contributions is sound.

**What does not survive.** The final sentence — the design prescription — is an **unsupported extrapolation that inverts the source's own logic**. Nowhere does Cowan license "for communication design, the usable working-memory budget is ~3–4 units." The 3–5 limit is obtained *only* under boundary conditions that experimentally **prevent** strategy (Cowan 2010, ["The Magical Mystery Four"](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2864034/)). Communication design is the regime where prior knowledge, grouping and rehearsal are *available* — which is to say, the exact regime where the number does not apply. The verifier also notes the constant is contested even as a number (Morra, Patella & Muscella 2024, *Journal of Cognition* 7(1):60, [reports ~6–7 unit capacity estimates and argues 4 vs 7 depends on what is counted](https://journalofcognition.org/articles/10.5334/joc.387)).

### REFUTED: "in a live audience with no rehearsal time, the limit is ~3 items"

*(REFUTED, vote 0-3 — unanimous — [source: Cowan 2015](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4486516/))*

**What people believe.** When attention must be applied to a whole ensemble at once — i.e. a live listener in conversation or a presentation, with no time to rehearse — the limit tightens to about 3.

**What survives.** The quoted numbers are genuine and primary: *"when attention must be focused on an ensemble of items all at once, the limit seems severe, in the range of about 3 for adults"*, and *"Estimates of short-term storage capacity may be inflated by contributions of the long-term store… The resulting estimate for adults is two or three items."*

**What does not survive — and this is the whole point.** The claim **smuggles an unsupported applied inference inside an "i.e."**. Cowan's "ensemble of items all at once" denotes laboratory paradigms with *simultaneous* presentation — visual change-detection arrays, whole-display enumeration, subitizing — or lists where grouping and rehearsal are experimentally blocked. In Cowan (2015) the sentence sits inside a passage about **telephone numbers being divided into groups of 2 to 4 digits**, and the ~3 figure is tied to simultaneous visual arrays.

Speech in conversation and in presentations is **the opposite input regime**: a serial, temporally extended, meaningful, richly chunkable stream. The claim asserts an equivalence between the two that the source never makes and that its own boundary conditions contradict. All three verifiers rejected it.

**Coaching consequence:** never tell a speaker "your audience can hold 3 things." Tell them "your audience can hold as many things as they already have names for — so give them the names first."

### REFUTED (by extension): "working memory holds 4 slots" / "never more than 4 bullets"

*(Recorded in the synthesis's REFUTED block [F17, votes 1-2 / 0-3] and in the binding DO-NOT-OVERREACH list.)*

The synthesis states the trap explicitly: an `easy_speak` skill **must NOT ship a rule like "never present more than 3–4 things"**, because (i) the ~4 figure is a pure-capacity estimate valid only under Cowan's boundary conditions, (ii) the slot-vs-resource mechanism is contested, and (iii) capacity is over knowledge-relative chunks — so the number of presentable "things" is a **function of the audience's prior knowledge, not a constant.**

> **The correct operational move is to reduce the chunk count FOR THIS AUDIENCE — pre-teach labels, group by familiar structure — never to enforce a magic number.**

### The single sentence to remember

A slide with 7 bullets naming things the audience already owns is cheaper than a slide with 3 bullets full of unexplained terms. **The bullet count is not the variable. The chunk count in the receiver's head is.**

## 4. Cognitive Load Theory: a useful frame, an invalid rule

CLT is the vocabulary most people reach for ("reduce cognitive load"). It is worth knowing precisely how far it holds, because the popular operationalization — "eliminate extraneous load" as an audience-independent checklist — is not supported by the theory's own critical literature.

### The tripartite split is not cleanly separable

**CLT's central intrinsic / extraneous / germane distinction is not cleanly separable: the extraneous-vs-germane definition is tautological, and whether a load counts as germane or extraneous depends on the learner's expertise and the learning goal rather than on the nature of the process itself. For a communication skill this means "reduce extraneous load" cannot be applied as a hard, audience-independent rule.** (de Jong 2010, "Cognitive load theory, educational research, and instructional design: some food for thought", *Instructional Science* 38:105–134 — [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11251-009-9110-0))

> VERBATIM: Paas et al. (2004, p. 3) present the following distinction: "If the load is imposed by mental activities that interfere with the construction or automation of schemas, that is, ineffective or extraneous load, then it will have negative effects on learning. If the load is imposed by relevant mental activities, i.e., effective or germane load, then it will have positive effects on learning." **The first issue apparent in this definition is its quite tautological character (what is good is good, what is bad is bad).** … "A cognitive load that is germane for a novice may be extraneous for an expert." … Taken together, this implies that it is not the nature of the processes that counts but [rather the way they function].

Note that *"A cognitive load that is germane for a novice may be extraneous for an expert"* is **Paas et al.'s own concession**, quoted by de Jong — not a critic's paraphrase. The verifier also records de Jong stating directly: *"like intrinsic load, extraneous load is not independent of the prior experience of the learner."*

**This is the same mechanism as §1, arriving from the other side.** "Extraneous" is not a property of a sentence. It is a relation between a sentence and a reader.

### Load is often inferred backwards from test scores

**Much CLT research never measures cognitive load directly — load is inferred backwards from post-test performance, so "load was too high" becomes a post-hoc explanation of any poor result.** (de Jong 2010, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11251-009-9110-0))

> VERBATIM: "In many studies there is no direct measurement of cognitive load; the level of cognitive load is induced from results on knowledge post-tests. It is then argued that when results on knowledge post-test are low(er) cognitive load (obviously) has been (too) high(er) … Stull and Mayer (2007, p. 808) stated: 'Although we do not have direct measures of generative and extraneous processing during learning in these studies, we use transfer test performance as an indirect measure. In short, higher transfer test performance is an indication of less extraneous processing and more generative processing during learning'. Of course, there is much uncertainty in this type of reasoning"

**Hedge preserved:** the word *"unfalsifiable"* is this knowledge base's gloss, flagged as such in the synthesis ([F2](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11251-009-9110-0)). De Jong's own words are **"post-hoc"** and **"much uncertainty in this type of reasoning."** Use his words, not ours, when citing him. De Jong applies the "post-hoc" label specifically to Sweller et al.'s explanation of the high-variability effect as germane load, and a second time to Stull & Mayer.

### The dominant load instrument is not standardized

**Paas's (1992) one-item subjective mental-effort rating — the dominant instrument — has no standardized format, and its scores are not comparable across studies: the same numeric load score marks the best condition in one study and the worst in another.** (de Jong 2010, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11251-009-9110-0))

> VERBATIM: "Pollock et al. (2002, Experiment 1), for example, report cognitive load scores of around 3 (43% on a scale of 7) for their 'best' condition, whereas Kablan and Erden (2008) found a value for cognitive load of 2.5 (36% on a scale of 7) for their 'poorest' (separated information) condition. The highest performing group for Pollock et al. thus reports a higher cognitive load than the poorest performing group for Kablan and Erden … Therefore, it seems as if there is no consistency in what can be called a high (let alone a too high) cognitive load score or a low score."

Scales range from 5-point to 100-point to continuous; anchors variously ask about "effort", "difficulty" or "understanding"; timing (during vs. after learning) varies.

**Consequence for coaching:** you may not say "this rewrite lowers cognitive load" as if that were a measured quantity. There is no instrument behind the sentence. What you *can* say is: "this rewrite replaces a term the audience has not chunked with one they have" — a statement about the receiver's knowledge, which is the thing the evidence actually supports.

### The theory's own authors have already conceded the weak part

The synthesis records that CLT itself moved: Sweller (2010) redefined germane load as redistributed working-memory resources rather than an additive third load, and Kalyuga (2011) called the concept redundant — **modern CLT is a two-load theory** (reported in the pass-1 synthesis [F2, confirmed 3-0]; Sweller 2010 at [link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-010-9128-5](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-010-9128-5); no URL for Kalyuga 2011 is recorded in the dossier — do not fabricate one). Note the dossier flags the CLT critique as time-sensitive: it is dated 2010, and differentiated load instruments and physiological measures are more common now.

### Where the audience-dependence *is* quantified: expertise reversal

The only place in this dossier where audience-dependence carries an effect size is the **expertise reversal effect** — the same instructional design that helps novices actively harms experts.

| Effect | Value | CI | k / n | Source | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| High-assistance instruction, **low** prior-knowledge learners | d = **+0.505** | not reported in dossier | not reported in dossier | *Learning and Instruction* meta-analysis, via synthesis [F2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0959475225000660) | confirmed 3-0 |
| High-assistance instruction, **high** prior-knowledge learners | d = **−0.428** | not reported in dossier | not reported in dossier | same | confirmed 3-0 |

**Read the sign change.** It is not that the help stops working for experts. It goes **negative**. Explaining a term to someone who already owns it is not a harmless courtesy — in this literature it measurably degrades their performance. "Always simplify" is therefore not a safe default; **"simplify for whom" is the only well-formed question.**

## 5. Kintsch: comprehension runs in cycles through a small buffer — so verbosity is not free

**Comprehension runs in cycles with a small short-term buffer, so redundant or irrelevant wording is not cost-free: it lengthens the text and increases the chance that a critical earlier proposition has already been evicted from active memory by the time it is needed.** (Kintsch 1988, "The role of knowledge in discourse comprehension: A construction-integration model", *Psychological Review* 95(2):163–182 — [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://condor.depaul.edu/dallbrit/extra/hon207/readings/kintsch-1988-construction-integration.pdf))

> VERBATIM: "redundant specifications increase the length of the text and thus the likelihood that some important piece of information is no longer in active memory when it is required… Only propositions from the third sentence are carried over, while the propositions from the second sentence are no longer held in active memory at this point… With a slightly larger buffer, or with a little less irrelevant material intervening (pink marbles, as a present), the critical information from the second sentence could have been maintained in the buffer and used to solve the problem."

In Kintsch's simulation, only the propositions from the most recent sentence survive into the next processing cycle. The model then **fails a question because the information it needs — introduced two sentences earlier — is simply no longer in the buffer.** That is the shape of the failure a listener experiences when you bury the load-bearing fact behind three sentences of scene-setting: not disagreement, not inattention, just eviction.

### The three hedges the verification pass required (do not strip them)

1. **Kintsch explicitly refuses the blanket "redundancy is bad."** His own words: *"overspecifying a set can be helpful because it provides more than one way to refer to it… It may be quite harmless, or may even facilitate problem solving."* So the coaching rule is **not** "cut all repetition." It is "repetition that adds no new way to refer to the material still costs distance."
2. **"Purely because" overstates a multi-causal account.** The model's failure is not attributed to buffer eviction alone.
3. **Kintsch says "could have been maintained", and adds that this is "far from an unequivocal prediction of failure."** The mechanism raises the *likelihood* of failure; it does not guarantee it.

### Human corroboration (the Kintsch result is a simulation, not an experiment)

| Effect | Value | CI | k / n | Source | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Word problems **with** irrelevant information vs. matched problems without (2nd-graders) | d = **0.73** (solved significantly worse **with** irrelevant info) | not reported in dossier | n = **701** | Fuchs et al., [PMC5300308](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5300308/) | independent corroboration cited in the R22 verification |

Also cited in the same verification as converging human evidence: Englert et al. (1987), [doi:10.2307/1510752](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.2307/1510752); Marzocchi et al. (2002), [doi:10.1207/s15326942dn2101_4](https://doi.org/10.1207/s15326942dn2101_4).

### The theory was revised by its own author — and this rescues the expert

Ericsson & Kintsch (1995), "Long-Term Working Memory", *Psychological Review* 102(2):211–245 ([doi](https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295x.102.2.211)), argue that **skilled, domain-knowledgeable readers extend working memory via long-term-memory retrieval structures** — a direct revision of the small-buffer theory by Kintsch himself (reported in the synthesis [F10, confirmed 3-0]).

Notice that this lands in exactly the same place as §1 and §4: **the buffer is small for a novice in the domain, and effectively much larger for an expert in it.** Three independent literatures — capacity research, CLT, and discourse comprehension — converge on one variable: *what the receiver already knows.*

**Operational rules:**

- **Put the load-bearing proposition close to the point where it is needed** — not because "short is good", but because distance is what evicts it. *Evidence: Kintsch 1988 buffer eviction, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://condor.depaul.edu/dallbrit/extra/hon207/readings/kintsch-1988-construction-integration.pdf).*
- **Cut material that is irrelevant, not material that is repetitive-but-referentially-useful.** *Evidence: Kintsch's own hedge that overspecification "may even facilitate problem solving"; Fuchs et al. d = 0.73 for **irrelevant** information specifically ([PMC5300308](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5300308/)).*
- **Expect the cost to be smaller for a domain expert reader.** *Evidence: Ericsson & Kintsch 1995 long-term working memory ([doi](https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295x.102.2.211)).*

### Two things the Kintsch paper does NOT license

**REFUTED: "comprehension is strictly bottom-up; context does not pre-filter ambiguity."** *(REFUTED, vote 0-3 — unanimous — [source: Kintsch 1988](https://condor.depaul.edu/dallbrit/extra/hon207/readings/kintsch-1988-construction-integration.pdf))*

**What people believe.** Kintsch showed that initial processing activates word senses and inferences *without regard to discourse context*, producing an incoherent network later resolved by integration — so ambiguous wording imposes a real settling cost that context cannot prevent.

**What survives.** The quote is real, and it accurately describes the **CI model's architecture**: *"a model is developed in which the initial processing is strictly bottom-up."*

**What does not survive.** That is a statement about a **proposed model**, not an empirical finding about human comprehension. The verifiers found Kintsch himself framing the bottom-up construction phase as a deliberate simplifying engineering choice — *"The integration phase is the price the model pays for the necessary flexibility in the construction process"* — not as a demonstrated fact about the human parser. And the modern consensus runs the other way: Altmann & Kamide (1999) show listeners launch **anticipatory** eye movements at the verb *before* the noun arrives, and Kuperberg & Jaeger (2016), ["What do we mean by prediction in language comprehension?"](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4850025/), conclude that prediction/pre-activation is the modern consensus. **The quote licenses "the CI model posits X." It does not license "X is how comprehension works."**

The practical implication people wanted from this claim — *be less ambiguous* — is not established by this route. Do not assert it on Kintsch's authority.

**REFUTED: "concrete, familiar framing makes logically identical content dramatically easier."** *(REFUTED, vote 0-3 — unanimous — [source: Kintsch 1988](https://condor.depaul.edu/dallbrit/extra/hon207/readings/kintsch-1988-construction-integration.pdf))*

**What people believe.** Kintsch's worked case: *"Five birds saw three worms… How many birds didn't get a worm?"* is easy for first graders while the formally identical *"five red marbles / three green marbles / how many more red than green?"* is very hard — because **world knowledge about birds and worms supplies the whole/part structure.**

**What survives.** The Kintsch quote is verbatim, and the performance swing is real and large.

**What does not survive — the mechanism.** The verifiers went to the primary study Kintsch is summarizing (Hudson 1983, *Child Development* 54:84–90, [doi:10.2307/1129864](https://doi.org/10.2307/1129864)) and found that **Hudson held the birds-and-worms content CONSTANT across both conditions** and varied only the *question wording*:

| Question put to the SAME birds-and-worms display | Nursery | Kindergarten | 1st grade |
|---|---|---|---|
| "How many **more birds than worms** are there?" | 17% | 25% | 64% |
| "Suppose the birds all race over and each one tries to get a worm… How many birds **won't get a worm**?" | 83% | 96% | 100% |

Concreteness was the **control variable, not the manipulation** — it was held fixed while performance swung ~60 points. So concreteness cannot be the active ingredient, and "world knowledge about birds and worms supplies the structure" is a misattribution. Hudson attributed the difficulty to **comprehension of the comparative construction** ("how many more X than Y"), not to abstractness.

**What you may take from this instead:** the *relational wording of the question* was worth up to 66 points of accuracy on identical content. That is a finding about **phrasing a relation in terms the receiver can act out**, and it is a stronger, better-sourced lever than "use concrete examples." Do not sell "concrete beats abstract" as an established Kintsch finding.

## 6. Unverified — do not rely on

The following were extracted from primary sources and are quoted accurately, but were **never adjudicated** in the verification pass (budget). They are listed because they all point the same way as the confirmed material — but a coaching rule may **not** be built on them, and they may not be cited as established.

- **[UNVERIFIED]** Real-world comprehension reflects *"compound STM estimates"* — capacity-limited storage **plus** non-capacity-limited mechanisms (rehearsal, LTM, sensory memory) — *"These presumably play an important role in real-world tasks such as problem-solving and comprehension"* (Cowan 2001). This would, if adjudicated, be the cleanest formal statement of why the pure ~4 number cannot be a design ceiling.
- **[UNVERIFIED]** Cowan enumerates **seven opposing views** to the 4-chunk thesis, and identifies the task-specific-limits view (*"Capacity limits exist, but they are completely task-specific, with no way to extract a general estimate. (This may be the 'default' view today.)"*) as possibly the field's default (Cowan 2001).
- **[UNVERIFIED]** *"the issue of capacity limits cannot be solved until the actual chunks that people form and use can be consistently identified across many situations"* (Cowan 2015) — i.e. any "count the items" heuristic (bullets per slide, clauses per sentence) is at best a proxy.
- **[UNVERIFIED]** Cowan reports that peer reviewers of his own capacity work **radically split**, some calling the conclusion already known, others denying that any clearly identifiable item limit exists (Cowan 2015).
- **[UNVERIFIED]** CLT's **additivity premise** (intrinsic + extraneous + germane = total load) is asserted by Sweller et al. (1998) but not established; de Jong argues the load types may interact rather than add, and that intrinsic and germane load belong to different ontological categories ("material" vs. "cognitive processes") that cannot be summed in principle (de Jong 2010).
- **[UNVERIFIED]** The predicted coupling between measured load and learning performance **frequently fails to replicate**: de Jong lists roughly fourteen studies — including Paas's own 1992 study — where instructional formats produced performance differences with **no** corresponding difference in measured cognitive load, plus studies showing the reverse (de Jong 2010).
- **[UNVERIFIED]** **Context is not uniformly helpful**: Kintsch's simulation solves *"Fred has a nice collection of antique cars. Four of his cars are Chevies, and three are Fords"* correctly for "how many altogether" but **fails** on "how many more Chevies than Fords", because the phrasing had already locked both sets in as PART sets. Kintsch: *"Context, however, does not always facilitate problem solution, it may also interfere with it… Thus, the more specific the question the better."* (Kintsch 1988.)

## What this does NOT license

Read this section before turning anything above into a rule.

1. **"Working memory holds 4 slots."** No. ~4 is a *pure* capacity estimate, observable only under boundary conditions that deliberately block rehearsal, recoding and LTM grouping (Cowan 2001, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://www.cambridge.org/core/services/aop-cambridge-core/content/view/44023F1147D4A1D44BDC0AD226838496/S0140525X01003922a.pdf/the-magical-number-4-in-short-term-memory-a-reconsideration-of-mental-storage-capacity.pdf)) — and the slot mechanism itself is contested (Bays & Husain 2009; Ma, Husain & Bays 2014). Named on the dossier's binding DO-NOT-OVERREACH list.
2. **"Never more than 4 bullets" / "no more than 3 things per slide."** Explicitly refuted ([F17](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4486516/)). The countable unit is the audience's chunk, not your bullet. Reduce the chunk count *for this audience*; do not enforce a magic number.
3. **"In a live audience the limit is ~3 items."** Refuted 0-3. Cowan's ~3 refers to *simultaneously presented* laboratory arrays and subitizing, not to the serial, meaningful, chunkable stream that is human speech.
4. **"7±2, therefore seven is the target."** Refuted at the source: Miller called it *"a pernicious, Pythagorean coincidence"* ([Miller 1956](https://psychclassics.yorku.ca/Miller/)). Correcting 7 to 4 while keeping the *habit* of budgeting by raw item count reproduces the original error with a smaller number.
5. **"Reduce extraneous load" as an audience-independent checklist.** Refused by CLT's own critical literature: the germane/extraneous distinction is tautological, and *"A cognitive load that is germane for a novice may be extraneous for an expert"* is Paas et al.'s own concession (de Jong 2010, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11251-009-9110-0)). Named on the DO-NOT-OVERREACH list.
6. **"This rewrite lowers cognitive load" as a measured statement.** There is no standardized instrument behind it (de Jong 2010, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11251-009-9110-0)), and in much of the literature load is inferred *backwards* from test scores. Say what you actually did to the chunk structure.
7. **"Always simplify."** The expertise reversal effect turns the same high-assistance design from d = +0.505 to d = **−0.428** across the novice/expert boundary ([F2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0959475225000660)). Over-explaining to an expert is not a harmless courtesy.
8. **"Cut all redundancy."** Kintsch explicitly declines this: *"overspecifying a set can be helpful because it provides more than one way to refer to it… It may be quite harmless, or may even facilitate problem solving."* The cost is carried by **irrelevant** material (Fuchs et al., d = 0.73, [PMC5300308](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5300308/)), not by all repetition.
9. **"Comprehension is bottom-up, so context can't help with ambiguity."** Refuted 0-3 — that was the CI *model's* architectural stipulation, and the modern consensus is prediction/pre-activation (Kuperberg & Jaeger 2016).
10. **"Concrete beats abstract."** Refuted 0-3 — in the study Kintsch cites, concreteness was held constant and only the question's relational wording varied (Hudson 1983, [doi:10.2307/1129864](https://doi.org/10.2307/1129864)).
11. **Generalizing FBICIAUSA across languages.** The dossier's LANGUAGE SCOPE caveat names the FBICIAUSA chunking illustration as an **English-specific artifact**. The *mechanism* — chunk-relative capacity — is listed as language-neutral. Rebuild the illustration in the target language; never port the example.
12. **Using item counts as a diagnostic.** The truly load-bearing point is not directly countable from your message at all: the real unit is audience-dependent (see also the unverified Cowan line that capacity cannot be settled until the chunks people actually form can be consistently identified). Any per-slide or per-sentence count is a proxy, and a weak one.
