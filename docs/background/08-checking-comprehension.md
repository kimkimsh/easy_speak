# Checking comprehension: the only move that actually verifies understanding

> **What this is.** The operational core of the skill: how to find out whether the other person actually understood, why every passive check ("Any questions?", a nod, an "okay") fails, and what to say instead — with each rule pinned to the evidence that does and does not support it.
>
> **Evidence base.** 2 confirmed claims and 5 refuted claims — the complete adversarially-verified dossier for this topic — plus 7 cross-cutting findings from the pass-1 synthesis used for framing. The refuted claims are the most valuable content here: four of the five are overreaches on real findings, and each one is a trap that a confident coach would otherwise walk into.
>
> **Load this when.** The coaching task involves verifying that an explanation landed: closing a meeting, handing off work, teaching, giving instructions, writing a doc that must be acted on, or diagnosing why a previous explanation failed.

---

## 1. The asymmetry that makes checking necessary

Two independent failures stack on top of each other.

**The speaker cannot introspect their own clarity.** Better-informed agents cannot ignore private information when predicting what a less-informed person will grasp, and this is a cognitive failure, not a motivational one. Newton's tappers predicted listeners would identify 50% of songs (range 10–95%); actual was 3 hits in 120 tries, or 2.5% — an overestimate falling entirely outside the range of predictions. Camerer, Loewenstein & Weber: "Better-informed agents are unable to ignore private information even when it is in their interest to do so." Incentives produced no difference, and "feedback apparently did not reduce bias"; only market forces reduced it, and only by roughly half, never eliminating it. (Newton 1990; Camerer, Loewenstein & Weber 1989 — synthesis finding [F0], [confirmed 3-0 / 2-1 / 3-0](https://www.cmu.edu/dietrich/sds/docs/loewenstein/CurseknowledgeEconSet.pdf), primary PDF at https://gwern.net/doc/psychology/cognitive-bias/illusion-of-depth/1990-newton.pdf)

> Operational rule: your own sense that "that was clear" carries no diagnostic weight. Clarity must be verified against the actual receiver. Do not tell users that experts are "literally unable" to simulate ignorance — the defensible statement is that they are systematically and persistently miscalibrated, and that markets, feedback and incentives at best halve the bias.

**The listener is under social pressure to feign understanding.** Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs (1986, p.34) on why a listener lets an inadequate reference pass: she "risks offending him... risks revealing her own incompetence... wants to minimize collaborative effort. All this encourages her to tolerate a certain lack of understanding, even to feign understanding when it is not justified" — and the coordinating signals appear "however insincere they may be." Clark & Brennan (1991): "if negative evidence is all we looked for, we would often accept information we had little justification for accepting... people ultimately seek positive evidence of understanding." (Synthesis finding [F5], [confirmed 3-0](https://web.stanford.edu/~clark/1990s/Clark,%20H.H.%20_%20Brennan,%20S.E.%20_Grounding%20in%20communication_%201991.pdf))

**And self-report does not track comprehension.** In the Griffey ED teach-back RCT, teach-back raised *actual* comprehension (follow-up OR 3.61, 95% CI 2.09–6.22; medications OR 1.84; self-care OR 1.83) but produced **no improvement in perceived comprehension** — the two are decoupled. Engel found patients with deficient comprehension perceived difficulty only 20% of the time. (Both reported under synthesis finding [F5], [confirmed 3-0](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4659395/); [F5]'s trial sources are https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4659395/ and https://doi.org/10.1186/s12245-020-00306-9. The dossier lists them together and does not disambiguate which URL is which paper — treat the pairing as reported, not as independently confirmed.)

The conclusion is forced: **the only valid check is one that makes the listener produce something.**

## 2. Why "Any questions?" fails, and how rarely anyone does better

The mechanism is stated by the authors of the tell-back study, verbatim:

> "Simply asking patients whether they understand or have any questions does not seem to be sufficient. Because they may not understand the situation well enough to formulate a question, patients often answer 'no' when asked if they have any questions. The inherent power differential between patient and physician may also result in patients being timid or hesitant to ask questions during medical encounters."
>
> — Kemp, Floyd, McCord-Duncan & Lang (2008), [confirmed 2-1](https://www.jabfm.org/content/21/1/24)

A person who has misunderstood cannot generate a question about the thing they misunderstood. "No" is the default output of a failed comprehension process, not a signal of a successful one. (The dossier notes this sentence is the authors' interpretation, citing Weiss, rather than a result they measured — carry the attribution.)

**Base rates for how often anyone actually checks:**

| Setting | Rate of checking | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Audiotaped primary-care visits, new concepts | **12%** (15 of 124 new concepts); 20% of visits (12 of 61) | Schillinger et al. 2003, [refuted 0-3 on its effect-size half — the base rate itself verified verbatim](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12523921/) |
| Audiotaped encounters | ~**2%** of encounters, clinicians confirmed understanding | Braddock 1997, cited in Kemp et al. 2008 |
| Physicians' own self-selected "best" videotapes | **99.7%** failed to confirm understanding consistently | Campion 2002 (>10,000 tapes, 2,094 physicians), cited in Kemp et al. 2008 |

The Campion figure is the one to internalise: even when people are hand-picking their best work to be judged on, they almost never check.

**Silence is not evidence of understanding — in any language.** Other-initiated repair ("signal trouble → speaker repeats or reformulates") is universal conversational infrastructure: repair sequences are documented in every spoken language investigated, no language appears to lack an interjection for open repair initiation, and the system is used roughly once every 1.4 minutes across languages. But universality of the *mechanism* does not entail cross-cultural uniformity in *willingness* to initiate repair — face and politeness norms modulate this heavily. (Dingemanse, Torreira & Enfield 2013; Dingemanse, Roberts, Baranova et al. 2015 — synthesis finding [F11], [confirmed 3-0](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0078273))

> Operational rule: the quieter and more deferential the listener's position, the *less* their silence means, and the more you need an active check. Never read "no questions" as "understood".

## 3. The illusion of explanatory depth — REFUTED as usually stated; here is exactly what survives

**What people believe (and what the popular version says):** "People cannot self-assess comprehension. Self-rated understanding drops the moment they must produce a real explanation, and drops *further* after a diagnostic question. Therefore any check relying on the listener's own confidence signal is invalid."

**What the evidence actually says** (Rozenblit & Keil 2002, [refuted 0-3](https://cogdevlab.yale.edu/sites/default/files/files/rozenblit%20&%20keil%20%202002.pdf)):

The quote is genuine and the samples are real. Verbatim: "Nearly all participants showed drops in estimates of what they knew when confronted with having to provide a real explanation, answer a diagnostic question, and compare their understanding to an expert description... A repeated measures ANOVA showed time was significant, F(4, 56) = 16.195, p < .001, η² = .536." Samples verified: Study 1, 16 Yale graduate students; Study 2, 33 Yale undergraduates; Study 3, 16 students at a regional, non-selective state university campus — so it is not an elite-overconfidence artifact.

It is refuted on two counts, both by the paper's own text:

1. **The hedge was stripped.** The "drops further after a diagnostic question" step is explicitly **non-significant**. The sentences immediately following the quoted ANOVA read: "The contrasts for Time 2 versus Time 3, and Time 2 versus Time 4 were not significant." The authors' own summary: "Attempts to answer the diagnostic questions led to a further **(non-significant)** decrease." The cited F(4,56)=16.195 is an omnibus test of time, not evidence for the specific second drop.

2. **The universality is contradicted by the source.** Rozenblit & Keil's own abstract: "The illusion is far stronger for explanatory knowledge than many other kinds of knowledge, such as that for facts, procedures or narratives." In the body, for procedures: "there was no drop in the self-ratings across time." Study 8 (procedures): F(2,70) = 1.598, p = .210 — no drop. Study 9 (narratives): no drop. So "people cannot self-assess comprehension" is false as a blanket statement.

**What survives, and it is enough:**

- The significant collapse is the **Time 1 → Time 2** step: self-rated understanding falls when a person is made to produce a step-by-step causal explanation. That is the finding, and it is the finding the skill needs.
- It is specific to **explanatory / mechanistic knowledge** — how and why something works. It is *not* demonstrated for facts, procedures, or narratives.

> Operational rule: a self-rating ("I get it", a confident nod) is worthless as a check on whether someone understands **how or why** something works. It is *not* shown to be worthless for whether they know a fact or can execute a procedure. If what you need them to have is a causal model, make them produce the causal model. If what you need is a fact or a step sequence, the IOED literature does not license the same alarm — check it by other means (restatement, application) rather than by invoking this study.

**Language caveat:** US university students, tested in English, on Western consumer artifacts (zipper, flush toilet, cylinder lock, helicopter). The phenomenon is *claimed* as a general property of explanatory knowledge, but the specific magnitudes were never measured cross-linguistically or cross-culturally. Do not port the numeric drop to other populations.

## 4. What works: make them produce the explanation

This is the strongest quantitative warrant in the dossier for the entire skill.

> "a random effects analysis of 69 effect sizes (5917 participants) obtained an overall point estimate of g = .55 with a 95% confidence interval ranging from .45 to .65 favoring participants who were prompted or directed to self-explain (p < .001). There was statistically detectable heterogeneity, Q = 196.63 (p < .001, df = 68)"
>
> — Bisra, Liu, Nesbit, Salimi & Winne (2018), *Inducing Self-Explanation: a Meta-Analysis*, Educational Psychology Review 30(3):703–725, [confirmed 2-1](https://gwern.net/doc/psychology/spaced-repetition/2018-bisra.pdf)

| Effect | Value | 95% CI | k | N | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Prompted to self-explain vs. not (overall, random effects)** | **g = 0.55** | **0.45 – 0.65** | **69 effect sizes / 64 reports** | **5,917** | Q = 196.63, df = 68, p < .001; I² = 65.42 — substantial heterogeneity |

Non-significant moderators (i.e. the effect held across all levels of each):

| Moderator | Test | Levels (all statistically detectable) |
|---|---|---|
| Task type | Q_B = 10.147, df 6, p = .119 (n.s.) | solve problems g = .476; study text g = .787; study worked examples g = .362 |
| Subject area | Q_B = 3.855, df 5, p = .571 (n.s.) | all levels significant |
| Education level | Q_B = 3.051, df 3, p = .384 (n.s.) | elementary .483; high school .426; undergraduate .611; professional .680 |

**Honest correction — the claim as originally written said "no moderator killed it", and that clause is refuted by the paper's own Discussion (p.715):** "Only three moderator variables showed statistically detectable differences that have implications for theory or practice: (a) comparison treatments, (b) type of self-explanation elicited, and (c) region." Five moderators reached significance in total, including comparison treatment (Q_B = 11.648, p = .009), type of self-explanation elicited (p < .001), and inducement format (p = .048). What survives is what the table above shows: **the effect is robust across task type, subject area, and education level** — it is *not* robust in the sense of "nothing moderates it".

**Language caveat.** An explicit inclusion criterion was "Be available in English", so the pooled estimate is drawn from the English-language literature only, and the authors coded geographic region as a moderator precisely because "regional or cultural differences affect student learning". Region *was* a significant moderator. Two things follow, and both must be said: (a) the effect is **not** an English-only artifact — East Asian samples scored *higher* (g = 1.19); (b) but magnitude varies by region, so g = 0.55 should not be assumed to hold as a constant in non-English instruction.

> Operational rule: the highest-confidence move in this entire knowledge base is **prompt the other person to explain the material back**. g = 0.55, 5,917 participants, holding across task type, subject, and level of schooling.

## 5. Teach-back: strong practice, and the citation trap

**THE TRAP. Teach-back has no pooled effect size in Talevski et al. Anyone who writes "teach-back, d = 0.xx" and cites Talevski is fabricating.** The review says so in its own limitations, verbatim: "Additionally, given the heterogeneity of outcome measures, it was not possible to conduct a meta-analysis." It is a **narrative synthesis**. It describes itself in the introduction as "This narrative review aims to synthesize...".

What Talevski et al. (2020) *does* support:

| Element | Value |
|---|---|
| Studies included | 20 (nine RCTs, two controlled clinical trials, four pre-post studies) |
| Quality ratings | 4 high, 9 moderate, 7 weak |
| Effective in | **19 of 20 (95%)** studies on the primary outcome |
| Outcome domains | knowledge recall and retention; medication adherence; inhaler technique; hospital re-admissions; quality of life |

> "Use of teach-back proved effective in 19 of the 20 studies, ranging from learning-related outcomes (e.g. knowledge recall and retention) to objective health-related outcomes (e.g. hospital re-admissions, quality of life)."
>
> — Talevski, Wong Shee, Rasmussen, Kemp & Beauchamp (2020), PLoS ONE 15(4):e0231350, [refuted 0-3](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0231350)

**Why the claim is nonetheless marked REFUTED — and this matters.** The original claim's load-bearing framing was "the evidence base is NARRATIVE, not meta-analytic — **there is no pooled effect size to cite**." That universal negative is false. Talevski said *this review* could not pool; that does not mean *the field* has none. A counter-source exists: Oh, Choi, Oh & Lee (2023), "Effectiveness of discharge education using teach-back method on readmission among heart failure patients: A systematic review and meta-analysis," *Patient Education and Counseling* — a genuine meta-analysis of teach-back for one narrow outcome. **The dossier does not report its pooled value, so this document cannot give you one. Do not invent it, and do not go looking for it in memory.**

The precise, correct statement, which is the one to ship:

- There is **no general-purpose pooled effect size for teach-back** available here.
- Any effect size **attributed to Talevski** is fabricated.
- Narrow pooled estimates **do exist** in the literature (at minimum, teach-back discharge education and heart-failure readmission). If someone needs a number, that is where to look — not here.
- The practice itself is well-supported directionally: 19 of 20 studies, nine of them RCTs, across five outcome domains.

**Language note (a point in teach-back's favour).** The 20 studies were mostly USA (n=10) and Iran (n=7), plus Australia, China, and India — so teach-back is **not** an English-only finding. But the review also found **zero** studies that adapted or tailored it: "No studies reported an interpersonal focus by tailoring or adapting the teach-back intervention." "Explain it back in your own words" is a face-threatening request whose acceptability is culturally variable, and **no evidence exists on how to phrase it across cultures.** Treat the *practice* as portable and the *wording* as untested.

## 6. Preparing to teach vs. actually explaining — the decay story is REFUTED

**What people believe:** "The protégé effect is carried by the ACT of explaining, not the INTENTION to explain. Preparing to teach helps immediately (d = 0.59) but the benefit evaporates after a week (d = 0.24, n.s.); actually delivering the explanation holds up (d = 0.82 immediate, d = 0.79 at delay). So telling someone 'you'll need to explain this later' is not a substitute for making them explain it now."

**What the evidence actually says.** The single study is real and the numbers are exact. Fiorella & Mayer (2013), verbatim:

> "Results of Experiment 1 indicated that both the preparation group and teaching group significantly outperformed the control group on an immediate comprehension test (Teaching vs. Control: d = 0.82; Preparation vs. Control: d = 0.59). However, when the same test was given following a one-week delay (Experiment 2), only the teaching group significantly outperformed the control group (Teaching vs. Control: d = 0.79; Preparation vs. Control: d = 0.24)."
>
> — Fiorella & Mayer (2013), Contemporary Educational Psychology 38(4):281–288, [refuted 0-3](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0361476X13000209)

**But a meta-analysis explicitly names and rejects this contention.** Kobayashi (2019), *Learning by Preparing-to-Teach and Teaching: A Meta-Analysis*, Japanese Psychological Research 61(3):192–203 (https://doi.org/10.1111/jpr.12221), synthesizing 28 studies, states in its Discussion: **"contrary to Fiorella and Mayer's (2013, 2014) contention, the present research found no evidence suggesting that preparing-to-teach is effective only in the short term. Rather, preparing-to-teach produced a medium effect on delayed test performance."**

| Preparing-to-teach vs. control (Kobayashi 2019, 28 studies) | g | 95% CI | k |
|---|---|---|---|
| Immediate test | 0.28 | 0.20 – 0.35 | 20 |
| **Delayed test** | **0.53** | **0.39 – 0.68** | **12** |

The delayed effect is significant and **larger** than the immediate one — the exact inverse of the decay story.

**What survives:**

- **Making someone actually explain works, immediately and at a one-week delay** (d = 0.82 / d = 0.79 in Fiorella & Mayer). This is the primary move and it is unaffected by the refutation.
- **"You'll need to explain this to someone later" is a legitimate additional move, not a worthless one.** It is *not* a substitute for the act of explaining, but the claim that its benefit evaporates does not survive meta-analysis.
- Do not coach anyone with the line "teaching expectancy decays after a week." It is refuted.

**Limits of the Fiorella & Mayer numbers:** US undergraduates, English, one narrow STEM topic (the Doppler Effect), taught **to a video camera** rather than a live person. The "teaching" was a non-interactive monologue. That is a feature — it isolates the act of explaining — but it means these numbers license nothing about interactive dialogue teaching.

## 7. The phrasing of the check matters — and it is not "open vs. closed"

Kemp et al. (2008) ran a 100-adult video-vignette study comparing three ways of checking understanding after a deep-vein-thrombosis explanation. The collaborative tell-back script, verbatim from their Table 1:

> "I imagine you're really worried about this clot. I've given you a lot of information. **It would be helpful to me to hear your understanding about your clot and its treatment.**"

Results (paired t-tests on 0–15 cm visual analog scales):

| Comparison | Preference | Effectiveness |
|---|---|---|
| Tell Back-Collaborative vs. Tell Back-Directive | t = 5.08, P ≤ .001 | t = 5.67, P ≤ .001 |
| Tell Back-Collaborative vs. Yes-No ("Do you understand? / Any questions?") | t = 4.71, P ≤ .001 | t = 4.89, P ≤ .001 |

(Kemp, Floyd, McCord-Duncan & Lang 2008, [confirmed 2-1](https://www.jabfm.org/content/21/1/24))

**The honest correction, and it changes the design rule.** The claim as originally written said "closed-ended checks are the ones patients neither prefer nor find effective." **The paper's own Table 2 contradicts that.** Means on the 0–15 scale:

| Check | Preference | Effectiveness |
|---|---|---|
| Tell back — **collaborative** | 11.99 ± 3.25 | 11.40 ± 3.64 |
| Tell back — **directive** | 9.09 ± 4.86 | 8.36 ± 4.60 |
| **Yes-No** (closed) | **9.32 ± 4.67** | **8.48 ± 5.05** |

The **closed-ended Yes-No scored numerically higher than the open-ended Tell Back-Directive on both dimensions**, and the Results section states explicitly that there was no significant difference between Tell Back-Directive and Yes-No.

> **The active ingredient is not open-endedness. It is the collaborative framing** — putting the burden on the speaker ("I've given you a lot of information"; "it would be helpful **to me**"), not on the listener. A bluntly directive "Tell me what I just said" buys you nothing over "Do you understand?" on preference or perceived effectiveness. An open-ended check delivered as an interrogation is not the finding.

**Caveats that must travel with this result — it is the weakest evidence in the document:**

- **This is an analog video-vignette study, not real encounters.** The authors concede: "A limiting factor in this study was the artificial nature of the scenario in which participants were not required to respond with their actual understanding."
- **It measures PREFERENCE and PERCEIVED EFFECTIVENESS, not comprehension gain.** No comprehension was measured.
- Sample: 100 adults (111 approached, 90% agreed), 94 white, US Southern Appalachia, English.

## 8. "Metacognitive prompts are the weakest kind" — REFUTED; here is what survives

**What people believe:** "Prompts asking learners to self-explain their own understanding (metacognitive) produced g = 0.19 and were not statistically detectable, whereas prompts asking them to explain the actual concept produced g = 0.87. So 'Does that make sense?' is the empirically weak form, 'Explain how X works' is the strong form — and the check must not be multiple-choice."

**The numbers are exact.** From Bisra et al. (2018) Table 2, verified:

| Inducement type | g | 95% CI | k | N | Statistically detectable? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Conceptualize ("define or elaborate the meaning of a **named concept** presented in the material", e.g. *iron oxide*) | 0.87 | 0.563 – 1.183 | 13 | 1,678 | Yes |
| Justify ("provide reasoning for a choice or decision") | 0.42 | — | — | — | Yes |
| Metacognitive ("self-explain their **planning or performance**") | 0.19 | −0.073 – 0.456 | **3** | 241 | **No** |
| Multiple-choice *format* inducement | 0.24 | −0.247 – 0.731 | **2** | 204 | **No** |

Post-hoc: conceptual self-explanations outperformed metacognitive ones, z = 3.284, p = .001.

**Why the claim is REFUTED anyway** ([refuted 1-2](https://gwern.net/doc/psychology/spaced-repetition/2018-bisra.pdf)):

1. **"The check must not be multiple-choice" is directly contradicted by the source.** The paper: "Although the overall comparison of inducement types [format] returned p = .048, **post-hoc tests found no significant differences among them**." No format ranking is supported. The multiple-choice cell rests on **k = 2**, and the authors themselves write that "k = 2 warrants caution" and "we recommend that further research directly compare" the formats.

2. **The example prompts are mismapped to the paper's own categories.** The paper defines *metacognitive* as "the inducement asked the participant to self-explain their **planning or performance**." That is **not** "Does that make sense?" or "How well do you understand this?" — those are confidence/monitoring ratings and are not self-explanation prompts at all. **Nothing in the k = 3 metacognitive studies tested them.** Conversely, *conceptualize* is defined as defining or elaborating a **named concept** — so "Explain how X works" is not the conceptualize category either, and the g = 0.87 figure cannot be attached to it.

**What survives:**

- Prompts that make the listener **define or elaborate a named concept** are strongly supported: g = 0.87, k = 13, N = 1,678.
- Prompts that make them **justify a choice or decision** are supported: g = 0.42, statistically detectable.
- Prompts that make them **self-explain their own planning or performance** showed **no detectable benefit** — but on only 3 studies and 241 participants. State this as **"no detectable benefit", never as "proven useless"**: it is an absence of evidence as much as evidence of absence.
- **You may not condemn multiple-choice checks on this evidence.** No format difference survived post-hoc testing.
- **You may not condemn "Does that make sense?" on this evidence either — it was never tested.** Condemn it on the *other* grounds in this document: a listener who has misunderstood cannot generate a question (Kemp et al.), listeners feign understanding (Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs), and perceived comprehension does not move with actual comprehension (Griffey RCT). Those are the real warrants; the g = 0.19 figure is not one of them.

## 9. How much checking is enough

Understanding in conversation is reached to a **grounding criterion** — "sufficient for current purposes", never perfect. Clark & Brennan (1991): "The contributor and his or her partners mutually believe that the partners have understood what the contributor meant to a criterion sufficient for current purposes. This is called the grounding criterion... So asking a question requires more than uttering an interrogative sentence. It must also be established that the respondent has understood what the questioner meant." Criterion-relativity is the authors' own point: if B needs to reach A's house she sets the criterion high; if she is merely making party conversation she sets it low. (Synthesis finding [F4], [confirmed 3-0](https://web.stanford.edu/~clark/1990s/Clark,%20H.H.%20_%20Brennan,%20S.E.%20_Grounding%20in%20communication_%201991.pdf))

> Operational rule: set the check's depth from the consequence of misunderstanding, not from a fixed policy. A restatement is enough for "which room are we in"; a step-by-step causal explanation is required for "why we're changing the deployment order". Perfection is not the target and is not achievable.

## What this does NOT license

Every item below is an overreach that the adversarial pass specifically killed, or a language-scope violation the dossier explicitly forbids.

1. **"Teach-back, d = 0.xx" citing Talevski et al.** Fabrication. The review states verbatim that "given the heterogeneity of outcome measures, it was not possible to conduct a meta-analysis." Equally, do **not** say "there is no pooled effect size for teach-back anywhere" — that universal negative is false (a heart-failure-readmission meta-analysis exists, Oh et al. 2023), and the dossier does not supply its value, so no number may be quoted here.

2. **"People cannot self-assess comprehension."** Refuted by its own source. Rozenblit & Keil: "The illusion is far stronger for explanatory knowledge than many other kinds of knowledge, such as that for facts, procedures or narratives" — and there was **no drop** for procedures (F(2,70) = 1.598, p = .210) or narratives. Restrict the claim to explanatory/mechanistic understanding.

3. **"And their confidence drops even further after a diagnostic question."** The source marks this **non-significant** in parentheses and reports that the T2-vs-T3 and T2-vs-T4 contrasts were not significant. Never state the second drop as a finding.

4. **"Preparing to teach is worthless — the benefit evaporates after a week."** Refuted by a 28-study meta-analysis that names Fiorella & Mayer and rejects the contention; preparing-to-teach produced g = 0.53 (CI 0.39–0.68) on **delayed** tests, larger than its immediate effect.

5. **"Metacognitive prompts are the weakest kind, so never ask 'does that make sense?'"** The conclusion is right; **this evidence does not support it.** Bisra's metacognitive category means "self-explain their planning or performance" — confidence questions were never in the k = 3 studies. Reach the same conclusion via Kemp, Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs, and the Griffey RCT.

6. **"The check must not be multiple-choice."** Directly contradicted: "post-hoc tests found no significant differences among them" (formats), on a cell with k = 2.

7. **"Open-ended checks beat closed-ended checks."** Contradicted by Kemp's own Table 2: the closed Yes-No check scored numerically *higher* than the open Tell Back-Directive on both preference and perceived effectiveness, with no significant difference between them. The lever is **collaborative framing**, not open-endedness.

8. **"Closing the loop is one of the strongest predictors of outcome (OR 15.15)."** Refuted 0-3. The authors disclaim the magnitude — "the small sample size limited our ability to accurately quantify the strength of this association" — the CI is 2.07–110.78 on n = 74 and overlaps the health-literacy CI across nearly its whole range, and the temporality is **inverted**: the HbA1c outcome was measured **before** the observed visit, so the "outcome" precedes the "exposure". The authors: "the design of our study calls for caution in making causal inferences." **Generalize only the direction, never the odds ratio.** The 12%-of-new-concepts base rate is sound.

9. **"g = 0.55 holds for everyone."** Bisra's inclusion criterion was "Be available in English", and **region was a statistically significant moderator**. The effect is not English-only (East Asian samples: g = 1.19) but its magnitude is not a constant. Also: the effect held across task type, subject, and education level — it did **not** survive every moderator, and the "no moderator killed it" phrasing is false.

10. **Language-scope violations.** Do not export as universal: English backchannel tokens ("uh huh", "yeah"); readability formulas of any kind (the eye-tracking evidence restricts itself to English); the Kemp tell-back wording (US, English, 94/100 white, Southern Appalachia); the Rozenblit & Keil magnitudes (US students, English, Western artifacts). **Cross-linguistically established and safe to rely on:** the curse of knowledge; the need for positive evidence of understanding; and repair infrastructure — other-initiated repair is documented in every spoken language investigated, so *silence is not evidence of understanding in any language*. But note that **willingness** to initiate repair is modulated by face and politeness norms, which makes an active check *more* necessary, not less, in high-deference contexts.

11. **Teach-back phrasing is not known to be culturally portable.** The 20-study review found **zero** studies that tailored or adapted the intervention to the population ("No studies reported an interpersonal focus by tailoring or adapting the teach-back intervention"). "Explain it back to me in your own words" is a face-threatening request. The *practice* is evidenced across the USA, Iran, Australia, China and India; the *wording* is not.
