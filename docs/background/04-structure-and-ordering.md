# Ordering: frame first — and why that is not BLUF

> **What this is.** The evidence on *what goes first*. It separates two rules that practitioners routinely merge: **give the frame (topic/schema) before the details**, which is strongly supported, and **give the conclusion before the reasoning (BLUF / inverted pyramid)**, which is not supported as a comprehension rule.
> **Evidence base.** 7 confirmed claims, 4 refuted claims, 1 unverified claim, from the adversarial verification pass on the `structure-ordering` dossier, plus the binding caveats from the pass-1 synthesis.
> **Load this when.** The coaching task involves ordering an explanation, an email, a document, a talk, or an update — deciding what sentence goes first, whether to "lead with the answer", how to open a section, or whether to add headings and signposts.

---

## 1. The load-bearing rule: the frame arrives BEFORE the details

**Rule.** Before the first detail, state what the thing *is about*. One sentence. A topic, not a summary and not a conclusion.

**Evidence.** Bransford & Johnson (1972), Experiment II — the "washing clothes" passage. 52 students, three groups: No Topic (17), Topic After (17), Topic Before (18). The entire manipulation for the Topic Before group was one added sentence on the instruction sheet: *"The paragraph you will hear will be about washing clothes."* (Bransford & Johnson 1972, [confirmed 3-0](https://memlab.yale.edu/sites/default/files/files/1972_Bransford_Johnson_JVLVB.pdf))

| Experiment II | Comprehension (max 7) | Recall (max 18 idea units) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| No Topic (n=17) | 2.29 (SE .22) | 2.82 (SE .60) |
| Topic **After** (n=17) | 2.12 (SE .26) | 2.65 (SE .53) |
| Topic **Before** (n=18) | **4.50 (SE .49)** | **5.83 (SE .49)** |

> "Comprehension ratings were higher in the Topic Before condition than in either the No Topic or the Topic After conditions, Dunnett's test (3, 49) = 4.46 and 4.80, p < .005, respectively. Likewise, recall was greater in the Topic Before condition than in the No Topic or Topic After conditions, d(3, 49) = 3.97 and 4.20, p < .005, respectively."

Experiment III replicated the pattern (comprehension 5.27 Topic Before vs 3.40 Topic After; recall 7.00 vs 3.30 of max 20).

**Three things this result is, precisely:**

1. **The frame roughly doubled both measures.** Same text, same words, same syntax — one sentence of topic, delivered first.
2. **The frame was a schema label, not an answer.** "This will be about washing clothes" is a topic. It is not a conclusion, not a summary, not a recommendation, not a headline. Do not silently upgrade it into one.
3. **Topic AFTER did not help. It was numerically *worse* than no topic at all on both measures.** The frame has to arrive before the material it frames; retrofitting it at the end buys nothing.

**Worked example (the manipulation itself).** There is no rewrite here — the two versions of the passage were word-for-word identical. The *only* difference between a text scoring 2.29/7 on comprehension and the same text scoring 4.50/7 was a single sentence, delivered before the first detail, naming what the text was about. This is the cheapest structural intervention in the dossier, and it is the best-evidenced one.

**Language caveat.** The Bransford & Johnson passages were deliberately English-language referentially-vague prose ("arrange things into different groups"); the *size* of the effect depends on how far the English wording underdetermines the referent. **The before/after asymmetry is the generalizable finding, not the magnitudes** ([language caveat carried from the dossier's own note on this claim](https://memlab.yale.edu/sites/default/files/files/1972_Bransford_Johnson_JVLVB.pdf)). A related ceiling, from the pass-1 synthesis: the passages were engineered "bizarre texts" (Sadoski, Paivio & Goetz 1991), the magnitude does not transfer to ordinary well-referenced prose, and for one passage ("Modern-Day Romeo") a *title* was insufficient — only a picture restored comprehension ([synthesis F9](https://nschwartz.yourweb.csuchico.edu/sadosky%20paivio%20&%20goetz.pdf)). So: frame first, but do not assume a bare label is always a sufficient frame.

## 2. Why a missing topic is fatal: the given-new contract

**Rule.** Every sentence you write asks the audience to attach *new* information to something they already hold. If they hold nothing — no topic — there is nothing to attach to, and comprehension fails **even when every individual sentence is plain, short, and grammatical**.

**Evidence.** Clark & Haviland (1977) have a section literally titled *Negligent Violations*: "One way the speaker can be negligent is, simply, in not informing the listener about the topic of conversation." They then reinterpret the Bransford & Johnson passage (Clark & Haviland 1977, [confirmed 3-0](https://web.stanford.edu/~clark/1970s/Clark,%20H.H.%20_%20Haviland,%20S.E.%20_Comprehension%20and%20the%20given-new%20contract_%201977.pdf)):

> "As presented here, this paragraph is almost impossible to understand… The reason is, of course, that there is no clue as to what the paragraph is about. But everything falls neatly into place for the reader told ahead of time what the paragraph is about… But why is the topic so important? Apparently, it is the topic that enables the listener to compute the intended antecedents of each [sentence in the paragraph]."

That last clause is the mechanism, and it is why "just write clearly" does not rescue an unframed text: **the topic is what lets the listener compute the antecedent for *any* sentence.** Plain prose without a frame is still unparseable prose.

The verification pass tried to kill this and failed: even the strongest critics of schema theory (Alba & Hasher 1983, *Psychological Bulletin* 93(2):203-231, doi:10.1037/0033-2909.93.2.203) do not dispute the phenomenon — they call these "the classic studies", note the deficits have been "reported frequently", and record that schema-activating cues given **after** the passage leave recall as poor as no cue at all. That is independent corroboration of the Topic-After null.

**Within the sentence: given before new.** The same contract runs at sentence level (Clark & Haviland 1977, [confirmed 2-1](https://web.stanford.edu/~clark/1970s/Clark,%20H.H.%20_%20Haviland,%20S.E.%20_Comprehension%20and%20the%20given-new%20contract_%201977.pdf)):

> "The listener has to identify the given information (Step 1) before he can find an antecedent for the given information (Step 2), and he must do this before he can attach the new information to the antecedent (Step 3). […] When new comes before given, however, he has a problem. He must hold the new information in abeyance while he waits for the given information and searches for its antecedent. This increases the load on his memory and makes comprehension less than optimal. So the strategy applies most efficiently when given comes before new."

**Operational form (language-neutral):** open each sentence with what the audience already holds; end it with what is new.

**Two honest qualifications you must carry.**

- **This is an argument, not a demonstration.** No experiment in that chapter manipulates given/new *order*. The memory-load account is a theoretical argument; the chapter's *measured* effects are bridging-inference costs, not ordering costs. Write "Clark & Haviland **argue**", never "demonstrated" ([synthesis F8](https://web.stanford.edu/~clark/1970s/Clark,%20H.H.%20_%20Haviland,%20S.E.%20_Comprehension%20and%20the%20given-new%20contract_%201977.pdf)).
- **Modern qualification:** Arnold, Wasow, Losongco & Ginstrom (2000, *Language* 76(1):28-55) show given-before-new is a **gradient, probabilistic preference** operating alongside an independent heaviness/length factor, and favour a *production*-planning rather than a comprehension/memory-load explanation ([synthesis F8](https://muse.jhu.edu/article/451470/summary)).

**LANGUAGE CAVEAT (mandatory).** The three-step *processing* argument (isolate given → find antecedent → attach new) is presented as **language-general**. The surface evidence marshalled for it is **English-specific**: focal stress falling at or near the end of the sentence, cleft/pseudocleft/passive constructions for repackaging information, and English's tendency to open with definite noun phrases and delay indefinites. Clark & Haviland scope these facts to English themselves ("Simple **English** sentences with normal intonation… this tendency **in English** makes good sense"). **Do not carry the stress facts or the word-order facts into languages with different information-structure marking.** Coach the *processing* order (anchor first, new last); do not coach English clefting or English stress placement as if they were universal repair tools.

## 3. What is robustly supported: signaling the structure

**Rule.** Make the structure visible. Headings, typographic cues, organizational signals, "here are the three parts", "the important one is the second". This is the best-evidenced structural lever in the dossier, and it is **not** the same as ordering by conclusion.

**Evidence.** Schneider, Beege, Nebel & Rey (2018), meta-analysis of signaling, k = 103 studies, N = 12,201 (Schneider et al. 2018, [confirmed 3-0](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.edurev.2017.11.001)):

| Outcome | Effect | 95% CI | Basis |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Retention | g+ = **0.53** | [0.42, 0.64] | 139 retention measures |
| Transfer | g+ = **0.33** | [0.22, 0.43] | 70 transfer measures |
| Cognitive load | significantly **reduced** | — | reported as a dependent variable |
| Prior knowledge as moderator | **not** identified | — | contrary to the expertise-reversal hypothesis |

> "The retention (g+ = 0.53, 95% CI [0.42, 0.64]) and transfer (g+ = 0.33, 95% CI [0.22, 0.43]) sizes support the positive effect of signaling on motivation/affect, learning time, and learning-relevant fixations. Cognitive load was significantly reduced. In contrast to the expertise reversal effect hypothesis, prior knowledge was not identified as a moderator of the signaling effect."

**Read this precisely.** Signaling = cues that *highlight the organization of relevant information*. It is satisfied by headings, a stated topic, and explicit signposting. **It does not require putting the conclusion in sentence one.** The fact that prior knowledge was not a moderator is useful: unlike most instructional supports, signposting does not appear to backfire on experts.

## 4. "Advance organizer" evidence is real, small, and is not BLUF evidence

**Rule.** You may cite the advance-organizer literature for *frame-first*. You may not cite it for *conclusion-first*, and you may not cite it as a large effect.

**Evidence.** Luiten, Ames & Ackerson (1979/1980), meta-analysis of 135 published and unpublished advance-organizer studies (1960–1979), yielding 110 learning effect sizes and 50 retention effect sizes (Luiten et al. 1979/1980, [confirmed 3-0](https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/ED171803.pdf)):

| Outcome | Mean effect size | SE | k |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Learning | **.21** | .04 | 110 |
| Retention (increasing delays) | .19 / .20 / .23 / .30 / .38 | .15 / .10 / .16 / .11 / .16 | 8 / 17 / 8 / 9 / 8 (=50) |

The authors' own gloss: "the average subject receiving the advance organizer treatment performed better than 58 percent of the control group subjects."

**The definitional point is the whole point.** An Ausubelian advance organizer is, by definition, introductory material at a **higher level of abstraction, generality and inclusiveness** than the content — a scaffold, an anchor. It is **not a preview of the content's conclusion**. So d ≈ 0.21 is evidence for *giving people an anchoring frame*; **it cannot be cited as support for stating your answer first.**

## 5. BLUF: the honest verdict

BLUF ("bottom line up front") and the inverted pyramid claim that the *conclusion* should precede the *reasoning*. Practitioners cite the frame-first evidence above as if it established this. It does not — frame-first and conclusion-first are different constructs, and the dossier's caveat list names "Clark & Haviland validate BLUF" as one of the specific overreaches to avoid ([synthesis, DO-NOT-OVERREACH list](https://web.stanford.edu/~clark/1970s/Clark,%20H.H.%20_%20Haviland,%20S.E.%20_Comprehension%20and%20the%20given-new%20contract_%201977.pdf)).

Here is everything the dossier actually contains about conclusion-first ordering.

### 5.1 Kieras: the main-idea-first sentence helps — for one passage type, on unfamiliar content

Kieras (1980) built passages in two macrostructures. **Generalization** passages = a general statement followed by specific examples. **Construction** passages = a conclusion followed by a supporting argument. Each existed in a "good" version (main idea stated explicitly in the first sentence) and a "bad" version (main idea never stated).

For the **generalization** passages, first-sentence framing produced a large reading-time win — **but only when the content was unfamiliar** (Kieras 1980, [confirmed 3-0](https://apps.dtic.mil/sti/tr/pdf/ADA089910.pdf)):

| Generalization passage | Good (main idea first) | Bad (no main idea) | Test |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Timekeeping (unfamiliar) | 52.1 s | 81.3 s | t(30) = 2.437, p < .05 |
| Instruments (unfamiliar) | 65.6 s | 89.9 s | t(30) = 2.579, p < .05 |
| Metals (**familiar**) | 50.9 s | 57.4 s | t(30) = .923, **n.s.** |

On the familiar passage the benefit vanished, and readers of the bad version produced "equally consistent, but different" main ideas — they built a frame anyway, just not yours.

**So the demonstrated win is:** (a) a **topic/generalization frame**, (b) on **unfamiliar** material. It is **not** (c) a conclusion up front, and it is **not** (d) any framing on material the reader already knows.

### 5.2 What people believe: "Kieras proved BLUF doesn't work." What the evidence says: he didn't test it.

**[REFUTED, 0-3]** The tempting claim — that Kieras "tested exactly the BLUF manipulation" on his construction passages and found no reading-time benefit, yielding a falsifiable prediction that *putting the conclusion first, holding content constant, will not reliably speed comprehension* — **was killed on verification** (Kieras 1980, [refuted 0-3](https://apps.dtic.mil/sti/tr/pdf/ADA089910.pdf)).

**What survives (numbers verified verbatim against the primary source):**

| Construction passage | Good (conclusion first) | Bad (conclusion never stated) | Test |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Transportation (familiar) | 53.9 s | 55.4 s | n.s. |
| Jutland (unfamiliar) | 90.1 s | 88.6 s | t(30) = .108 — "the difference is in the wrong direction, but fails to approach significance" |
| Watergate | 47.1 s | 57.2 s | t(30) = 1.376, one-tail p < .1 — **the other direction** |

Also verified: unlike the generalization passages, "the initial main idea sentence in the good version was **not** the unanimous first choice for the most important sentence."

**Why the BLUF-negative reading is refuted, and what that costs you:**

- **It is not the BLUF manipulation.** Every "bad" version **deletes** the conclusion; it does not **move** it. ("The bad version of this passage was prepared by deleting the initial statement of the intended main idea and adding another sentence to the end.") A test of *ordering* requires the same content in two orders. Kieras never ran that.
- **The construction results are not uniformly null.** The Watergate passage went the *other* way (47.1 s vs 57.2 s, one-tail p < .1), which a "BLUF does nothing" summary suppresses.
- **The conclusion sentence did change what readers extracted.** In the Jutland cluster analysis, only good-version readers produced "the British won."

**Net effect on the skill:** you do **not** get a clean negative result against BLUF from Kieras. You get *no evidence for it* from his construction passages, and *no valid evidence against it either*. Do not ship either overstatement.

**Language caveat.** English technical prose, English-speaking US undergraduates. The "initial mention = theme" convention Kieras is probing is a **convention of English expository prose**. It must not be assumed for rhetorical traditions where the point conventionally lands last (Japanese *ki-shō-ten-ketsu*, Chinese *qǐ-chéng-zhuǎn-hé*) or for languages with different topic-marking conventions.

### 5.3 The inverted pyramid lost to chronological narrative

Ameseder, Devetak & Skubic (2025). Online experiment, 461 Austrian adult non-experts, randomized between-subjects; the same science-journalism content written as a **chronological narrative** vs an **inverted pyramid** (most important information first). Stimuli were content-matched: same authors rewrote both versions, comparable word count, comparable readability index, **same scientific terms**, same headline count (Ameseder et al. 2025, [confirmed 2-1](https://www.cepsj.si/index.php/cepsj/article/view/1899)).

> "The results indicate significantly higher comprehension and general situational interest and significantly lower cognitive load after reading journalistic science narratives compared to inverted pyramids. However, situational interest in journalistic science narratives was significantly lower after participants had read the first paragraph."

| Measure | Narrative | Inverted pyramid | Test | **Effect size (the authors' own word: "small")** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Comprehension | mean rank 160.98 | mean rank 141.76 | U = 12,849.50, p < .05 | **r = .11** — and **medians were identical (Mdn = 1.00 in both)** |
| Cognitive load (5-pt) | M = 2.45 (SD .88) | M = 2.81 (SD .93) | F(1,335) = 14.65, p < .01 | Cohen's f = .21 |
| Situational interest | M = 3.52 | M = 3.21 | F(1,335) = 7.34, p < .01 | f = .15 |
| Interest after **paragraph 1** | **lower** | **higher** | U = 12,213.00, p = .02 | r = −.13 (narrative overtakes on ratings 2 and 3: r = .20, r = .12) |

**Hedges that travel with this result — never strip them:**

- The dissenting verifier's decisive objection: **the authors themselves label both headline effects "small"** (r = .11; Cohen's f = .21), and the comprehension medians were **identical** in both conditions. This is not a rout; it is a small directional difference. Anyone reporting this as "the inverted pyramid loses" without the effect sizes is misquoting the source.
- **The news literature is genuinely mixed in direction.** DeAngelo & Yegiyan found inverted-pyramid stories were recalled *better* (the dossier notes the correct year is **2019**, not 2018, per the CEPS paper's own reference list). Sternadori's dissertation found null memory effects and only a marginal comprehension edge *for* the inverted pyramid. **That mixedness is itself the finding: there is no clean empirical mandate for conclusion-first.**
- **LANGUAGE CAVEAT:** materials and comprehension test were in **German**, with Austrian participants and German-language nutrition journalism. German journalistic and expository conventions differ from Anglo-American inverted-pyramid norms, so the magnitude — and possibly the direction — **must not be transferred to English news prose without replication.**

**One thing the inverted pyramid did buy:** early situational interest. Narrative interest was significantly lower after the first paragraph, then overtook. So "most important first" bought **attention at the top, not understanding.**

### 5.4 The verdict the skill must ship

| Claim | Status |
| --- | --- |
| Give the topic/frame before the details | **Supported, strongly** (B&J 1972 Exp II/III; Clark & Haviland's negligent-violation account) |
| Make structure visible (headings, signposts, importance cues) | **Supported** (Schneider et al. 2018: retention g+ = 0.53, transfer g+ = 0.33) |
| Give an anchoring, higher-abstraction organizer first | **Supported but small** (Luiten et al.: d = .21) |
| Put the **conclusion** before the reasoning, for **comprehension** | **Not established.** No confirmed claim in this dossier supports it. The one apparent negative test (Kieras) is refuted as a test. The one direct head-to-head (Ameseder) went *against* the inverted pyramid, with small effects, in German. |
| "The Federal Plain Language Guidelines mandate BLUF/answer-first" | **Refuted** ([synthesis F19, 0-3](https://digital.gov/guides/plain-language/principles)). Note the honest nuance the synthesis itself records: the likely cause is a **citation** problem, not necessarily a falsity problem — `plainlanguage.gov/guidelines/` now redirects to a page that does not carry the guideline text, so the mandate could not be confirmed at that URL. Do not cite it as a sourced fact until re-verified. |

**BLUF may still be right for scanning and triage reasons** — a reader deciding whether to read at all, an inbox being swept, an on-call engineer triaging an alert. The Ameseder result is even consistent with that: conclusion-first won *early interest*. **But the skill must not claim comprehension evidence it does not have.** If you coach BLUF, coach it as a *triage* convention and say so out loud. Frame-first is the rule with the comprehension evidence behind it; the two can coexist (a topic sentence and a bottom line are not mutually exclusive), but only one of them is earning its keep on comprehension grounds.

## 6. What people believe vs what the evidence actually says

These are the dossier's refuted blocks. Each is an **overreach on a real finding**, not a wholly false claim. Both halves matter.

### 6.1 "Every referring expression must resolve to exactly one antecedent the audience already holds"

**[REFUTED, 1-2]** (Clark & Haviland 1977, [refuted 1-2](https://web.stanford.edu/~clark/1970s/Clark,%20H.H.%20_%20Haviland,%20S.E.%20_Comprehension%20and%20the%20given-new%20contract_%201977.pdf))

**What survives.** The *Maxim of Antecedence* is real and verbatim: "Try to construct your utterance such that the listener has one and only one direct antecedent for any given information and that it is the intended antecedent." It genuinely sits under Grice (specifically **Manner**: "it can be considered as one specific part of Grice's maxim of manner"). *Uniqueness* and *Computability* are genuine stated conditions, and Table 2 does say a Uniqueness violation makes the listener "judge the sentence unacceptable in context" and a Computability violation "unacceptable or incomprehensible in context."

**What does not survive — and it is the part a skill would encode.** The maxim is **explicitly violable, and violation predicts repair, not failure.** Table 2, section A: violation by the speaker is "**ALLOWED** under special circumstances"; the listener's strategies are "**Bridging**" and "**Addition**"; the consequence is "**Listener draws an implicature**." The paper states plainly: "the speaker does not have to provide given information with a direct antecedent," and immediately after stating the maxim: "But suppose the speaker has violated the maxim and the [listener] cannot find a direct antecedent. Then, we assume, the listener can turn to one of three procedures: bridging, addition, or restructuring."

**Consequence for the skill:** do **not** ship a mechanical checker that flags every referring expression without a pre-established antecedent as a defect. That rule is contradicted by the source it claims to come from. The defensible version is softer and still useful: *an expression whose antecedent the audience cannot compute forces them into a repair procedure — sometimes cheap, sometimes not.*

### 6.2 "Making the reader infer costs 140–180 ms per sentence"

**[REFUTED, 1-2]** (Clark & Haviland 1977 / Haviland & Clark 1974, [refuted 1-2](https://web.stanford.edu/~clark/1970s/Haviland,%20S.E.%20_%20Clark,%20H.H.%20_What's%20new_%20Acquiring%20new%20information%20as%20a%20process%20in%20comprehension_%201974.pdf))

**What survives — the numbers are verbatim-accurate.** Haviland & Clark (1974), *JVLVB* 13:512-521. Experiment I: 68 context-target pairs, 16 subjects; Direct antecedent 835 ms vs Indirect antecedent 1016 ms, a 181 ms difference, minF'(1,36) = 6.47, p < .025. Experiment II (controlling for lexical repetition of the critical word): 1031 ms vs 1168 ms, a 137 ms difference, minF'(1,23) = 15.7, p < .001. Both sequence types were "perfectly acceptable."

**What does not survive: the quantitative headline.** The authors themselves disclaim it, in the General Discussion: *"there may have been some instances of 'incomplete' contextual comprehension, and so **the absolute differences between conditions cannot be taken very seriously**."* The verifiers further note that the same paper's third experiment contradicts the quantitative reading, and that "replicated" is not earned by a follow-up condition inside the same paper.

**Consequence for the skill:** never quote "bridging costs ~150 ms per sentence" as a fact — you would be quoting a number the original authors told you not to take seriously. The surviving, defensible statement is **directional**: requiring a bridging inference measurably slowed comprehension in both experiments, even when both versions were perfectly grammatical and acceptable. Direction, not magnitude.

### 6.3 "A late frame makes people *feel* they understood without improving retention"

**[REFUTED, 0-3]** (Bransford & Johnson 1972, Exp IV, [refuted 0-3](https://memlab.yale.edu/sites/default/files/files/1972_Bransford_Johnson_JVLVB.pdf))

**What survives — the numbers and the tested contrasts.** Experiment IV ("making and flying a kite"), N = 31 (No Topic 9, Topic After 11, Topic Before 11):

| Experiment IV | Comprehension (max 7) | Recall (max 14) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| No Topic | 2.44 (SE .47) | 3.22 (SE .55) |
| Topic After | 3.82 (SE .52) | 3.18 (SE .57) |
| Topic Before | 4.00 (SE .59) | 5.54 (SE .76) |

> "In the analysis of the comprehension ratings, the Topic Before scores were higher than the No Topic scores, d(3, 28) = 2.01, p < .05. However, there was no significant difference between ratings of the Topic Before and Topic After Ss, p > .05. Recall of the Topic Before Ss was superior to that of both No Topic and of Topic After Ss, d(3, 28) = 2.49 and 2.68, p < .05, respectively."

So the **recall** half is solid and matters: a topic delivered *after* the passage left recall (3.18) indistinguishable from no topic at all (3.22), and far below Topic Before (5.54). That is a second, independent confirmation of the Topic-After null.

**What does not survive: the "felt clarity" story.** The claim asserted that Topic After *raised subjective comprehension ratings to a level statistically indistinguishable from* Topic Before — i.e. that a late frame makes readers *feel* understanding they do not have. Three defects:

- **A null was converted into an equivalence.** "No significant difference between Topic Before and Topic After ratings" with n = 11 and 9 and SEs around .5 is a non-detection, not a demonstration of sameness.
- **The load-bearing contrast was never run.** The paper reports *no* Topic After vs No Topic ratings comparison in Experiment IV. The 3.82-vs-2.44 gap the claim leans on is an untested descriptive difference.
- **The rest of the same paper points the other way.** In Experiment II, Topic After comprehension was 2.12 vs No Topic 2.29 — a late topic did **not** raise felt comprehension. In Experiment III, Topic After was 3.40 vs Topic Before 5.27, p < .005.

**Consequence for the skill:** you may say *"a topic delivered late does not improve what people retain"* — that is confirmed twice over. You may **not** say *"a late frame creates an illusion of understanding"* or *"self-reported clarity is an invalid proxy for ordering benefit"* on the strength of this study. (Whether self-report tracks comprehension is a separate question addressed by a different literature; do not import this study as its evidence.)

## 7. Unverified — do not rely on

**[UNVERIFIED — never adjudicated]** The given-new contract can be weaponized: because listeners accept given information as already true and scrutinize only the new information, a false presupposition smuggled into the given slot changes what people believe they saw. Loftus & Zanni (1975), as reported in Clark & Haviland: asking "Did you see **the** broken headlight?" (vs "**a** broken headlight") when no broken headlight existed cut "I don't know" responses from 38% to 12% and raised false "yes" responses from 7% to 17% ([unverified, not adjudicated](https://web.stanford.edu/~clark/1970s/Clark,%20H.H.%20_%20Haviland,%20S.E.%20_Comprehension%20and%20the%20given-new%20contract_%201977.pdf)).

**This did not go through verification. Do not cite the numbers.** It is listed here only because, if it holds, it constrains frame-first advice in an ethically important direction: anything you place in the given slot is asserted as shared background and will not be checked by the audience — so never presuppose a claim you have not established. Treat that as a prudential rule of thumb, not as an evidenced finding.

## What this does NOT license

1. **"Frame-first proves BLUF."** It does not. Bransford & Johnson's manipulation was a **topic label** ("this will be about washing clothes"); an Ausubelian advance organizer is by definition **more abstract and more general** than the content. Neither is a conclusion. "Clark & Haviland validate BLUF" is named in the dossier's own do-not-overreach list.
2. **"There is comprehension evidence for leading with the answer."** There is none in this dossier. The one direct head-to-head (Ameseder et al. 2025) went *against* the inverted pyramid; the one study people cite as the BLUF test (Kieras 1980) did not run the BLUF manipulation. If you recommend BLUF, recommend it as a **scanning/triage** convention and label it as such.
3. **"Kieras proved conclusion-first doesn't work."** Refuted 0-3. His "bad" versions *deleted* the main idea rather than *moving* it; one construction passage (Watergate) went the *other* direction; and the conclusion sentence *did* change what readers extracted (only good-version readers concluded "the British won").
4. **"The inverted pyramid is worse for comprehension."** Only with the source's own hedges attached: **small** effects (r = .11; Cohen's f = .21), **identical medians**, German-language materials with Austrian participants, and a news literature that is genuinely mixed (DeAngelo & Yegiyan 2019 found inverted-pyramid stories recalled *better*).
5. **"Any framing helps."** No. On **familiar** material Kieras found the benefit vanished (metals: 50.9 s vs 57.4 s, n.s.) and readers built their own consistent main idea anyway. Frames pay where the content is unfamiliar to *this* audience.
6. **"A frame added at the end still helps."** No. Topic After was numerically *worse* than No Topic on both comprehension and recall in Experiment II, and left recall at No-Topic levels in Experiment IV. Retrospective framing does not repay the debt.
7. **"…and a late frame at least makes people feel they understood."** Not licensed either — that specific inference was refuted 0-3 (untested contrast, null read as equivalence, contradicted elsewhere in the same paper).
8. **"Every sentence must have a computable unique antecedent or it is broken."** Refuted 1-2 by the source itself: the maxim is explicitly violable, violation is "ALLOWED under special circumstances", and listeners bridge, add, or restructure. Do not build a mechanical linter on this.
9. **"Forcing a bridging inference costs ~150 ms."** Do not quote the magnitude — Haviland & Clark state "the absolute differences between conditions cannot be taken very seriously." Direction only.
10. **"Given-before-new means: use clefts, put the stress at the end, open with a definite NP."** **English-specific.** The 3-step processing argument is language-general; end-of-sentence focal stress, cleft/pseudocleft/passive packaging, and definite-NP-first are English surface artifacts, and Clark & Haviland scope them to English themselves. Coach *anchor-first, new-last*; do not export English information-packaging machinery to languages with different topic-marking.
11. **"Point-last rhetorical traditions are just bad structure."** Not supported. Kieras's "initial mention = theme" convention is a convention of **English expository prose**; the dossier explicitly warns against assuming it for Japanese *ki-shō-ten-ketsu* or Chinese *qǐ-chéng-zhuǎn-hé*.
12. **"BLUF is a federal plain-language mandate."** Refuted 0-3 — and note the honest reason: the guideline URL that supposedly carried it now redirects to a page without the text, so this failed on **citation** grounds. Either way, it is not a sourced fact today.
13. **"Signaling means putting the conclusion in the heading."** Signaling means making the **organization** visible — headings, typographic cues, importance cues. That is what the g+ = 0.53 / 0.33 effects were measured on. Do not launder the BLUF rule through the signaling citation.
