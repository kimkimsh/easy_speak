# Grounding: understanding is something you build together

> **What this is.** The evidence base for treating "being understood" as a jointly achieved state rather than a property of a well-formed message — the grounding criterion, positive evidence of understanding, the compression that grounding produces, the repair infrastructure that makes it work, and the hard boundary where all of this stops applying (one-way writing).
> **Evidence base.** 14 confirmed claims, 7 refuted claims, 6 unverified claims, from the adversarial verification pass. Primary sources: Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs 1986, Clark & Brennan 1991, Grice 1975, Dingemanse et al. 2013/2015, Bell 1984, Keenan 1976, Wierzbicka 1991.
> **Load this when.** The coaching task involves live dialogue, meetings, teaching, code review conversation, explaining to a newcomer, checking whether someone actually understood, deciding how much to polish an utterance before saying it, or deciding whether a "conversational" rule transfers to a written document. Also load it before asserting anything about Grice's maxims or about "know your audience".

## 1. The grounding criterion: understanding is joint, partial, and purpose-relative

**The rule.** An utterance is not "communication" because it is well-formed. It is communication only when the speaker and the addressee *mutually believe* the addressee has understood well enough for the current purpose. Until that mutual belief exists, the speech act has not been performed — only the sentence has been uttered.

Clark & Brennan, verbatim (Clark & Brennan 1991, [confirmed 3-0](https://web.stanford.edu/~clark/1990s/Clark,%20H.H.%20_%20Brennan,%20S.E.%20_Grounding%20in%20communication_%201991.pdf)):

> We assume that the criterion people try to reach in conversation is as follows (Clark & Schaefer, 1989; Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs, 1986): The contributor and his or her partners mutually believe that the partners have understood what the contributor meant to a criterion sufficient for current purposes. This is called the grounding criterion. ... So asking a question requires more than uttering an interrogative sentence. It must also be established that the respondent has understood what the questioner meant.

The verifier extracted the surrounding sentence too: **"Of course, understanding can never be perfect."** Perfect understanding is not the target and never was; *sufficient for current purposes* is.

The same authors state the general principle (Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs 1986, [confirmed 3-0](http://www.web.stanford.edu/~clark/1980s/Clark,%20H.H.%20_%20Wilkes-Gibbs,%20D.%20_Referring%20as%20a%20collaborative%20process_%201986.pdf)):

> Principle of mutual responsibility. The participants in a conversation try to establish, roughly by the initiation of each new contribution, the mutual belief that the listeners have understood what the speaker meant in the last utterance to a criterion sufficient for current purposes.

Three operational consequences, each grounded in the same paper:

1. **Acceptance happens before you move on, explicitly or tacitly.** "A and B must mutually accept that B has understood A's references before they let the conversation go on"; "allowing a new contribution to proceed is tantamount to a mutual acceptance of the old one" (Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs 1986, pp. 9, 34–35, [confirmed 3-0](http://www.web.stanford.edu/~clark/1980s/Clark,%20H.H.%20_%20Wilkes-Gibbs,%20D.%20_Referring%20as%20a%20collaborative%20process_%201986.pdf)).
2. **The required standard is task-dependent, not absolute.** The authors' own "criterion problem": if the addressee needs to physically reach the speaker's house, she sets the criterion high; if she is making party conversation, she sets it low (synthesis F4, sourced to the same paper). *Coaching move: before optimizing an explanation, ask what the listener must end up able to DO. That fixes the criterion.*
3. **A perfectly well-formed utterance can fail as communication.** This is the direct prediction of the criterion. The paper explicitly rejects the "literary model" idealization in which "the speaker satisfies her intention simply by the issuing of that noun phrase" ([confirmed 3-0](http://www.web.stanford.edu/~clark/1980s/Clark,%20H.H.%20_%20Wilkes-Gibbs,%20D.%20_Referring%20as%20a%20collaborative%20process_%201986.pdf)).

**Scope caveats that travel with this (do not strip them).** Clark concedes the "asking a question = only accomplished on uptake" reading is a definitional commitment (an Austinian uptake requirement), not a bare empirical finding. Pickering & Garrod (2004, BBS) argue much dialogue coordination is automatic priming rather than mutual-belief modelling ([source](https://www.pure.ed.ac.uk/ws/files/11823730/Toward_a_mechanistic_psychology_of_dialogue.pdf)). Both hedges are recorded in the synthesis (F4) and must not be dropped when this is presented as settled.

**Citation hygiene.** The widely-circulated CMU-hosted PDF of Clark & Brennan 1991 (`cs.cmu.edu/~illah/CLASSDOCS/Clark91.pdf`) is an image-only scan with no text layer and cannot be quote-checked by any downstream verifier. Cite the Stanford author copy linked above.

## 2. Positive evidence of understanding — and why nods, "okay" and "any questions?" are worthless as checks

**The rule.** Never infer understanding from the *absence* of visible confusion. Seek positive evidence. And treat the cheapest forms of positive evidence as compromised, because the listener is under structural pressure to produce them insincerely.

### 2a. The three forms of positive evidence

Clark & Brennan, verbatim (Clark & Brennan 1991, [confirmed 3-0](https://web.stanford.edu/~clark/1990s/Clark,%20H.H.%20_%20Brennan,%20S.E.%20_Grounding%20in%20communication_%201991.pdf)):

> But if negative evidence is all we looked for, we would often accept information we had little justification for accepting. In fact, people ordinarily reach for a higher criterion. As the contribution model says, people ultimately seek positive evidence of understanding. ... First, acknowledgments are the most obvious form of positive evidence. By acknowledgments we mean much of what has been called back-channel responses. ... A second, common form of positive evidence is the initiation of the relevant next turn. ... The third and most basic form of positive evidence is continued attention.

| Form | What it is | Strength as evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Acknowledgments / back-channels | continuers, assessments, head nods | Weakest — cheap to fake (see 2b) |
| Initiation of the relevant next turn | an appropriate second part of an adjacency pair *displays* the hearer's understanding | Stronger — the content of the next turn is diagnostic |
| Continued attention | unbroken attending / gaze | Baseline only |

No infinite regress arises (each piece of evidence needing its own evidence) because relevant next turns and continued attention supply evidence without needing a separate presentation of their own ([confirmed 3-0](https://web.stanford.edu/~clark/1990s/Clark,%20H.H.%20_%20Brennan,%20S.E.%20_Grounding%20in%20communication_%201991.pdf)).

**Language caveat.** The *categories* are cross-linguistic; the *tokens* are not. "uh huh", "yeah" and the British "m" are English back-channel forms cited by Clark & Brennan from Schegloff (1982). The synthesis explicitly lists English backchannel tokens as English-specific artifacts that must not be generalized. Coach the function ("elicit a next turn that displays understanding"), never the token.

### 2b. Listeners are under systematic pressure to feign understanding

Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs, verbatim (Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs 1986, p. 34, [confirmed 3-0](http://www.web.stanford.edu/~clark/1980s/Clark,%20H.H.%20_%20Wilkes-Gibbs,%20D.%20_Referring%20as%20a%20collaborative%20process_%201986.pdf)):

> If she rejects it, she risks offending him by indicating that it wasn't adequate. She also risks revealing her own incompetence if indeed it should have been adequate. Finally, like the speaker, the listener wants to minimize collaborative effort ... All this encourages her to tolerate a certain lack of understanding, even to feign understanding when it is not justified.

The verifier surfaced the immediately preceding lines, which are stronger still: coordinating signals such as back-channel responses and try markers appear **"however insincere they may be"**, and **"speakers should feel they are being understood well enough even when they are not."**

Three named pressures on the listener: (1) risk of offending the speaker, (2) risk of revealing her own incompetence, (3) her own interest in minimizing collaborative effort — extra acceptance steps cost her too.

**Therefore: a nod, an "okay", and "any questions?" are not checks.** They are exactly the signals the source says will be produced insincerely.

### 2c. What an actual check looks like — and the numbers

The 1986 passage is theoretical rationale, not data. The empirical support for the operational rule comes from a different literature and is recorded in the verifier evidence for the same claim and in synthesis F5:

| Finding | Value | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Teach-back vs. control, comprehension at post-ED follow-up | OR 3.61 (95% CI 2.09–6.22) | Griffey et al., ED teach-back RCT ([source](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4659395/)) |
| Teach-back, medications comprehension | OR 1.84 (95% CI 1.09–3.12) | same |
| Teach-back, self-care comprehension | OR 1.83 (95% CI 1.07–3.13) | same |
| Teach-back, *perceived* comprehension | **no improvement** | same |
| Patients with deficient comprehension who perceived any difficulty | 20% | Engel, cited in synthesis F5 |

The decisive pattern: teach-back raised **actual** comprehension while leaving **perceived** comprehension unchanged. Self-report is decoupled from comprehension. That is the empirical form of "listeners feign understanding".

Kemp et al. (JABFM 2008, [source](https://www.jabfm.org/content/21/1/24)), verbatim as recorded in the dossier: **"Simply asking patients whether they understand or have any questions does not seem to be sufficient... they may not understand the situation well enough to formulate a question"** — and "Tell-Back" was significantly preferred by patients.

**Operational rule.** Replace the passive check with an active one. Ask the listener to **restate in their own words** or to **apply** the thing. Do not ask "does that make sense?", "any questions?", or watch for nodding.

**Hedge that travels with the rule (from the dossier's own caveats):** the mechanism (repair) is universal, but universality of the mechanism does **not** entail cross-cultural uniformity in *willingness* to initiate repair — face and politeness norms modulate this heavily. Combined with the feign-understanding finding: **silence is not evidence of understanding in any language.**

## 3. Grounding compresses language — which is why expert shorthand fails on newcomers

**The finding.** In the tangram-matching task, common ground built over repeated trials shrinks the referring expression by a factor of ~5 and the number of turns by a factor of ~4.

Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs 1986, verbatim ([confirmed 3-0](http://www.web.stanford.edu/~clark/1980s/Clark,%20H.H.%20_%20Wilkes-Gibbs,%20D.%20_Referring%20as%20a%20collaborative%20process_%201986.pdf)):

> Figure 2 shows that directors used an average of 41 words per figure in trial 1 but only 8 words per figure in trial 6. This decline is highly significant, F(1,35) = 44.31, p < .001. ... the director averaged 3.7 turns per figure on trial 1 but only about one per figure by trial 6. This trend was also highly significant, F(1,35) = 79.59, p < .001.

| Measure | Trial 1 | Trial 6 | Test | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Words per figure | 41 | 8 | F(1,35) = 44.31, p < .001 | Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs 1986 |
| Turns per figure | 3.7 | ~1 | F(1,35) = 79.59, p < .001 | Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs 1986 |

Design (verified against the primary paper): 8 pairs, 12 tangram figures, 6 trials, 576 figure placements, 9,792 words transcribed.

**Illustration of the mechanism** (constructed to show the shape of the compression — the study reports only word and turn counts, no transcript is reproduced in the dossier, so treat the wording as illustrative and the numbers above as the data):

- *Trial 1, ~41 words, several turns:* a long descriptive attempt, a query from the matcher, a refashioning by the director, an acceptance.
- *Trial 6, ~8 words, ~1 turn:* a short agreed label, accepted with no repair.

**The load-bearing inference.** The eight-word label at trial 6 is not "clearer writing". It is a *receipt* for grounding work that both parties already did. Using it with someone who was not in trials 1–5 is guaranteed failure, because the label's entire meaning is the accumulated common ground it points at.

**Coaching rules (from synthesis F7, same source):**
- An internal team's compressed vocabulary is a **grounding artifact, not clarity**. Audit it before reusing it with outsiders.
- Do **not** use brevity alone as a diagnostic of shared understanding. Use the acceptance/repair mechanism instead: *name the thing briefly; if the other party proceeds without repair, it was grounded.*

**Contest and caveats (do not strip).** The matching task is "the workhorse experimental task for studying collaborative referring" with "repeatedly replicated findings" (Bangerter et al., JML, [source](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0749596X20300437)). But the *causal mechanism* of the compression is contested: Bangerter et al. found lexical diversity also drops in a "new cards" condition where object-specific conceptual pacts cannot form, so entrainment may partly reflect shared interactional history or speaker-side accessibility rather than mutual knowledge as such. Separately, the reduction is **contingent on a responsive addressee** — Krauss & Weinheimer (1964) found shortening occurred only if listeners could speak back, which is itself the strongest argument that the process is genuinely joint (synthesis F7).

## 4. Least COLLABORATIVE effort — and the hard boundary where it stops

### 4a. The principle

Clark & Brennan 1991, p. 226, verbatim ([confirmed 2-1](https://web.stanford.edu/~clark/1990s/Clark,%20H.H.%20_%20Brennan,%20S.E.%20_Grounding%20in%20communication_%201991.pdf)):

> The principle of least effort, it has been argued, must therefore be replaced with the following principle (Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs, 1986): The principle of least collaborative effort: In conversation, the participants try to minimize their collaborative effort—the work that both do from the initiation of each contribution to its mutual acceptance.

The classical speaker-centric version is the foil, and Clark & Brennan route it through Grice themselves: "The traditional version exhorts the speaker: Don't expend any more effort than you need to get your addressees to understand you with as little effort. Grice (1975) expressed this idea in terms of two maxims: Quantity ... and Manner—Be brief (avoid unnecessary prolixity). According to both versions, speakers are supposed to create what we will call **proper utterances**."

Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs 1986, pp. 27–28, verbatim ([confirmed 2-1](http://www.web.stanford.edu/~clark/1980s/Clark,%20H.H.%20_%20Wilkes-Gibbs,%20D.%20_Referring%20as%20a%20collaborative%20process_%201986.pdf)):

> Least effort predicts that every reference is made with (a) a standard (literary) noun phrase that (b) is as short as possible and yet (c) specifies the referent uniquely in that context. Least collaborative effort predicts that references can be made with (a) nonstandard, nonliterary noun phrases, (b) with ones the speaker believes are not adequate in context, and (c) with devices that draw addressees into the process. ... On all these counts the evidence favors least collaborative effort.

The paper says speakers do this *deliberately*: "in three examples they deliberately drew the addressees into the process; and in three they began by knowingly issuing a questionable or inadequate noun phrase."

**In live dialogue, therefore: a repairable draft beats a perfect utterance that costs excessive planning time.** This also explains Schegloff et al.'s (1977) documented preferences for self-initiated and self-made repair — other-initiated repair costs extra turns, hence extra collaborative effort.

**Note the votes.** Both least-collaborative-effort claims passed **2-1, not unanimously**. The joint-vs-individual framing is contested: Davies (2007, "Least Collaborative Effort or Least Individual Effort") argues "effort" is under-operationalized (utterance length ≠ effort) and the data may reflect individual motivation ([source](https://www.latl.leeds.ac.uk/wp-content/uploads/sites/49/2019/05/Davies_2007.pdf)); Pickering & Garrod (2004) explain the same coordination via automatic priming without any joint-effort minimization. Replication support: Hawkins et al. (2020, Cognitive Science, 83 pairs, >15,000 utterances) replicate the pattern ([source](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/cogs.12845)).

### 4b. The hard boundary — this licenses NOTHING for writing, broadcast or large lectures

This is the single most dangerous over-generalization available in this document, and the dossier flags it explicitly in its DO-NOT-OVERREACH list:

> "grounding/least-collaborative-effort means you should ship imperfect drafts" applied to WRITING or PRESENTATIONS — **Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs explicitly exclude novels, letters, broadcasts, sermons and large lectures — where the addressee cannot repair, the self-sufficient utterance IS the right target.**

| Channel | Can the addressee repair in real time? | Right target |
|---|---|---|
| Live conversation, small meeting, pairing | Yes | Repairable draft; invite refashioning; check by restatement |
| Novel, letter, broadcast, sermon, large lecture | No | **Self-sufficient utterance.** Polish it. It has to stand alone. |

**Coaching rule.** Before applying any "just say it roughly and let them fix it" advice, ask: *can this addressee interrupt and repair, right now?* If no, the collaborative machinery is unavailable and the classical target (a proper, self-sufficient utterance) is correct.

**Open problem, stated honestly.** The dossier's own open-questions list flags that modern artifacts (async chat, PR review comments, docs with comment threads) sit *between* the two columns, and that the literature draws its line at whether the audience can talk back. Where exactly the collaborative machinery stops being available in semi-interactive media is **not resolved by any source in this dossier**. Do not fabricate a rule for it.

## 5. Repair is universal, language-neutral infrastructure

Dingemanse, Torreira & Enfield 2013, verbatim ([confirmed 3-0](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0078273)):

> Other-initiated repair sequences have been found in all languages investigated so far, and no language appears to lack an interjection for this function. Thus huh? is universal in the sense that a short, questioning interjection like it with the function of initiating repair is likely to be attested in all natural spoken languages.

Evidence base as verified: 10 languages studied in depth from field recordings across 5 continents (196 corpus tokens — Cha'palaa, Icelandic, Spanish, Siwu, Dutch, Italian, Russian, Lao, Mandarin, Murrinh-Patha), plus 21 more from published sources (incl. Japanese, Korean, Tzeltal, Yeli Dnye, Zapotec, Kikongo, Herero).

Independent corroboration: Dingemanse, Roberts, Baranova et al. (2015), 12 languages from 8 families with varied typological profiles, conclude there is **"a universal system for the real-time resolution of frequent breakdowns in communication," used on average about once per 1.4 minutes in every language** ([source](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0136100)).

**Why this matters for a language-neutral skill.** "Signal trouble → speaker repeats or reformulates" is *not* an English or Western convention. It is documented infrastructure a coaching skill may rely on across languages. Note the frequency: repair firing roughly every 1.4 minutes is the normal operating state of successful conversation — **repair is not failure, it is the mechanism.**

**Mandatory hedges (from the dossier's caveats).**
- This is an **inductive generalization** over ~10+21 (2013) and 12 (2015) languages out of roughly 7,000. The authors hedge with "likely to be attested". Do not state it as proven-in-all.
- Universality of the mechanism does **not** entail cross-cultural uniformity in *willingness* to initiate repair. The 2015 study found cross-language variation in the mix of open vs. restricted repair formats.

## 6. Grice's Cooperative Principle — verbatim, and Grice's own limits on it

### 6a. What Grice actually wrote

The CP is a principle of **rational cooperation toward the accepted purpose of a talk exchange**. It is not a style guide. Grice 1975, "Logic and Conversation", *Syntax and Semantics 3: Speech Acts*, pp. 45–47 ([confirmed 3-0](http://www.sfu.ca/~jeffpell/Cogs300/GriceLogicConvers75.pdf)):

> We might then formulate a rough general principle which participants will be expected (ceteris paribus) to observe, namely: **Make your conversational contribution such as is required, at the stage at which it occurs, by the accepted purpose or direction of the talk exchange in which you are engaged.** One might label this the COOPERATIVE PRINCIPLE.

> Echoing Kant, I call these categories **Quantity, Quality, Relation, and Manner.**

| Category | Maxims (verbatim as verified) |
|---|---|
| **Quantity** | 1. "Make your contribution as informative as is required (for the current purposes of the exchange)." 2. **"Do not make your contribution more informative than is required."** |
| **Quality** | Supermaxim: "Try to make your contribution one that is true." 1. "Do not say what you believe to be false." 2. "Do not say that for which you lack adequate evidence." |
| **Relation** | "Under the category of RELATION I place a single maxim, namely, 'Be relevant.'" (exactly one maxim) |
| **Manner** | Relates "not (like the previous categories) to what is said but, rather, to HOW what is said is to be said". Supermaxim: **"Be perspicuous"**, with **four** sub-maxims. |

**Honest gap.** The dossier's verbatim quote is truncated at Manner's sub-maxim list, so this document does **not** reproduce all four. The sub-maxims named elsewhere in the dossier are *avoid ambiguity* and *be orderly* (dossier's own language caveat on this claim), plus *"Be brief (avoid unnecessary prolixity)"* as quoted by Clark & Brennan when they cite Grice. **The fourth is not present in the dossier and is therefore not stated here.** Do not fill it in from memory.

**Why the verbatim form matters (the dossier's own point).** Any skill that paraphrases these as "be clear, be brief, be honest, be relevant" has silently dropped: the **second Quantity maxim** (do not be MORE informative than required), the **Quality supermaxim / evidence distinction**, and the fact that these are **expectations hearers exploit, not instructions writers follow.**

### 6b. Grice's own limits — he excludes politeness and calls his scheme too narrow

Grice 1975, p. 47, verbatim ([confirmed 3-0](http://www.sfu.ca/~jeffpell/Cogs300/GriceLogicConvers75.pdf)):

> There are, of course, all sorts of other maxims (aesthetic, social, or moral in character), such as 'Be polite', that are also normally observed by participants in talk exchanges, and these may also generate nonconventional implicatures. The conversational maxims, however, and the conversational implicatures connected with them, are specially connected (I hope) with the particular purposes that talk (and so, talk exchange) is adapted to serve and is primarily employed to serve. **I have stated my maxims as if this purpose were a maximally effective exchange of information; this specification is, of course, too narrow, and the scheme needs to be generalized** to allow for such general purposes as influencing or directing the actions of others.

Two primary-source limits, in Grice's own words:
1. **"Be polite" is NOT one of the conversational maxims.** It is a different kind of maxim (aesthetic/social/moral). A skill that packages "Gricean maxims" as a tone or politeness rubric is misrepresenting the source.
2. **Grice flags his own scheme as too narrow** — stated as if the purpose of talk were maximally effective information exchange, and needing generalization.

### 6c. The maxims are not rules to maximally obey — but state the flouting point precisely

**What people believe.** "Grice's maxims are rules to follow: be brief, be clear, be relevant, be truthful."

**What the evidence actually says.** They are not style rules. Grice's own machinery depends on maxims being *breakable*: he distinguishes four ways of failing a maxim — quiet **VIOLATION**, **OPTING OUT**, **CLASH**, and **FLOUTING** — and when a maxim is blatantly and non-misleadingly failed, Grice says the maxim is being **EXPLOITED** to generate a conversational implicature. Grice's own examples of exploitation are irony, metaphor ("You are the cream in my coffee"), meiosis and hyperbole ("Every nice girl loves a sailor") — all floutings of the first maxim of Quality. This is all verbatim in the source ([full chapter](https://lawandlogic.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/grice1975logic-and-conversation.pdf), p. 49; verified word-for-word).

**But the claim as originally framed was REFUTED (1-2), and the refutation is the payload.** Two overreaches were killed:

- **"ONLY flouting generates conversational implicature" is false by Grice's own organization.** Grice divides his examples into **three** groups, and implicature arises in all three. Group A is titled verbatim "Examples in which no maxim is violated, or at least in which it is not clear that any maxim is violated" — the out-of-petrol/garage exchange yields an implicature with no maxim broken. Group B involves a CLASH. Only Group C is exploitation/flouting. So "the maxims generate meaning *precisely* when they are visibly broken" overstates: flouting is **one** of several implicature-generating routes.
- **"Purely descriptive" is false.** Grice explicitly rejects the reading of the CP as a mere "dull... empirical fact", declares himself "enough of a rationalist", and argues it is "REASONABLE for us to follow" and that we "SHOULD NOT abandon" it (p. 48). The CP has a normative/rational dimension.

**What survives, and is the operational rule:**
- The maxims are **not a style checklist**, and treating them as rules to be maximally obeyed destroys the mechanism by which irony, metaphor, meiosis and hyperbole mean anything. (Grice's own examples.)
- Flouting a maxim **blatantly and non-misleadingly** is a legitimate, meaning-generating move — Grice calls it exploitation.
- But do **not** claim flouting is the *only* source of implicature; Grice's Group A and Group B contradict that.

## 7. The anglocentrism challenge — this matters because the skill claims language-neutrality

If `easy_speak` ships Gricean maxims as its normative core while claiming to be language-neutral, two primary sources say it is shipping Anglo norms under a universalist label.

### 7a. Keenan (Ochs) on Malagasy

Keenan (Ochs) 1976, *Language in Society* 5(1):67–80, published abstract, verbatim ([confirmed 2-1](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/language-in-society/article/abs/universality-of-conversational-postulates/4E0D5F41B8D53C28FADFB5A0E04BD534)):

> Grice's analysis of conversational maxims and implicatures is examined in the light of Malagasy language and ways of speaking. A cultural contrast in primary assumptions is described. Grician analysis retains usefulness but within the perspective of a comparative typology in which locally valid systems may differ strikingly in what is marked and unmarked. An ethnographic base and ethnological comparison are required.

(The "Grician" spelling is the journal's own; it is reproduced to preserve the quote.)

Based on fieldwork in a Malagasy village, Keenan argues Grice's maxims — paradigmatically the **first maxim of Quantity** — do not hold as stated: Malagasy speakers routinely withhold information as **normal cooperative behaviour, not as implicature-generating flouting**, because possessing exclusive information carries prestige and committing to it risks face. A Stanford handout block-quotes Keenan p. 70 directly: "Interlocutors regularly violate this maxim. They regularly provide less information than is required by their conversational partner, even though they have access to the necessary information."

**Her own conclusion is a relativization, not a demolition** (this hedge is load-bearing): Gricean analysis "retains usefulness" but must be relativized to a comparative typology in which "locally valid systems may differ strikingly in what is marked and unmarked".

**The operational consequence.** A skill that scores an utterance as "under-informative → therefore implicating something" is applying a **locally-valid (Anglo) markedness assumption, not a universal inference rule.** The Quantity default ("say as much as is required") is an English/Anglo-American norm; in Malagasy the unmarked case is informational reticence — and by extension the same risk applies to any high-context, face-sensitive speech community.

**Note the split vote (2-1) and the counter-argument.** Ellen Prince ("Grice and Universality: A Reappraisal", 1982, Univ. of Pennsylvania ms., endorsed in von Fintel & Matthewson's "Universals in Semantics") re-analyses Keenan's own data and argues the Maxim of Quantity is *necessary* to account for Malagasy understandings "at least as necessary as it is in English" — because withholding confers prestige only if hearers recognize that the speaker *could have said more and did not*, which is itself a Quantity-driven inference. The dossier also notes it could retrieve only the publisher's abstract; the 14-page article body is paywalled. **State Keenan as a serious, live challenge to Gricean universality — not as a settled refutation of Grice.**

### 7b. Wierzbicka

Wierzbicka, *Cross-Cultural Pragmatics* (1991), Ch. 1, verbatim ([confirmed 3-0](https://api.pageplace.de/preview/DT0400.9783112329764_A41028527/preview-9783112329764_A41028527.pdf)):

> It demonstrates the anglocentrism of supposedly universal 'maxims' of human conversational behaviour of the kind put forward by Grice (1975) or Leech (1983). It also shows how progress in cross-cultural pragmatics has been hampered by the use of inadequate conceptual tools: in particular, of unanalysed, obscure and protean global labels such as 'directness', 'self-assertion', 'distance', 'intimacy', 'solidarity', 'harmony', 'informality', and so on, which have led to paradoxical and contradictory conclusions; and it proposes a method whereby different communicative styles can be clarified in terms of 'cultural scripts' written in the metalanguage of universal semantic primitives.

This is a **central thesis of the book, not an aside** (corroborated by the publisher's jacket copy: "This book challenges the approaches to human interaction based on supposedly universal 'maxims of conversation' and 'principles of politeness'").

Two consequences for this skill:

1. **The fix for a maxim-based system is not "add more maxims" — it is "state the local script explicitly."** Wierzbicka's positive proposal is culture-specific cultural scripts in a natural semantic metalanguage of universal semantic primitives, precisely so comparison is possible without ethnocentric bias.
2. **A rubric that rates text on "directness", "warmth", "harmony", "informality" or "solidarity" is NOT language-neutral.** Those are unanalysed English words whose senses differ across cultures. Scoring on them is scoring against Anglo concepts while claiming universality. This is a hard prohibition for any scoring rubric the skill ships.

## 8. Audience design (Bell) and its contested status

### 8a. What is confirmed

**The role hierarchy.** Bell 1984, verbatim ([confirmed 3-0](https://web.stanford.edu/~eckert/PDF/bell1984.pdf)):

> If a linguistic variable shows style variation according to any audience role, that presupposes variation according to all roles closer to the speaker. Speaker ⇄ Addressee ⇄ Auditor ⇄ Overhearer ... Still more rigorous is the quantitative hypothesis: The effect on linguistic variation of each role is less than the effect of the role next closest to the speaker. Speaker > Addressee > Auditor > Overhearer ... Eavesdroppers, being unknown, by definition cannot affect a speaker's style.

Roles are defined by three binary attributes:

| Role | Known | Ratified | Addressed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Addressee | + | + | + |
| Auditor | + | + | − |
| Overhearer | + | − | n.a. (unratified) |
| Eavesdropper | − | − | n.a. (unratified) |

**The natural experiment.** Bell 1984, p. 171, verbatim ([confirmed 2-1](https://web.stanford.edu/~eckert/PDF/bell1984.pdf)):

> In Figure 9, it is shown how four individual newscasters shifted systematically from more standard values of the (t)-voicing variable on station YA (higher-status audience) to less standard values on the lower-status ZB. This situation holds constant all the other, nonpersonal variables thought likely to influence style shift: topic, setting, degree of attention, and so forth. Only the two audiences - as characterized by a sample survey (Bell 1982b) - are different.

In the New Zealand public broadcasting system, a pool of newscasters read the same news from the same studios for two stations with demographically different audiences. Newscasters also converged on a station **house style**, "showing unusually little scatter around a station's mean" (p. 172).

**The defensible takeaway:** *who is listening* is a real lever on how one speaks, and it is graded by role distance.

### 8b. What is refuted — the overreach

**What people believe.** "Bell proved that audience is *the* primary controllable lever for how you speak, and that Labov's attention-to-speech account is dead."

**What the evidence actually says.** The *attribution* to Bell 1984 is textually accurate — Bell's section is literally headed "A NONSTARTER: ATTENTION PAID TO SPEECH" and reads "Empirical foundation for the attention variable is notably lacking, however." But the claim as framed was **REFUTED (1-2)** on three grounds:

1. **Bell retracts the topic/setting sub-claim himself.** In "Back in style: reworking audience design" (Eckert & Rickford eds., CUP 2001, 139–169, [source](https://www.scribd.com/document/705914734/back-in-style-reworking-audience-design)) he revisits his 1984 point that topic/setting shifts derive from audience association and writes that it "was one of the 'bolder' ones put forward in Bell (1984)... The evidence is, however, not clear."
2. **The 1984 abstract was cherry-picked.** The quote usually stops at "...derive their effect by association with addressee types." The very next sentences read: "These style shifts are mainly responsive - caused by a situational change. Speakers can also use style as initiative, to redefine the existing situation. Initiative style is primarily referee design: divergence from the addressee and towards an absent reference group." **Bell 1984 is a two-dimension theory (responsive/audience + initiative/referee).** Presenting only the responsive half and calling audience "the primary lever" misrepresents it.
3. **Attention is not dead.** Sharma (2018, "Style dominance: Attention, audience, and the 'real me'", *Language in Society* 47(1):1–31, [source](https://doi.org/10.1017/s0047404517000835)) is cited as the counter-source.

**Further heavy qualifications on the newscaster study (2-1 vote), recorded in synthesis F12:**
- "Only the audiences differ" is **Bell's inference**, not an independently validated fact. YA and ZB also differ in format and plausibly in institutional stylebook policy; the house-style convergence is itself compatible with an institutional-norm explanation.
- **n = 4 newscasters, 1974 data, no inferential statistics on Figure 9.**
- Bell immediately concedes it is "not a case of simple convergence".
- **The variable (/t/-voicing) is English/NZ-specific.** The *principle* of audience design may generalize; **this variable may not.**
- The cited Stanford PDF is an image-only scan with no text layer and cannot be quote-checked directly.

### 8c. Two more refuted claims that constrain "design for your audience"

**Horton & Keysar 1996 — "audience design is a second, optional stage that vanishes under time pressure": REFUTED (0-3).** The quote is authentic (Cognition 59(1):91–117, [source](https://europepmc.org/article/MED/8857472)) and the authors did conclude speakers "do not engage in audience design in the initial stages" and only monitor-and-adjust afterward. But: (a) **the first author abandoned the model** — Horton & Gerrig replaced monitoring-and-adjustment with a memory-based account in which speakers "could both strategically AND AUTOMATICALLY gain access to information about others through domain-general memory processes" (TopiCS 2016, doi:10.1111/tops.12216); (b) **direct empirical contradiction** — Lockridge & Brennan (2002) found addressees' needs influence speakers' *early* syntactic choices, and Vanlangendonck et al. (2016) found "common ground exerts an EARLY, but incomplete effect" during speech planning (doi:10.1080/23273798.2016.1148747). **What survives:** perspective-taking under load is imperfect and incomplete. **What does not:** the categorical two-stage architecture, and any coaching line that says audience design is optional and the first thing dropped under pressure.

**Keysar et al. 2000 — "mutual knowledge is pure post-hoc error correction on an egocentric first pass": REFUTED (0-3).** Every number is verbatim and correct (initial fixation on the shared referent delayed by 503 ms; decision fixation delayed by 1,449 ms; addressees' hands reached for the object hidden from the speaker on 23% of test trials vs. 0% control; n=20, [source](https://thegricean.github.io/xprag-ling247/readings/keysar2000.pdf)). The refutation is about **strength, not fact**: the paper itself says people "**occasionally** use an egocentric heuristic", and states "Our experiments cannot distinguish between the no-constraint and the partial-constraint theories" (p. 37). Asserting the architecture categorically ("not a filter on the initial search") states as established what the authors explicitly refuse to establish. Also **explicitly English** ("Twenty native English speakers participated"), co-present visual referential game with a confederate director — do not generalize to other languages or to written/asynchronous communication. **What survives:** listeners do sometimes consider referents the speaker cannot see, so "the reader knows what I know" fails in both directions — the speaker's *and* the listener's — but as an occasional heuristic, not a fixed processing architecture.

## Unverified — do not rely on

These claims were extracted but never adjudicated by the verification pass (budget). They may be true. They are **not** evidence, and the skill must not assert them.

| ID | Claim | Source |
|---|---|---|
| R101 | Basic exchanges (director presents an NP; matcher simply accepts, no repair/expansion turn) rose across trials 1–6 from 18% to 55, 75, 80, 88, 84% (F(1,55)=84.19, p<.001) — making repair/expansion turns a countable index of missing shared ground | Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs 1986 |
| R105 | Across 196 tokens from 10 unrelated languages, the open-repair interjection converges on a monosyllable with at most a glottal onset ([ʔ] or [h]), unrounded low front/central vowel, questioning intonation | Dingemanse et al. 2013 |
| R106 | The repair *form* is a conventionalized, learned word calibrated to each language's phonology and prosody (Spanish /e/ rising ~7 semitones; Cha'palaa /a/ falling ~2 semitones) — so repair markers do NOT transfer across languages | Dingemanse et al. 2013 |

## What this does NOT license

1. **Do NOT tell users to "ship a rough draft and let the reader fix it" in writing, broadcast, sermons, large lectures, or any one-way channel.** Clark & Wilkes-Gibbs explicitly exclude these. Where the addressee cannot repair, the **self-sufficient utterance IS the right target**. Least collaborative effort is a *dialogue* principle. (Dossier DO-NOT-OVERREACH list.)
2. **Do NOT present Gricean maxims as a style checklist or a tone/politeness rubric.** Grice himself excludes "Be polite" from the conversational maxims and calls his own scheme "too narrow". Maxims are expectations hearers exploit, not instructions writers follow. ([confirmed 3-0](http://www.sfu.ca/~jeffpell/Cogs300/GriceLogicConvers75.pdf))
3. **Do NOT say "only flouting generates implicature" or "the maxims are purely descriptive".** Both were refuted against Grice's own text: implicature also arises in his Group A (no maxim violated) and Group B (clash), and he explicitly rejects the purely-empirical reading of the CP.
4. **Do NOT paraphrase the maxims as "be clear, be brief, be honest, be relevant".** That silently deletes the second Quantity maxim ("do not make your contribution *more* informative than is required") and the Quality evidence sub-maxim.
5. **Do NOT claim the four Manner sub-maxims from memory.** The dossier's quote is truncated; only *avoid ambiguity*, *be orderly*, and (via Clark & Brennan) *be brief (avoid unnecessary prolixity)* appear in it.
6. **Do NOT score text on "directness", "warmth", "harmony", "solidarity", "informality" and call the result language-neutral.** Wierzbicka names these as unanalysed English labels that have produced "paradoxical and contradictory conclusions". ([confirmed 3-0](https://api.pageplace.de/preview/DT0400.9783112329764_A41028527/preview-9783112329764_A41028527.pdf))
7. **Do NOT treat Keenan as having refuted Grice.** She relativizes him ("Grician analysis retains usefulness"), the vote was 2-1, only the abstract was retrievable, and Prince's re-analysis argues Quantity is *necessary* to explain the Malagasy data.
8. **Do NOT generalize English back-channel tokens** ("uh huh", "yeah", the British "m") or Bell's /t/-voicing variable to other languages. The *functions* (acknowledgment, audience-graded style shift) are the transferable part; the *forms* are not.
9. **Do NOT claim repair universality is proven across all languages.** It is an inductive generalization over ~31 languages (2013) and 12 (2015) out of ~7,000; the authors hedge with "likely to be attested". And universality of the mechanism does not mean uniform *willingness* to use it — face norms modulate that heavily.
10. **Do NOT explain repair universality by convergent cultural evolution as if it were established.** [REFUTED 1-2] The mechanistic details are in the source ("Speaker change most often takes only between 100–300 milliseconds"; the codaless monosyllable requires "minimal encoding, planning, and articulation"), but the authors' own FAQ concedes: *"Does that mean we've demonstrated it's not innate? No, merely that the evidence militates against it"* ([source](https://markdingemanse.net/huh/frequently-asked-questions/)), and the paper says they "have to remain agnostic as to its ultimate origins." A UG/biological counter-account exists ([lingbuzz/002499](https://ling.auf.net/lingbuzz/002499)). **What survives:** repair signals are short, fast and low-cost everywhere, and making trouble cheap to signal is a defensible design goal. **What does not:** asserting the causal etiology.
11. **Do NOT cite "British operators always chunked 7+ digit numbers, and customers gave verbatim displays >70% of the time" as support for a general chunk-then-confirm rule.** [REFUTED 1-2] The real figure is **67.7%** (Clark & Schaefer 1987, Table 2, category (e), N=502) — "over 70%" is only reachable by hand-summing non-equivalent move categories, an aggregation the authors never perform. And chunk-then-confirm was the **minority** strategy, reserved for trouble — not the default. ([counter-source](https://web.stanford.edu/~clark/1980s/Clark,%20H.H.%20_%20Schaefer,%20E.F.%20_Collaborating%20on%20contributions%20to%20conversations_%201987.pdf)) **What survives:** the mechanism (limited immediate memory spans; conventional installments for long digit strings) and the verbatim quote about telephone numbers. **What does not:** the inflated percentage and the "this is what speakers normally do" prevalence framing.
12. **Do NOT present the eight medium constraints / eleven costs as a predictive engine "confirmed by Cohen (1984)".** [REFUTED 1-2] The eight constraints (copresence, visibility, audibility, cotemporality, simultaneity, sequentiality, reviewability, revisability) and the eleven costs (formulation, production, reception, understanding, start-up, delay, asynchrony, speaker change, display, fault, repair) are verbatim-accurate, as is "People should ground with those techniques available in a medium that lead to the least collaborative effort." But Clark & Brennan present the Cohen material as something the prediction "would account for" — a retrodictive fit, not a confirmed falsifiable prediction. Monk notes the framework "has yet to be fleshed out in sufficient detail" to yield mechanical predictions ([counter-source](https://www-users.york.ac.uk/~am1/Monk2003CommonGrnd.pdf)). **What survives:** the taxonomy, and the directional idea that medium costs shape grounding technique.
13. **Do NOT call audience "the primary lever" or declare attention-to-speech dead.** [REFUTED 1-2] Bell's 1984 theory has two dimensions (responsive/audience *and* initiative/referee); he walked back the topic/setting derivation himself in 2001; Sharma (2018) revives attention. And the newscaster evidence is n=4, 1974, with no inferential statistics and a plausible institutional-stylebook confound.
14. **Do NOT claim perspective-taking is an optional second stage that disappears under time pressure.** [REFUTED 0-3] The first author abandoned that model; Lockridge & Brennan and Vanlangendonck et al. found early common-ground effects. Perspective-taking under load is **incomplete**, not **absent**.
15. **Do NOT use brevity as a diagnostic of shared understanding.** Compression is *a consequence* of grounding, and the causal mechanism is contested (Bangerter et al. found lexical reduction even where object-specific pacts cannot form). Use the acceptance/repair test instead: name it briefly; if the other party proceeds without repair, it was grounded.
16. **Do NOT assume the dialogue rules extend to semi-interactive media** (async chat, PR comments, docs with comment threads). No source in this dossier resolves where the collaborative machinery stops. This is an open question, not a gap to be filled from intuition.
