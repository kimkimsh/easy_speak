# Presentations and slides: what the evidence actually supports

> **What this is.** The defensible evidence on slide, diagram and narration design — with the famous numbers (Mayer's "23 out of 23 tests, d = 0.86") removed, because they did not survive verification, and replaced with independent meta-analytic estimates.
> **Evidence base.** 7 confirmed claims, 6 refuted claims, 4 unverified claims, from the adversarial verification pass on the `multimedia-presentations` dossier.
> **Load this when.** The coaching task involves slides, decks, diagrams, figures with labels, narrated video, screencasts, captions, or any situation where the speaker's words and a visual are competing for the same audience at the same time.

---

## The one-paragraph summary

The behavioural effects are real and replicated: putting related things next to each other helps, signposting structure helps, and how you combine spoken and written words matters. But they are **smaller than the popular figures**, **conditional on the material and the audience**, and — most importantly — **the standard explanation for why they work is not established**. Cognitive-load measures largely fail to show the extraneous-load reduction that the whole "reduce cognitive load" story depends on. A skill may coach the behaviour. A skill may **not** coach the mechanism as if it had been measured.

## 2. Contiguity: put the label on the thing — but it is conditional, and closer is not always better

**Rule.** Put the label next to the thing it labels; put the caption next to the figure; speak alongside the visual you are describing rather than before or after it. This is the best-supported single layout move in this document.

**Evidence.** Ginns's meta-analysis of 50 independent experimental effects (2,375 students) found a large weighted mean advantage for presentations that integrate related information in space or time: *"the weighted mean effect size of the 50 effects was large, d = 0.85 (95% confidence interval 0.68–1.02)"* (Ginns 2006, [confirmed 3-0](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0959475206000806)). The newer, larger random-effects meta-analysis is more conservative: *"A random effects meta-analysis of 58 independent comparisons (n = 2426) produced an overall effect size of g = 0.63 (p < 0.001)"* (Schroeder & Cenkci 2018, [confirmed 2-1](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ1186641)).

### 2a. d = 0.85 is not a transferable constant

The pooled mean is significantly heterogeneous: Q = 117.77, df = 49, p < .001, which is exactly why Ginns tested a moderator (reported in the verifier evidence for the confirmed contiguity claims, Ginns 2006, [confirmed 3-0](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0959475206000806)). Do not quote 0.85 as the expected gain in an arbitrary context. If you must quote one number, quote g = 0.63 (larger k) or the pooled all-principles g = 0.38.

### 2b. The moderator is material complexity, and it is large

*"The analyses indicate that, for complex learning materials in particular, increasing either the spatial or temporal contiguity of related elements of information can lead to substantial learning gains"* (Ginns 2006, [confirmed 3-0](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0959475206000806)). The subgroup estimates: **high element interactivity d = 0.78; low element interactivity d = 0.28, and not statistically significant (p > .05)**.

Ginns's own wording for the simple-material case — carry the hedge, do not upgrade it: *"lack of integration of related elements may be inconsequential because the low levels of element interactivity, and consequent low levels of intrinsic cognitive load, leave sufficient cognitive capacity available for learning, despite the presence of extraneous cognitive load."*

**Operational consequence.** Spend contiguity effort where the content is hard and interdependent — a system diagram where the parts only make sense in relation to each other, a formula and the figure it describes. On a simple slide, moving a caption two inches is close to a rounding error. Coach it as a *priority ordering*, not a universal law.

**Do not read d = 0.28 as a demonstrated null.** The pass-1 synthesis flags it explicitly: low-EI d = 0.28 is *a non-detected effect, not a demonstrated null* (the primary studies had roughly n ≈ 10 per condition). "It probably doesn't matter much here" is supportable; "it provably doesn't matter" is not.

### 2c. Extreme proximity can backfire — "closer is always better" is false

Beege et al. (2019) document a **spatial contiguity failure** in which maximum proximity underperformed medium proximity: cramming related *and unrelated* elements together causes interference (synthesis F3, vote 3-0, [Frontiers in Education 2019](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/education/articles/10.3389/feduc.2019.00086/full)). The rule is *integrate what refers to each other*, not *minimize whitespace*.

### 2d. What Schroeder & Cenkci's moderator analysis does and does not say

The abstract says: *"Moderator analyses indicated that integrated designs have benefited learning across many intervention-related and context-related moderator variables"* ([confirmed 2-1](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ1186641)). The dissenting verifier's ground for objection is worth carrying: the abstract says **"many"**, not *all*, and the paper's own stated purpose was to find the conditions under which integration works best. So: the effect is robust across many conditions. It is **not** established to be boundary-condition-free. Do not coach "this always works".

## 3. THE MECHANISM RESULT — the most important thing in this document

Everyone, including most slide-design books, explains all of the above with one sentence: *"it reduces extraneous cognitive load."* **That explanation is not established, and for the split-attention effect specifically it is contradicted by the load measurements themselves.**

### What people believe

> The mechanism is extraneous cognitive load: split attention forces the audience to spend limited working memory searching for and matching mutually referring elements (a diagram and its formula) that are unintelligible in isolation, leaving less capacity for learning.

### What the evidence actually says — REFUTED (vote 1-2)

The quote usually cited for this sits in the **theoretical introduction** of Ginns (2006) — a meta-analysis of *learning outcomes*, not of load. Its own wording is hedged: *"Another mental process which is **potentially** extraneous to learning is the need to search for and match mutually referring but disparate elements of information…"*. The paper measures **whether** integration helps (it does). It does not measure **why**. Citing a theory-restatement paragraph as if it were a finding is citing framing as evidence (REFUTED 1-2, [Ginns 2006](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0959475206000806)).

Direct mechanism tests fail to find the predicted working-memory signature:

- **Cierniak, Scheiter & Gerjets (2009)**, *Computers in Human Behavior* 25(2), 315–324: split-source learners scored **worse on learning** *"but did not show worse secondary task performance than learners with integrated format"* — the dual-task load probe detected no extra load. The authors state the results *"contradict the assumption that only an increase in extraneous load"* explains the effect ([counter-source](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.chb.2008.12.020)).
- **Schroeder & Cenkci (2020)**, *Journal of Educational Psychology* 112(2), 254–270 — a systematic review of **41 comparisons**, verbatim: *"Our analysis of 41 comparisons found that measures of cognitive load largely do not support the extraneous cognitive load explanation, nor did we find compelling evidence that integrated graphic designs significantly influence any measure of cognitive load compared with spatially distant designs. We propose an alternative explanation for the principle in which integrated graphic designs may facilitate the allocation of germane resources and integrative cognitive processing rather than reduce extraneous cognitive load and discuss a theoretical foundation for testing this hypothesis."* ([confirmed 2-1](https://www.ovid.com/journals/jedup/pdf/10.1037/edu0000372~do-measures-of-cognitive-load-explain-the-spatial); ERIC record [EJ1240932](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ1240932))

Note precisely what the authors propose: a germane-processing/integration account, offered as a **hypothesis to be tested**, not as an established replacement mechanism. Do not swap one confident story for another.

### The honest dissent (this claim passed 2-1, not 3-0)

The dissenting verifier's objection is legitimate and must be carried: the 2020 paper is a **systematic review, not a meta-analysis of load effect sizes**. Its language is vote-counting language — *"largely do not support"*, *"no compelling evidence"* — with no pooled effect size and no CI for the load outcome. It establishes **absence of supporting evidence across a set of contested self-report instruments**, which is weaker than a positive demonstration that load is unmoved. A counter-source exists (Skulmowski 2023, *Cognitive Processing* 24(2), 187–198, [doi 10.1007/s10339-022-01123-z](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10339-022-01123-z)).

So state it at exactly this strength: **the extraneous-load explanation of the split-attention effect is unsupported by the load measurements, not disproven.**

### And the load story is not uniformly dead — signaling is the counterexample

The same verification pass confirmed, 3-0, that in the signaling meta-analysis *"Cognitive load was significantly reduced"* (Schneider et al. 2018, [confirmed 3-0](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1747938X17300581)), with a within-review estimate of g = 0.25, 95% CI [0.04, 0.45], k = 27 (as reported in Noetel et al. 2022). So load moves for signaling; it does not visibly move for spatial split-attention. Report the split; do not flatten it in either direction.

### A tempting quantitative version of this argument is ALSO refuted — REFUTED (vote 0-3)

> **What people (and an earlier draft of this knowledge base) want to say:** design interventions barely move cognitive load (g = 0.22, 95% CI [0.04, 0.40], k = 68) even though they clearly move learning (g = 0.38); for signaling, load reduction is g = 0.11 [0.04, 0.18] — roughly a *quarter* of the same principle's learning effect (g = 0.43 [0.35, 0.50], k = 209). The load pathway is far too small to carry the behavioural effect.

The **numbers are verbatim-correct** in Noetel et al. 2022. The **argument built from them is not** (REFUTED 0-3, [Noetel et al. 2022](https://doi.org/10.31234/osf.io/pynzr)):

1. **The "quarter" ratio is manufactured by mixing two different reviews.** Signaling's load figure g = 0.11 comes from Xie et al. 2017; signaling's learning figure g = 0.43 comes from Schneider et al. 2018. The *same-review* load figure from Schneider et al. 2018 is **g = 0.25, 95% CI [0.04, 0.45], k = 27** — roughly *half*, not a quarter. The refuting number is inside the claim's own quoted passage.
2. **"Barely move" overstates a significant effect.** The overall load effect g = 0.22 [0.04, 0.40] excludes zero.

**Consequence for the skill.** The mechanism critique must be made **qualitatively** (the load measures do not support the extraneous-load explanation of split attention) and **never** as an arithmetic "the load pathway is too small". The arithmetic version does not survive.

### The operational rule that follows

- **Do** coach: *"Put the label on the diagram. Independent meta-analysis, g = 0.63, k = 58."*
- **Do not** coach: *"Put the label on the diagram, because it reduces the audience's cognitive load."* That second clause is an unmeasured story.
- More generally, the pass-1 synthesis flags (vote 3-0 ×3) that **"reduce extraneous load" cannot be applied as a hard, audience-independent rule** at all — the extraneous/germane distinction is definitionally tautological and load is frequently inferred backwards from post-test scores (de Jong 2010, [Instructional Science 38:105–134](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11251-009-9110-0)). If the skill wants to say something about load, it has to first fix *who is listening*.

## 4. Signaling: signpost the structure

**Rule.** Add cues that highlight the organization of the essential material — headings, an outline, highlighting, verbal signposting ("three things; here is the first").

**Evidence.** 103 studies, N = 12,201, 145 pairwise comparisons spanning 46 years of research: *"The retention (g+ = 0.53, 95% CI [0.42, 0.64]) and transfer (g+ = 0.33, 95% CI [0.22, 0.43]) sizes support the positive effect of signaling… Cognitive load was significantly reduced. In contrast to the expertise reversal effect hypothesis, prior knowledge was not identified as a moderator of the signaling effect."* (Schneider, Beege, Nebel & Rey 2018, [confirmed 3-0](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1747938X17300581))

Three things to carry from this, all of them load-bearing:

1. **Retention and transfer are different sizes.** Signposting helps people *remember* (0.53) more than it helps them *use* the material in a new situation (0.33). Do not quote one number for both.
2. **Prior knowledge was NOT a moderator.** This directly contradicts the expertise-reversal prediction of cognitive load theory. So the standard advice "signpost for novices, drop it for experts" is **not supported** for signaling specifically — signal for everyone.
3. Point 2 sits in tension with the broader expertise-reversal literature, where high-assistance instruction gives d = +0.505 for low-prior-knowledge learners and **d = −0.428 for high-prior-knowledge learners** (synthesis F2, vote 3-0 ×3, [Learning and Instruction meta-analysis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0959475225000660)). Resolution: expertise reversal is real for *scaffolding and worked support in general*, but signaling did not show it. Do not extrapolate one to the other.

## 5. Redundancy: the "never put text on your slides" rule is half wrong

This is the most misused finding in slide advice, and the corrected version is genuinely actionable.

### What people believe — REFUTED (vote 1-2)

> Graphics + spoken narration beats graphics + narration + on-screen text duplicating the narration. Supported in 16 of 16 experimental tests, median d = 0.86. Therefore: never put your script on the slide and read it aloud.

### What the evidence actually says

The quote is real. Mayer & Fiorella (2014) do write it. But it is a **vote-count from a narrative self-review by the theory's own author**, and it does not survive contact with the independent literature (REFUTED 1-2, [Mayer & Fiorella 2014, Cambridge Handbook of Multimedia Learning, 2nd ed., ch. 12](https://edtechuvic.ca/wp-content/uploads/sites/11/2022/09/principles-for-reducing-extraneous-processing-in-multimedia-learning-coherence-signaling-redundancy-spatial-contiguity-and-temporal-contiguity-principles.pdf)):

1. **Same-page contradiction the popular retelling omits.** Three sentences after the quoted line, the chapter itself says: *"Three important boundary conditions are that the redundancy effect can be eliminated or even reversed when the learners are experienced, the on-screen text is short, or the material lacks graphics."*
2. **The 16/16 tally is transfer-only, and retention reverses.** The chapter restricts the tally to *"tests of problem-solving transfer"*. Mayer's own study (Mayer & Johnson 2008, *J. Educ. Psych.* 100(2), 380–386), reported in the same chapter, found the **redundant** group *outperformed* the nonredundant group on **retention** (d = 0.47 Exp 1, d = 0.70 Exp 2) with null transfer differences (d = 0.04, d = 0.15). "Produces deeper learning" is measure-dependent, so the unqualified form is false as written.
3. **The largest meta-analysis of Mayer's own corpus collapses the number.** Cromley & Chen (2025), *Educational Research Review* — 92 Mayer-authored articles, 181 studies, 591 effects, multilevel models — report *"avoiding redundant captions with narration (g = 0.14)"*, listed among the principles that **showed non-significant effects** ([counter-source](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.edurev.2025.100730)).

### What survives — CONFIRMED (vote 3-0), and it is more useful than the myth

Adesope & Nesbit (2012), 57 independent studies, *Journal of Educational Psychology* 104(1), 250–263 ([confirmed 3-0](https://doi.org/10.1037/a0026147)), verbatim:

> *"Overall, this meta-analysis revealed that outcomes comparing spoken–written and written-only presentations did not differ, but students who learned from spoken–written presentations outperformed those who learned from spoken-only presentations. This effect was dependent on learners' prior knowledge, pacing of presentation, and inclusion of animation or diagrams. Specifically, the advantages of spoken–written presentations over spoken-only presentations were found for low prior knowledge learners, system-paced learning materials, and picture-free materials. In comparison with verbatim, spoken–written presentations, presentations displaying key terms extracted from spoken narrations were [associated with better learning outcomes and accounted for much of the advantage]."*

Quantified via Noetel's re-extraction: overall **g = 0.15 [0.08, 0.22], k = 57**; **adding text to audio g = 0.29 [0.20, 0.39], k = 34**; **adding audio to text g = −0.04 [−0.14, 0.06], k = 23 (null)**.

So the direction of the popular rule is, on the independent evidence, **backwards in three named conditions**. Redundancy *helps* — spoken + written beats spoken-only — when:

| Redundancy HELPS when… | Source |
|---|---|
| the audience has **low prior knowledge** | Adesope & Nesbit 2012 ([confirmed 3-0](https://doi.org/10.1037/a0026147)) |
| the material is **system-paced** (the audience cannot pause or rewind — i.e. a live talk) | same |
| the material is **picture-free** (no diagram or animation competing for the eye) | same |
| the on-screen text is **key terms extracted from the narration**, not verbatim | same — partial redundancy beat verbatim and *"accounted for much of the advantage"* |

Note the second row carefully: a **live presentation is system-paced by definition**. That is one of the three conditions under which text-plus-speech beats speech alone.

### The worked rule

The harmful case is narrow and specific: **full-sentence verbatim script on the slide, read aloud, over a diagram, to an experienced audience.** Almost everything else is neutral-to-helpful.

**Before (the harmful case):**

> *Slide:* "Because the write-ahead log is flushed before the commit acknowledgement is returned to the client, a crash between the flush and the acknowledgement can leave a transaction durable but unacknowledged, which the client must handle by retrying idempotently."
> *Speaker, over a sequence diagram:* reads the sentence aloud, verbatim.

**After (partial redundancy — key terms extracted from the narration):**

> *Slide, beside the diagram:* **"Durable but unacknowledged → client retries idempotently"**
> *Speaker:* says the full sentence, once, while the diagram is on screen.

This satisfies contiguity (§2: the key term sits beside the arrow it describes), signaling (§4: the phrase is the cue to the structure), and the *partial*-redundancy finding above (key terms beat verbatim). It is an illustration of the rules, not an additional experimental result.

## 6. Language scope — read this before generalizing anything above

- **Contiguity (spatial and temporal) is treated as language-neutral** in the pass-1 synthesis's language-scope list. Putting the label next to the referent is not an English convention.
- **The redundancy findings are NOT safe to generalize across languages or audiences.** The dossier attaches an explicit language caveat to the confirmed redundancy claim: the redundancy benefit **concentrates in L2 learners, non-fluent L1 readers and struggling readers** — the mechanism is *speech-decoding repair, not load management* — and the underlying literature is **overwhelmingly English-language postsecondary**. Relatedly, the single largest effect in Noetel's overview, **captioning g = 0.99 [0.60, 1.38], k = 15**, is specifically for **second-language video**. For a fluent-L1 audience watching a deck in their native language, that number does not transfer.
- **Signaling:** the dossier does not adjudicate cross-linguistic scope. Treat the cross-linguistic generality of signaling as **unestablished** — assert neither that it generalizes nor that it does not.
- **General source-quality warning from the synthesis (verbatim caveat, binding):** the academic layer here is strong (peer-reviewed, quote-verified), while the practitioner layer is much weaker. *"Do not let the skill's rules inherit more confidence than their sources."*

## 7. The box-score figures: what they are, and why they are quarantined

The five numbers below are the most-quoted statistics in slide-design advice. They come from Mayer & Fiorella (2014), a **Cambridge Handbook chapter written by the theory's own originator**. They are reproduced here **only** so the refutation is legible. **Do not cite them.**

| Principle (Mayer & Fiorella 2014 box-score) | Tally | Median d | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| Coherence (exclude extraneous material) | 23 / 23 | 0.86 | REFUTED as stated (0-3) |
| Signaling | 24 / 28 | 0.41 | Superseded — use Schneider et al. 2018 (§4) |
| Redundancy | 16 / 16 | 0.86 | REFUTED as stated (1-2) |
| Spatial contiguity | 22 / 22 | 1.10 | REFUTED as stated (0-3) |
| Temporal contiguity | 9 / 9 | 1.22 | REFUTED as stated (0-3) |

Why they fail, verbatim from the refutations:

1. **They are vote-counts from a self-review, not meta-analyses.** *"Its '22 of 22 tests, median d = 1.10' is a vote-count plus a median of hand-selected experiments (heavily his own lab), with no random-effects pooling, no heterogeneity test, no publication-bias correction, and no confidence intervals."* ([REFUTED 0-3](https://edtechuvic.ca/wp-content/uploads/sites/11/2022/09/principles-for-reducing-extraneous-processing-in-multimedia-learning-coherence-signaling-redundancy-spatial-contiguity-and-temporal-contiguity-principles.pdf))
2. **The tallies are unstable across editions of the same chapter** — proof they are an author-curated count, not an estimate. 1st ed. (2005): coherence 10/11, d = 1.32; signaling 3/3, d = 0.60; redundancy 10/10, d = 0.69; spatial contiguity 8/8, d = 1.11. 2nd ed. (2014): coherence 23/23, d = 0.86; signaling 24/28, d = 0.41; redundancy 16/16, d = 0.86; spatial 22/22, d = 1.10; temporal 9/9, d = 1.22. Coherence's median fell from 1.32 to 0.86 as more tests accrued.
3. **The "n of n" perfect-support tallies are contradicted by the chapter's own tables.** For coherence, Table 12.4 lists Mayer et al. 1996 Exp. 1 at **d = −0.17** — a *negative* effect favouring the embellished lesson — plus entries at d = 0.17 and d = 0.06. A "23 of 23" tally that contains a negative effect is a counting convention, not a result.
4. **Independent meta-analyses find effects roughly half to one-third the claimed size.** Spatial contiguity: Schroeder & Cenkci 2018 → **g = 0.63** (58 comparisons), not 1.10; Wang et al. 2016 (53 studies) → d_retention = 0.48, d_transfer = 0.39 ([source](https://devpsy.bnu.edu.cn/EN/Y2016/V32/I5/565)). Coherence: Rey 2012 → **11 of 39 studies support, 13 mixed, 15 no support; d = 0.30 retention / 0.48 transfer** ([counter-source](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.edurev.2012.05.003)).
5. **The superlatives are inventions.** "Coherence is the most consistently replicated of the five" is false — four of the five show 100% support rates in the same paragraph, and coherence's median (0.86) is only third-largest. "Contiguity yields the largest effects of the five" collapses using **Mayer's own later numbers**: Mayer (2017), *J. Computer Assisted Learning* 33(5), gives coherence 0.70, signalling 0.46, redundancy 0.87, spatial contiguity **0.79**, temporal contiguity 1.30 ([counter-source](https://doi.org/10.1111/jcal.12197)) — spatial contiguity now sits *below* redundancy.

### What survives from all of this

The **directions** survive. *Cut decorative filler; signpost structure; put labels on the thing; synchronize speech with the visual; do not read a verbatim script off a slide over a diagram to an expert audience.* Every one of those is directionally supported. **None of them may be sold with the box-score numbers.**

### One more refuted framing to avoid — the "shrinkage" narrative (REFUTED 1-2)

It is tempting to narrate the field as clean shrinkage: Ginns 2006 d = 0.85 → Schroeder & Cenkci 2018 g = 0.63 → Noetel's pooled contiguity g = 0.74 [0.67, 0.82], k = 46. The **numbers check out** (all three were verified during refutation), but the claim built around them was refuted for a load-bearing fabrication: it described Ginns's element-interactivity moderator as **"pre-registered"**. It was not, and could not have been — PROSPERO launched in **February 2011**, five years after publication; the paper's own abstract calls it *"a hypothesised moderator, the complexity of learning materials"* ([REFUTED 1-2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0959475206000806)). Say **"a hypothesised moderator, stated a priori in the introduction"**. Never say "pre-registered". And note that g = 0.74 is *not* monotonically below g = 0.63 — the sequence is not a clean downward trend, so do not narrate it as one.

## Unverified — do not rely on

These claims were extracted but never adjudicated in the verification pass. They may be true. They are **not** evidence, and the skill must not assert them.

- **Temporal tolerance window.** *"Baggett's (1984) study on learning from film with a soundtrack, found presenting audio sources of information up to 7 s after visual sources did not retard learning… At higher levels of asynchrony, Baggett found learning declined considerably (e.g. 14–21 s before or after visual presentation)."* This would be a lovely checkable timing heuristic for narration/slide alignment. **It was never adjudicated.** Do not ship "keep narration within 7 seconds of its visual" as a sourced rule.
- **Expertise-reversal boundary conditions on redundancy and signaling** (Mayer & Fiorella's *"the redundancy effect can be eliminated or even reversed when the learners are experienced, the on-screen text is short, or the material lacks graphics"*). Never adjudicated as a standalone claim — **but note** that the same three boundary conditions arrive independently and CONFIRMED via Adesope & Nesbit (§5), which is where the skill should source them from. Do not cite the Mayer chapter for them.
- **Signaling is the weakest of the five principles (24/28, d = 0.41).** Never adjudicated, and it is a box-score figure regardless. Superseded by Schneider et al. 2018 (§4).
- **The standalone heterogeneity claim (Q = 117.77, df = 49, p < .001).** Never adjudicated as its own claim, though the same statistic appears in the verifier evidence supporting the confirmed contiguity claims and is used on that basis in §2a.

## What this does NOT license

1. **Do not quote Mayer's box-scores.** Not "23 of 23", not "d = 0.86", not "22 of 22, d = 1.10", not "9 of 9, d = 1.22", not "16 of 16". They are vote-counts from the theory's own author, unstable across editions of the same chapter, internally contradicted by his own tables, and roughly half to one-third the size in independent meta-analysis. Use g = 0.38 (all principles), g = 0.63 (spatial contiguity), g+ = 0.53 / 0.33 (signaling retention / transfer), g = 0.15 (verbal redundancy) instead.
2. **Do not say "this reduces cognitive load."** For spatial contiguity / split attention, 41 comparisons found that load measures *"largely do not support the extraneous cognitive load explanation"* and that there is *"no compelling evidence"* that integrated designs move **any** measure of load. Coach the behaviour; do not narrate an unmeasured mechanism. (And note the split: load *was* significantly reduced for signaling — so do not flatten this into "load theory is dead" either.)
3. **Do not build the quantitative version of the mechanism critique.** "Load effects are far too small to carry the learning effects" was REFUTED 0-3 — the ratio was manufactured by mixing figures from two different reviews, and the overall load effect (g = 0.22 [0.04, 0.40]) excludes zero.
4. **Do not say "closer is always better."** Extreme proximity underperformed medium proximity in the documented spatial-contiguity failure. Integrate what mutually refers; do not merely minimize distance.
5. **Do not apply contiguity discipline uniformly.** It is large for complex, interdependent material (d = 0.78) and small and non-significant for simple material (d = 0.28, p > .05). Equally: do not call the simple-material case a demonstrated null — it is an underpowered non-detection.
6. **Do not treat d = 0.85 as a transferable constant.** Heterogeneity was highly significant; moderators condition the mean.
7. **Do not say "never put text on your slides."** Spoken + written beat spoken-only for **low-prior-knowledge audiences, system-paced material (which every live talk is), and picture-free material**; **key terms extracted from the narration** beat verbatim text and accounted for much of the advantage; and **adding audio to text was null** (g = −0.04). The genuinely harmful case is narrow: verbatim full-sentence script, read aloud, over a diagram, to an experienced audience.
8. **Do not generalize the redundancy or captioning results to a fluent-L1 native-language audience.** The redundancy benefit concentrates in L2 learners, non-fluent L1 readers and struggling readers — the mechanism is speech-decoding repair, not load management — and the captioning effect (g = 0.99) is specifically for second-language video. The literature is overwhelmingly English-language postsecondary.
9. **Do not apply expertise reversal to signaling.** Prior knowledge was **not** a moderator of the signaling effect across 103 studies — this directly contradicts the CLT prediction. Signpost for experts too. (Expertise reversal is real elsewhere; it is not a licence to strip structure cues for a senior audience.)
10. **Do not say "no strong boundary conditions were found" for contiguity.** The abstract says integration benefited learning across **many** moderators — not all — and the paper's own purpose was to find the conditions where it works best.
11. **Do not call Ginns's element-interactivity moderator "pre-registered."** It is a *hypothesised* moderator stated a priori in the introduction. Pre-registration did not exist for 2006 education meta-analyses.
12. **Do not derive slide-count or bullet-count rules from any of this.** The synthesis's do-not-overreach list is explicit: *"'working memory holds 4 slots' / 'never more than 4 bullets'"* is a defensible claim turned into a false one. Nothing in this document supports a numeric bullet cap.
13. **Do not assert anything about Mehrabian's 7-38-55, the "8-second attention span", or learning styles.** No verified claim in this evidence base addresses them, in either direction. They must be researched and cited separately; do not fill the gap from memory.
