# Myths, and claims that did not survive verification

> **What this is.** The refuted output of two adversarial verification passes plus three dedicated myth investigations — the claims a communication coach is most tempted to repeat, and precisely how much of each one is left standing after the primary sources were opened and read.
> **Evidence base.** 17 confirmed claims and 50 refuted claims (71 verification blocks; four claims were adjudicated twice), from the adversarial verification pass. Every claim carries its vote: 3-0 = unanimous, 2-1 = split.
> **Load this when.** The coaching task involves quoting a famous number (7-38-55, "8-second attention span", "7±2", any Mayer effect size, any NN/g percentage), asserting a mechanically checkable writing rule ("paragraphs under 150 words", "BLUF", "one idea per sentence"), invoking learning styles, claiming a readability score proves clarity, or making any confident claim about what "the research shows" about being understood.

**How to read a refutation here.** A REFUTED verdict almost never means "wholly false". It nearly always means one of four things, and the distinction is the entire point of this document:

1. **The numbers are real; the inference is not.** (Most common. The quote checks out verbatim; the sentence built on it does not.)
2. **The hedge was stripped.** (The source said "we suggest", "theoretical", "non-significant", "in English" — and the popular version deleted it.)
3. **The scope was widened.** (A lab finding became a law; a preference became a pedagogy; one lecturer became "researchers".)
4. **The citation is dead or misattributed.** (The practice may still be sound; the specific provenance is not.)

Where a claim fails only on (4), the underlying practice may survive on other evidence. That is said explicitly each time.

# PART A — THE THREE FAMOUS MYTHS

## A1. Mehrabian 7-38-55 ("communication is 93% nonverbal")

### What people believe

That in any act of communication, 7% of the meaning is carried by words, 38% by tone of voice, and 55% by body language — and that this was measured.

### What the evidence actually says: the provenance chain

**Step 1 — The numbers were never the output of an experiment that compared all three channels.** They appear exactly once in the primary literature, as a speculative extrapolation in the final Discussion paragraph of Mehrabian & Ferris (1967), combining results across two separate studies. (Mehrabian & Ferris 1967, *Inference of attitudes from nonverbal communication in two channels*, J. Consulting Psychology 31(3):248-252, p.252, [CONFIRMED 2-1](https://ibralc.com.br/wp-content/uploads/sites/5/2026/01/mehrabian-1967-inference-of-attitudes-from-nonverbal-communication.pdf))

The verbatim quote, in full:

> "A final comment is required to integrate the implications of the findings of the present study with the findings of the Mehrabian and Wiener study (1967). It is suggested that the combined effect of simultaneous verbal, vocal, and facial attitude communications is a weighted sum of their independent effects—with the coefficients of .07, .38, and .55, respectively. Analytic procedures outlined by Anderson (1962, 1964) can presently be employed to test this proposed weighted-sum model for any single decoder. In view of these extrapolations of experimental findings..."

Mehrabian's own three words govern everything downstream: **"suggested"**, **"proposed"**, **"extrapolations"**, and — decisively — **"can presently be employed to test this"**. A model that still needs testing has not been measured.

**Step 2 — What the 1967 experiment actually measured.** The Mehrabian & Ferris design was 3 (Vocal Attitude) × 3 (Facial Attitude) × 2 (Speaker) × 2 (Model) = 36 conditions. Verbal content was held constant at a single pre-tested neutral word, "maybe" (rated .28 on a −4/+4 attitude scale, SD .72). There is **no verbal term in the regression at all.** The measured result (p.251) is:

> A_T = 1.50·A_F + 1.03·A_V   (95% CIs: facial 1.32–1.68; vocal .79–1.28)

and, in variance terms, "The Facial Attitude factor accounted for 41.4% of the total sum of squares, whereas the Vocal Attitude factor accounted for 19.3% of the total sum of squares." The paper's own abstract describes the facial component as receiving "approximately 3/2 the weight received by the vocal component."

**Be scrupulous about what this does and does not license.** Two seductive-sounding debunks of this study were themselves **REFUTED 0-3**, and a coach must not repeat them:

- **"The 7% figure is an artifact of a design that gave words no chance to matter."** Rhetorically satisfying, and false. The .07 coefficient is not derived from Mehrabian & Ferris's data at all — that study has no verbal factor and yields no verbal coefficient. The .07 is imported from the companion study, Mehrabian & Wiener (1967), which *did* vary verbal content. ([REFUTED 0-3](https://ibralc.com.br/wp-content/uploads/sites/5/2026/01/mehrabian-1967-inference-of-attitudes-from-nonverbal-communication.pdf))
- **"Converting 1.50/1.03 to 55/38 is a rescaling the paper never performed."** Also false, and arithmetically self-defeating: 1.50/1.03 = 1.456 and 55/38 = 1.447 are the same ratio, and the .55/.38/.07 trio is printed on p.252 of that very paper. The renormalization is Mehrabian's own. ([REFUTED 0-3](https://ibralc.com.br/wp-content/uploads/sites/5/2026/01/mehrabian-1967-inference-of-attitudes-from-nonverbal-communication.pdf))

This is why the headline claim carried a **2-1** vote rather than 3-0: the dissenting verifier's objection is that "never measured in any experiment" is *imprecise* — the .55/.38 pair is a renormalization of genuinely measured coefficients. **The honest statement is therefore narrower and stronger: no single study ever compared verbal, vocal and facial channels simultaneously; the three-way weighted-sum model combining them was proposed, flagged as untested by its own author, and presented as an extrapolation.**

**Step 3 — The sample.** 62 subjects total, all female University of California undergraduates fulfilling an introductory-psychology course requirement: 25 to pre-select the neutral word, 17 to rate single-channel stimuli, and just 20 to judge the combined facial-vocal conditions that produced the .55/.38 weights. The "body language" channel was not body language: it was 3½ × 4½ inch black-and-white **head-only still photographs** of three female models against a neutral background. No gestures, no posture, no motion, no male subjects, no interaction. (Mehrabian & Ferris 1967, p.249-250, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://ibralc.com.br/wp-content/uploads/sites/5/2026/01/mehrabian-1967-inference-of-attitudes-from-nonverbal-communication.pdf))

**Step 4 — The "38% tone" half came from a study of deliberately inconsistent single words.** Mehrabian & Wiener (1967) tested isolated English words (HONEY, DEAR, MAYBE, DON'T, BRUTE, TERRIBLE) spoken by two female speakers in positive/neutral/negative tone; the main experiment had N=30, only 10 subjects per instruction condition. The paper restricts its own generalizability in the Discussion (p.113):

> "These findings regarding the relative contribution of the tonal component of a verbal message can be safely extended only to communication situations in which no additional information about the communicator-addressee relationship is available (as was the case in the above experiment)."

The authors supply their own counterexample: a boss can fire an assistant in a pleasant tone of voice — "the tone of voice indicates the boss' pleasure about his communication, rather than a positive attitude towards the assistant." Once real-world context or committed action is present, tone stops dominating. (Mehrabian & Wiener 1967, *Decoding of inconsistent communications*, JPSP 6(1):109-114, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://ibralc.com.br/wp-content/uploads/sites/5/2026/01/mehrabian1967-decoding-of-inconsistent-communications.pdf))

**Step 5 — Mehrabian's own standing caveat, published alongside the formula on his own site.** This is the single most important line in this section, and it must travel with the numbers every time they are mentioned:

> "Total Liking = 7% Verbal Liking + 38% Vocal Liking + 55% Facial Liking
>
> **Please note that this and other equations regarding relative importance of verbal and nonverbal messages were derived from experiments dealing with communications of feelings and attitudes (i.e., like-dislike). Unless a communicator is talking about their feelings or attitudes, these equations are not applicable.**"

(Albert Mehrabian, *"Silent Messages" — Description and Ordering Information*, kaaj.com, Copyright 1995-2016, [CONFIRMED 3-0](http://www.kaaj.com/psych/smorder.html))

**Step 6 — Mehrabian in correspondence, quoted in a peer-reviewed critique.** He calls the general reading absurd:

> "My findings are often misquoted. Please remember that all my findings on inconsistent or redundant communications dealt with communications of *feelings and attitudes*. This is the realm within which they are applicable. Clearly, it is absurd to imply or suggest that the verbal portion of *all communication* constitutes only 7% of the message. Suppose I want to tell you that the eraser you are looking for is in the second right-hand drawer of my desk in my third floor office. How could anyone contend that the verbal part of this message is only 7% of the message? Instead, and more accurately, the verbal part is nearly 100% of the message."

Lapakko's peer-reviewed critique additionally establishes that the formula was pieced together from two studies with no study comparing all three channels, that the verbal component "was never given a fair test" (Burgoon, Buller & Woodall), and that the method "lacks any sort of external validity." (Lapakko 1997, *Three cheers for language*, Communication Education 46:63-67, p.65, quoting A. Mehrabian, personal communication, 1995, [CONFIRMED 3-0](http://www.communicationcache.com/uploads/1/0/8/8/10887248/three_cheers_for_language_-_a_closer_examination_of_a_widely_cited_study_of_nonverbal_communication.pdf))

### The two scope conditions, stated exactly

The equation applies **only** where both hold:
1. The message is about **feelings and attitudes** (like/dislike) — Mehrabian's own restriction.
2. The channels are **inconsistent** with each other — this is what both source studies actually manipulated ("Decoding of *inconsistent* communications").

Outside those two conditions, per the author himself, the equations **are not applicable**.

### The 2025 replication attempt — a gap this dossier cannot fill

The dossier records, as a counter-source to the headline claim, Sato, Shimokawa & Minato (2025), *Exploration of Mehrabian's communication model with an android*, Scientific Reports 15:25986 ([PMC12271524](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12271524/)), characterised as the first single experiment [to test all three channels together]. **The dossier's entry for this source is truncated and does not preserve the coefficients it reported.** This document therefore does not state them. Do not fill this gap from memory; re-verify against the paper before the skill asserts any replication figure.

### Language caveat — do not generalize

The entire stimulus set is **English**. The carrier word "maybe" and the 15 candidate words were rated by American (UCLA) English-speaking undergraduates; the tone manipulation is English pitch/stress variation — in Mehrabian & Wiener's own words, "those permissible variations of pitch and stress which are linguistically unnecessary." **In a tone language (Mandarin, Thai, Vietnamese), pitch variation is lexically contrastive rather than "linguistically unnecessary", so the content/tone separation the experiment depends on does not exist in the same form.** These findings must not be generalized cross-linguistically.

### Operational rule for the skill

- Never cite 7-38-55 as a fact about communication in general. If a user cites it, correct it with the provenance chain above.
- Do **not** replace it with a different number. The evidence in this dossier supports no channel-weighting constant of any kind.
- Do not tell a user their words are only 7% of the message. Mehrabian's own eraser example ("the verbal part is nearly 100% of the message") is the counterexample, in his own words.
- What *is* defensible: when a message is about feelings/attitudes **and** the channels contradict each other, receivers weight the nonverbal channels heavily. That is a narrow, conditional finding, not a law of communication.

## A2. The "8-second attention span" (shorter than a goldfish)

### What people believe

That the average human attention span fell from 12 seconds in 2000 to 8 seconds in 2013, which is less than a goldfish's 9 seconds — and that Microsoft measured it.

### What the evidence actually says: the citation chain, traced to its end

**Link 1 — Microsoft Canada (2015). The report did not measure it.** The 2015 Microsoft Canada *Attention spans* consumer-insights report presents 12s (2000) / 8s (2013) / 9s (goldfish) on a single infographic page whose **only** attribution line is "Source: Statistic brain". The page's heading is "We know human attention is dwindling". The numbers are imported wholesale from a third-party aggregator. (Microsoft Canada, *Attention spans — Consumer Insights*, Spring 2015, p.6, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://static1.squarespace.com/static/59bf2bf68fd4d28e59627113/t/5ab01bdff950b70546a08167/1521490918597/265348695-Microsoft-Attention-Spans-Research-Report.pdf))

The report's own methodology is (1) a gamified online survey of 2,000 Canadian respondents (Q4 2014) and (2) an EEG "neuro deep-dive" with 112 Canadian respondents. **Neither instrument yields a seconds-valued attention span.** Every original finding in the report is expressed as "% high sustained attention" terciles or as index scores. The figure "8 seconds" never recurs anywhere in the report's own findings; it sits on a scene-setting slide *before* the methodology section.

> **A refuted defence of the report, for honesty.** A claim that the Microsoft report *"explicitly repudiates the scalar attention-span-in-seconds framing"* was **REFUTED 0-3**. It is true that the report adopts Sohlberg & Mateer's three-part model and writes, verbatim: "This study breaks attention into three parts because we don't think that attention can be simply characterized as how long people can concentrate — different tasks, devices, and lifestyles require different sets of attention types." It is also true the foreword says "Think digital is killing attention spans? Think again." **But the report never retracts, hedges or criticises the 8-second slide.** A grep of the full 52-page deck finds the stat exactly once, uncaveated. The report is the vector that laundered an unsourced aggregator figure into a Microsoft-branded document — it is the meme's origin, not its corrective. Do not defend it.

**Link 2 — Statistic Brain. The trail runs into a void.** The archived page offers no study, no author, no methodology, no sample — only a "Statistic Verification" line naming two institutions and a wire service:

> "Statistic Verification
> Source: National Center for Biotechnology Information, U.S. National Library of Medicine, The Associated Press
> Research Date: April 2nd, 2015
> […]
> The average attention span in 2015 — 8.25 seconds
> The average attention span in 2000 — 12 seconds
> The average attention span of a gold fish — 9 seconds"

**And the number is not even stable across archived snapshots.** Verified byte-for-byte across three Wayback captures:

| Snapshot | Value | Year label | Source line |
|---|---|---|---|
| 3 Jan 2014 | 8 seconds | "in 2013" | NCBI, U.S. NLM, The Associated Press |
| 3 Jan 2015 | 8 seconds | "in 2013" | *identical* |
| 4 May 2015 | **8.25 seconds** | **"in 2015"** | *character-identical to the 2014 line* |

The value changed and the year label changed while the citation stayed frozen. **A real measurement does not change its value and its year while its citation stays unchanged.** (Statistic Brain, Wayback snapshot 4 May 2015, [CONFIRMED 3-0](http://web.archive.org/web/20150504135207/http://www.statisticbrain.com/attention-span-statistics/) — source quality: **unreliable**)

**Link 3 — BBC *More or Less* chased it to the end and found nothing.**

> "And when I contact the listed sources - the National Center for Biotechnology Information at the US National Library of Medicine, and the Associated Press - neither can find any record of research that backs up the stats. My attempts to contact Statistic Brain came to nothing too. I have spoken to various people who dedicate their working lives to studying human attention and they have no idea where the numbers come from either."

(Simon Maybin, *Busting the attention span myth*, BBC World Service / More or Less, 10 March 2017, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://www.bbc.com/news/health-38896790))

**The chain terminates in a void. There is no study to cite.**

**Link 4 — The likely real origin is a dwell-time measurement, which is not attention.** The only real study anywhere in the chain is Weinreich, Obendorf, Herder & Mayer (2008), and it measured web-page **stay time**, not attention. Verbatim:

> "the median stay time was 8.0s (as opposed to 12.4s) […] However, we could not identify if users actively used a Web page, which implies that the attention times per page were definitely shorter than the stay times we recorded."

The 8.0s is the median for **Google search-result pages**; 12.4s is the median for first-time (non-revisit) page visits — plausibly the "12 seconds". The authors explicitly warn their stay times are an **upper bound** on attention, not a measure of it. On the archived Statistic Brain page this citation sits under a *separate* "Internet Browsing Statistics" block and is never attached to the attention-span rows at all. (Weinreich et al. 2008, *Not Quite the Average: An Empirical Study of Web Use*, ACM TWEB 2(1) Art.5, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://www.eelcoherder.com/images/publications/2008/not_quite_the_average.pdf))

**Language caveat:** Weinreich et al. is a client-side log study of **25 participants** (German university-affiliated web users, 2004-05). Page dwell times depend on reading speed, script, and page language, and cannot be generalised across languages — still less into a species-wide "attention span".

### The defensible replacement construct: the vigilance decrement

What attention science actually measures is the **vigilance decrement** — a gradual decline in the probability of detecting rare critical signals with time-on-task, operating over **tens of minutes to hours, not seconds**.

> "the vigilance decrement, which is defined as a decreased probability of detecting rare critical events in streams of stimuli with increased time-on-task (Mackworth, 1948). […] The defining feature across these examples is that the vigilance decrement is a consequence of performing attention-demanding tasks over time spans ranging from **tens of minutes to hours** (e.g., See et al., 1995)."

| Quantity | Value | Source (as reported by Haubert et al. 2018) |
|---|---|---|
| Detection-accuracy decline | ~10–15% after about 30 min, continuing to decline gradually thereafter | Mackworth 1948 |
| Typical onset window | within the first 20–35 min; **half the decrement in the first 15 min** | Teichner 1974 |
| Demand sensitivity | onset "may come sooner in more challenging tasks" | See et al. 1995 |

(Haubert et al. 2018, *Relationship of Event-Related Potentials to the Vigilance Decrement*, Frontiers in Psychology 9:237, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2018.00237/full))

**Sustained attention is a demand-sensitive performance curve over minutes-to-hours, not a fixed scalar duration in seconds.** Note the hedges travel: "~10-15%", "roughly 30 min", "may come sooner".

### The stronger position: the premise is wrong, not just the number

Attention researchers reject the premise, not merely the value:

> "'I don't think that's true at all,' says Dr Gemma Briggs, a psychology lecturer at the Open University. 'Simply because I don't think that that's something that psychologists or people interested in attention would try and measure and quantify in that way.' […] she says the idea of an 'average attention span' is pretty meaningless. 'It's very much task-dependent. How much attention we apply to a task will vary depending on what the task demand is.'"

(BBC / Maybin 2017, [CONFIRMED 2-1](https://www.bbc.com/news/health-38896790))

**Carry the split vote honestly.** This block passed 2-1, not unanimously. The dissenting verifier's objection is that the claim asserts a *field-wide ontological position* ("attention researchers reject the premise"; "it is not the kind of thing that has a value") while the article supports only that **one named psychology lecturer** says this — and pointed to work that does construct an objective attention-span metric (Kim, Esterman et al., *Quantifying attention span across the lifespan*, [PMC10621754](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10621754/), N=262, ages 7–85). **The defensible form is therefore:** there is no evidence for a declining "average attention span", the 8-second figure has no study behind it, and prominent attention researchers reject the scalar framing as task-dependent and meaningless — *not* "no researcher anywhere has ever quantified attention span".

### Operational rule for the skill

- Never cite the 8-second figure, the goldfish comparison, or any "attention span is falling" trend as fact.
- Do not replace it with a different number of seconds. Do not say "you have 8 seconds to hook them" or "attention spans are now N seconds".
- If a user wants a time-based design constraint, the defensible construct is the **vigilance decrement**: attention to a demanding task degrades over **tens of minutes**, faster under higher demand. Design for a decline over the length of a meeting, not over the length of a sentence.
- The strongest correct framing: attention is **task-dependent**. "How much attention will this audience apply?" has an answer; "what is the average human attention span?" does not.

## A3. Learning styles and the meshing hypothesis

### Be scrupulous about scope before anything else

**Pashler et al. do NOT claim that modality preferences are fictitious, nor that people are cognitively identical.** They affirm both that people reliably report presentation preferences and that people genuinely differ in aptitudes. What collapses is the inferential leap from preference to pedagogy — **the meshing hypothesis**: that instruction delivered in the format matching a learner's stated preference improves learning.

> "Our review of the literature disclosed **ample evidence** that children and adults will, if asked, express preferences about how they prefer information to be presented to them. There is also **plentiful evidence** arguing that people differ in the degree to which they have some fairly specific aptitudes for different kinds of thinking and for processing different types of information. **However**, we found virtually no evidence for the interaction pattern mentioned above, which was judged to be a precondition for validating the educational applications of learning styles."

Elsewhere: "the existence of preferences with some coherence and stability is not in dispute."

**Saying "learning styles don't exist" overstates the source.** The correct target is the *meshing* claim. (Pashler, McDaniel, Rohrer & Bjork 2008, *Learning Styles: Concepts and Evidence*, Psychological Science in the Public Interest 9(3):105-119, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1111/j.1539-6053.2009.01038.x))

### The exact design they require

> "First, students must be divided into groups on the basis of their learning styles, and then students from each group must be randomly assigned to receive one of multiple instructional methods. Next, students must then sit for a final test that is the same for all students. Finally, in order to demonstrate that optimal learning requires that students receive instruction tailored to their putative learning style, the experiment must reveal a specific type of interaction between learning style and instructional method: Students with one learning style achieve the best educational outcome when given an instructional method that differs from the instructional method producing the best outcome for students with a different learning style."

In the body they are stricter still: the hypothesis is supported **"if and only if"** an experiment reveals a **"crossover interaction between learning style and method when learning style is plotted on the horizontal axis."**

**A merely statistically significant interaction is NOT sufficient.** If the same method is optimal for every group, the result does not count, no matter how significant. (Pashler et al. 2008, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1111/j.1539-6053.2009.01038.x))

### Their verdict: essentially zero studies meet that bar

> "Remarkably, despite the vast size of the literature on learning styles and classroom instruction, we found only one study that could be described as even potentially meeting the criteria described earlier, and as we report in the following text, even that study provided less than compelling evidence."

They describe the literature as **"enormous"**. The one candidate (Sternberg, Grigorenko, Ferrari & Clinkenbeard, 1999) they reject: the reported interaction "was found only with highly derived measures"; "only about one third of the subjects were classified into the groups that produced the interaction" (112 of 324 students); and "the interaction was achieved only after the outliers were excluded for unspecified reasons." They call it "only tenuous evidence" with "serious methodological issues."

Their SUMMARY section states flatly: **"we have been unable to find any evidence that clearly meets this standard."**

Worse than absence of evidence: several methodologically sound studies (Massa & Mayer 2006; Constantinidou & Baker 2002) produced results that **contradict** the meshing hypothesis. (Pashler et al. 2008, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1111/j.1539-6053.2009.01038.x))

Their conclusion, in their own words: "there is no adequate evidence base to justify incorporating learning-styles assessments into general educational practice", and such use is **"unwise and a wasteful use of limited resources."**

### Both testable predictions fail — Willingham, Hughes & Dobolyi (2015)

The theory makes two testable predictions, and **both** fail — not just the pedagogical one.

**Prediction 1 (a learning style is a stable attribute of a person, constant across tasks and settings) fails at the measurement layer:** "Although there are a multitude of inventories and models for assessing learning styles, **most are not reliable**" (citing Coffield, Moseley, Hall & Ecclestone 2004). The construct cannot even be measured consistently. They add: "A recent survey of 92 learning styles researchers showed that problems of reliability were among their chief concerns."

**Prediction 2 (performance is better when instruction matches the style) fails empirically**, and they stress the practical bar is higher still: "Practical classroom implications require a particular pattern of data that not only supports the theory but also shows that instruction matched to learning styles optimizes achievement for each group. In other words, **the two lines in the graph would have to cross**."

They surveyed 313 US adults (Mechanical Turk) and found mean agreement of **6.35/7 (SD 1.11)** with the learning-styles statement — near-ceiling public belief in a theory that eight reviews spanning 1978–2009 all reject:

> "Several reviews that span decades have evaluated the literature on learning styles (e.g., Arter & Jenkins, 1979; Kampwirth & Bates, 1980; Kavale & Forness, 1987; Kavale, Hirshoren, & Forness, 1998; Pashler et al., 2009; Snider, 1992; Stahl, 1999; Tarver & Dawson, 1978), and each has drawn the conclusion that there is no viable evidence to support the theory."

(Willingham, Hughes & Dobolyi 2015, *The Scientific Status of Learning Styles Theories*, Teaching of Psychology 42(3):266-271, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://career.ucsf.edu/sites/g/files/tkssra15591/files/Article%20UCSF%20SEJC%20January%202017.pdf))

### The direct experimental test, in the population the practice actually targets

Rogowsky, Calhoun & Tallal (2020) replicated the null in K-12 children: the entire 5th-grade cohort of a Pennsylvania public middle school (n=125, ages 10-11), the age-matched Dunn & Dunn inventory (LSCY), counterbalanced crossover of reading vs listening instruction.

> "Contrary to the expectations predicted by the learning styles hypothesis, we found (1) no significant positive relationship between auditory learning style and listening comprehension, (2) no significant positive relationship between visual learning style and reading comprehension, and (3) no differential effect of learning style on performance on a listening as compared to a reading comprehension test. Overall, matching instruction to meet a student's auditory or visual learning style had no effect on student achievement."

| Finding | Value |
|---|---|
| Instruction × learning-style interaction | F(1, 38) = 1.16, p = 0.29 (no interaction) |
| Students with **no clear style preference at all** | **68%** (73 of 107) — "Most students, 68%, do not even have a clear learning style preference" |
| Crossover | none — Figure 2C caption: "there is no crossover effect" |
| Direction of group means | **wrong way for meshing**: both auditory-preferring and visual-preferring students scored higher on **listening** (listening M=36.0, SE 0.9 > reading M=34.4, SE 0.8) |
| Main effect (visual > auditory learners, on **both** measures) | F(1, 32) = 12.92, p = 0.001, η² = 0.29; visual M=39.07 (SE 1.73) vs auditory M=28.58 (SE 2.35) |

The 68% figure is the practical killer: **the classification the whole method depends on is undefined for two-thirds of a class.** (Rogowsky, Calhoun & Tallal 2020, Frontiers in Psychology 11:164, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7033468/))

> **A refuted framing, for honesty.** A claim describing the same team's **2015 adult study** as "the FIRST direct experimental test built to Pashler's specification... 121 college-educated adults... randomly assigned to receive the same nonfiction content either as a digital audiobook or as e-text" was **REFUTED 0-3** — not on its null result, which is verbatim-verified ("Results demonstrated no statistically significant relationship between learning style preference (auditory, visual word) and instructional method (audiobook, e-text) for either immediate or delayed comprehension tests"), but on its **sample framing**: the 121 participants were randomized across **four** groups, with only ~61 in the audiobook/e-text comparison the claim describes. The systematic review by Aslaksen & Lorås (2018), which screened 1,215 records across six databases and found this was the only study "totally aligned with Pashler's criteria", is itself verbatim-verified. **Lean on the 2020 K-12 study (CONFIRMED 3-0) for the null; cite the 2015 study only with its correct sample description.** (Rogowsky, Calhoun & Tallal 2015, J. Educ. Psychol. 107(1):64-78, [REFUTED 0-3](https://core.ac.uk/download/pdf/144844129.pdf))

### The belief is near-universal — and correctable

| Finding | Value |
|---|---|
| Educators endorsing matching instruction to learning styles | **89.1%** weighted (range 58–97.6%), 37 samples, 15,405 educators, 18 countries, 2009–early 2020 |
| Trend over time | **none** — no evidence of decline |
| Trainee (pre-service) teachers — the newest cohort | **95.4%** (the highest group) |
| Say they use or plan to use matching | 79.7% |
| **After being explicitly taught about the missing evidence** (4 studies) | belief fell **78.4% → 37.1%**, Cohen's d = 3.6, p = 0.012 |

**The authors' own honesty caveat, which must travel with the number:** they warn the 89.1% may be **inflated** because survey items did not clearly separate "matching instruction improves learning" (false) from "learners have preferences" (true). Treat 89.1% as an **upper bound**, not a precise figure. (Newton & Salvi 2020, *How Common Is Belief in the Learning Styles Neuromyth, and Does It Matter?*, Frontiers in Education 5:602451, [CONFIRMED 3-0](https://cronfa.swansea.ac.uk/Record/cronfa55948/Download/55948__19073__020e8797d9b54dfda1831503535621d2.pdf))

**Language note:** the 18-country spread makes the belief finding genuinely cross-national rather than an anglophone artifact. However, the core survey item derives from Howard-Jones et al. (2009), written in English and then translated — a wording-sensitivity problem that translation can only amplify.

### Language caveats on the null results — do not overstate

Both Rogowsky studies carry an **English-only artifact risk**. "Visual" means reading English text; "auditory" means listening to English audio; participants were native English speakers on English-normed instruments. The null crossover is the load-bearing finding and is not obviously language-dependent, **but it has never been replicated in another orthography.** In the 2020 study the risk is stronger still: the secondary finding that "visual learners" outperform "auditory learners" is interpreted by the authors themselves as reflecting **reading proficiency** ("an individual's preference for auditory learning may reflect difficulty in learning to read or failure to become proficient"). English has an unusually deep orthography, so the reading-acquisition difficulty plausibly driving this correlation may not transfer.

### Operational rule for the skill

- Never adapt an explanation to a person's self-reported "learning style" on the theory that it will improve their comprehension. That is the meshing hypothesis, and it has no evidence base.
- **Do not say "learning styles don't exist."** Say: people do report stable presentation *preferences*, and people do differ in *aptitudes* — but matching instruction to the stated preference does not improve learning, and the crossover interaction required to demonstrate it has essentially never been found.
- If a user asks for their material to be "made visual because I'm a visual learner", it is fine to add a diagram (contiguity and multimedia effects have their own, separate evidence — see the Mayer section below for what survives). It is **not** fine to justify it by their style, or to withhold text from them because of it.
- Expect belief to be near-universal and do not treat it as a sign of bad faith. It is also correctable: explicitly showing people the missing evidence roughly halved endorsement (78.4% → 37.1%).

# PART B — CLAIMS FROM THE LITERATURE THAT DID NOT SURVIVE

Every claim below is an **overreach on a real finding**, not a fabrication. Each entry states the popular claim, then what survives, then what does not.

## B1. The curse of knowledge — four over-tellings

The headline finding is solid and lives elsewhere in this knowledge base: tappers in Newton (1990) predicted ~50% listener success and got 2.5% (3 hits in 120 tries); and Camerer, Loewenstein & Weber (1989) found "Better-informed agents are unable to ignore private information even when it is in their interest to do so", with incentives and feedback producing no reduction and markets halving but never eliminating the bias. **The four elaborations below are the ones that failed.**

### B1.1 "Mere possession of the knowledge is sufficient — knowing the answer alone destroys your model of an audience"

**What survives.** Newton's descriptive numbers are verbatim. Informed observers who never tapped were also overconfident: "Across 240 songs, subjects' guesses averaged 50%, with a range of 2 to 98." So **authorship/tapping is not necessary** for the bias.

**What does not survive.** The **sufficiency** claim is contradicted by the same dissertation's own Table 2. In the experimenter-taps round, the *listeners* were also told the tune in advance, also did no tapping, and heard the identical stimulus — the exact profile the claim says is sufficient — and they predicted only **3%**. Possessing the answer was not sufficient. (Newton 1990, [REFUTED 1-2](https://gwern.net/doc/psychology/cognitive-bias/illusion-of-depth/1990-newton.pdf))

### B1.2 "Only the knowledgeable party is wrong; uninformed people model other uninformed people accurately"

**What survives.** The arithmetic. Table 2 (experimenter-taps condition): tappers predicted 50%, listeners predicted 3%.

**What does not survive.**
- **Cross-condition splice.** The 3% and 50% come from the experimenter-taps round, in which *nobody's guesses were scored* — there is no measured accuracy for that round. The "true 2.5%" comes from a **different** round ("Tappers Tap for Listeners"). "~3% ≈ the true 2.5%" compares a prediction in one condition against a hit rate measured in another.
- **The ground truth is internally inconsistent in the source.** The abstract says listeners identified "two out of one hundred fifty tunes" (1.3%); the Results say "3 hits in 120 tries... 2.5 percent"; the Discussion says "3 out of 150 tunes" (2.0%). A claim whose force depends on the precision of "3% ≈ 2.5%" rests on a denominator the source reports three different ways.
- **"Uninformed people model other uninformed people accurately" is false** per peer-reviewed evidence: Lau, Geipel, Wu & Keysar (2022), *The extreme illusion of understanding*, J Exp Psychol Gen 151(11):2957-2962 — "Though speakers **and listeners** monitor communication success, they systematically overestimate it." Uninformed listeners overestimated their **own** understanding by 41 percentage points.

(Newton 1990, [REFUTED 0-3](https://gwern.net/doc/psychology/cognitive-bias/illusion-of-depth/1990-newton.pdf))

**Consequence for the skill:** do not tell users "the audience knows when it doesn't understand; only *you* are miscalibrated." Both sides are miscalibrated.

### B1.3 "The mechanism is vivid internal representation — the expert 'hears' the full orchestration"

**What survives.** Newton's Discussion prose does say tappers were "so caught up in the richness of the melodies they were 'hearing'". That is her **interpretation**.

**What does not survive.** It is a post-hoc rhetorical gloss, not a tested mediator. Newton **never measured vividness**, so no vividness-to-overconfidence correlation exists in the data, and "crowding out awareness" has no corresponding attentional measure anywhere in the study. Her own data cut against it: informed observers who never tapped were equally overconfident (~50%), so performer-generated phenomenology cannot be the mechanism. The field explicitly denies the mechanism is settled (Tullis & Feder 2022, Memory & Cognition), and Birch et al. (2017, *Cognition*, n=359) show **fluency misattribution alone is sufficient** to induce a curse of knowledge in the **absence** of knowledge. (Newton 1990, [REFUTED 0-3](https://gwern.net/doc/psychology/cognitive-bias/illusion-of-depth/1990-newton.pdf))

**Do not tell users:** "the expert literally hears the orchestration in their head, which is why they can't hear how thin their signal is." It is a vivid story with no experimental support at the strength claimed.

### B1.4 "Experts are literally *unable* to act inscrutably / to reason as if they did not know"

**What survives.** The quote is real: Camerer, Loewenstein & Weber write "Agents are unable to act 'inscrutably'; they cannot ignore their better information when they should."

**What does not survive.** "Literally unable" is the paper's **w = 1 hypothesis, which the paper explicitly rejects.** The model (eq.1, p.1235): "If w = 0, an agent is applying the law of iterated expectations correctly. If w = 1, agents who know I1 think that all other agents know I1 too. The parameter w thus measures the **degree** of curse of knowledge." Then p.1240: **"We can strongly reject both the no-bias (w = 0) and pure-bias (w = 1) hypotheses."** Rejecting w=1 means informed agents *do* partially discount their private information. The finding is **insufficient adjustment, not incapacity.** The abstract itself: "market forces reduce the curse by approximately 50 percent but do not eliminate it." A bias halved by context is not a hard-wired inability. (Camerer, Loewenstein & Weber 1989, JPE 97(5):1232-54, [REFUTED 0-3](https://www.cmu.edu/dietrich/sds/docs/loewenstein/CurseknowledgeEconSet.pdf))

**Also refuted (0-3): "even incentivized, experienced traders in real markets showed partial but robust knowledge projection."** The w-parameter mechanics are verbatim-correct, but the setting is **not real markets**: it is a laboratory double-oral-auction asset market with student subjects, 4-minute trading periods, shouted bids. Do not describe it as evidence from real markets. ([REFUTED 0-3](https://www.cmu.edu/dietrich/sds/docs/loewenstein/CurseknowledgeEconSet.pdf))

**The defensible statement:** informed people are **systematically and persistently miscalibrated** about how well their message lands, and cannot correct it by trying harder; incentives and feedback do not fix it; markets at best halve it. That is enough to justify external checking. Nothing stronger is supported.

## B2. Working-memory design constants ("never more than 3-4 things")

This is the single most seductive, most skill-ready number in the whole dossier — and it is exactly what failed.

### B2.1 "For communication design, the usable working-memory budget is ~3-4 units"

**What survives.** The **historical** claim is verbatim-confirmed. Cowan (2015) quotes Miller: "the running memory span was only about 4" and the 7 on intelligence tests "must have been a hooking together of a rehearsed initial segment with a final segment"; and Simon: "George had the right idea, but the wrong number. I think Herb favored 3." Cowan (1988): "Estimates of short-term storage capacity may be inflated by contributions of the long-term store... The resulting estimate for adults is two or three items." **So: 7±2 is inflated; pure capacity is ~3-4.**

**What does not survive.** The **design prescription** is nowhere in the source and is undercut by it. Cowan's ~3-4 is a **pure-capacity estimate observable only under conditions engineered to BLOCK the mechanisms real communication depends on** — the limit appears "when other steps are taken specifically to block the recoding of stimulus items into larger chunks... rehearsal and long-term memory cannot be used to combine stimulus items into chunks." Nothing licenses "for communication design, the budget is ~3-4 units." The 4-vs-7 question is also live: Morra, Patella & Muscella (2024, Journal of Cognition 7(1):60) report ~6–7 unit capacity estimates and argue the answer depends on what is counted. (Cowan 2015, *George Miller's Magical Number of Immediate Memory in Retrospect*, Psychological Review, [REFUTED 1-2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4486516/))

### B2.2 "A live listener has no time to rehearse, so the limit is ~3 items"

**What survives.** Both quotes are genuine: "when attention must be focused on an ensemble of items all at once, the limit seems severe, in the range of about 3 for adults"; "The resulting estimate for adults is two or three items in short-term storage."

**What does not survive.** The claim smuggles an unsupported inference inside an "i.e.". **Cowan's "ensemble of items all at once" means simultaneous presentation** — visual change-detection arrays, whole-display enumeration, subitizing — or lists where grouping and rehearsal are experimentally blocked. **Speech in conversation and presentations is the opposite input regime: a serial, temporally extended, meaningful, richly chunkable stream.** The claim asserts an equivalence the source does not make and whose boundary conditions it contradicts. (Cowan 2015, [REFUTED 0-3](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4486516/))

### The correct operational move

**Never ship a rule like "never present more than 3-4 things."** Three independent reasons:
1. The ~4 figure is a pure-capacity estimate valid **only** under boundary conditions where rehearsal and recoding are blocked — conditions a listener in a meeting is not in.
2. The slot-vs-flexible-resource mechanism is **contested** (Bays & Husain; Ma, Husain & Bays).
3. Capacity is over **knowledge-relative chunks**, so the number of presentable "things" is a function of the audience's prior knowledge, not a constant.

**Instead: reduce the CHUNK COUNT FOR THIS AUDIENCE** — pre-teach labels, group by structures the audience already holds, replace an unexplained term with a familiar one. Never enforce a magic number.

## B3. Mayer's box-score effect sizes — the numbers behind every slide-design deck

### The popular claim

Coherence supported in 23/23 tests (median d = 0.86); redundancy 16/16 (d = 0.86); spatial contiguity 22/22 (d = 1.10); temporal contiguity 9/9 (d = 1.22). Therefore: strip decorative material, never duplicate your script on the slide, put labels next to the thing they label, narrate *during* the animation.

### What survives

**The quotes are verbatim** — the box-scores really are printed in Mayer & Fiorella (2014), Cambridge Handbook of Multimedia Learning 2nd ed., ch.12. **And the directional advice is broadly supported.** The behaviour is real. What fails is the **magnitude**, the **universality**, the **superlatives**, and the **mechanism**.

### What does not survive

**(a) The tallies are vote-counts by the theory's own author, not meta-analyses.** No random-effects pooling, no heterogeneity test, no publication-bias correction, no confidence intervals. Vote-counting is a discredited synthesis method.

**(b) The numbers are not stable — across editions of the same chapter, or across the same author's own later summaries.**

| Principle | Mayer 2005 (1st ed.) | Mayer & Fiorella 2014 (2nd ed.) | Mayer 2017 (same author, J. Computer Assisted Learning) | Independent meta-analysis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Coherence | 10/11, d = 1.32 | 23/23, d = 0.86 | d = 0.70 | Rey 2012: 11/39 support, 13 mixed, 15 no support; d = 0.30 retention / 0.48 transfer |
| Signaling | 3/3, d = 0.60 | 24/28, d = 0.41 | d = 0.46 | learning g = 0.43, 95% CI [0.35, 0.50], k = 209 (Schneider et al. 2018, via Noetel et al. 2022) |
| Redundancy | 10/10, d = 0.69 | 16/16, d = 0.86 | d = 0.87 | **Cromley & Chen 2025: g = 0.14, non-significant** |
| Spatial contiguity | 8/8, d = 1.11 | 22/22, d = 1.10 | **d = 0.79** | Ginns 2006: d = 0.85 [0.68, 1.02]; Schroeder & Cenkci 2018: **g = 0.63** (58 comparisons, n=2,426); Wang et al. 2016 (53 studies): d_retention = 0.48, d_transfer = 0.39 |
| Temporal contiguity | — | 9/9, d = 1.22 | d = 1.30 | Noetel et al. 2022 pooled contiguity: g = 0.74 [0.67, 0.82], k = 46 |

Coherence's median d fell from 1.32 to 0.86 and signaling from 0.60 to 0.41 **as more tests accrued** — the signature of an author-curated tally, not a stable estimate. (Mayer & Fiorella 2014, [REFUTED 0-3 / 1-2 / 0-3](https://edtechuvic.ca/wp-content/uploads/sites/11/2022/09/principles-for-reducing-extraneous-processing-in-multimedia-learning-coherence-signaling-redundancy-spatial-contiguity-and-temporal-contiguity-principles.pdf))

**(c) "23 of 23 supported" contains negative effects.** Table 12.4's own entry (1) — Mayer et al. 1996 Exp. 1 — is listed at **d = −0.17**, a negative effect *favouring the embellished lesson*, inside a "23 of 23 supported" tally. Entries at d = 0.17 and d = 0.06 are also counted as support.

**(d) "Coherence is the most consistently replicated of the five" is contradicted by the source's own abstract.** Four of the five principles show 100% support rates in that chapter, and coherence's median (0.86) is only third-largest, behind spatial (1.10) and temporal (1.22). The superlative is the claim's invention.

**(e) The redundancy principle has boundary conditions the popular version deletes.** Three sentences after the 16/16 line, the same page reads: **"Three important boundary conditions are that the redundancy effect can be eliminated or even reversed when the learners are experienced, the on-screen text is short, or the material lacks graphics."** And the 16/16 tally is **transfer-only**: Mayer & Johnson (2008), reported in the same chapter, found the **redundant** group *outperformed* the nonredundant group on **retention** (d=0.47 Exp1, d=0.70 Exp2) with null transfer differences (d=0.04, d=0.15). "Deeper learning" is measure-dependent.

**(f) The largest meta-analysis of Mayer's own corpus collapses the redundancy number.** Cromley & Chen (2025), *A meta-analysis of Richard Mayer's multimedia learning research*, Educational Research Review — 92 Mayer-authored articles, 181 studies, 591 effects, multilevel models: "avoiding redundant captions with narration (**g = 0.14**)", listed among the principles that **"showed non-significant effects"**. They write: "meta-analytic analyses that include more studies and take into account a range of outcome variables show **smaller effects** than Mayer (2021, 2024) reported in his summaries of his own work."

### The cognitive-load mechanism does not carry the effect

**The popular claim:** split attention hurts *because* it consumes working memory searching for and matching mutually referring elements — the causal bridge from Cognitive Load Theory to layout rules.

**What survives.** The quote is real, but it sits in the **theoretical introduction** of Ginns (2006), restating Sweller's account, and is itself hedged ("potentially extraneous to learning"). Ginns' meta-analysis measured **learning outcomes**, not the mechanism.

**What does not survive.** Direct mechanism tests fail to find the predicted working-memory signature:
- Cierniak, Scheiter & Gerjets (2009): split-source learners scored worse on learning "but did **not** show worse secondary task performance than learners with integrated format" — the dual-task load probe detected no extra load.
- Schroeder & Cenkci (2020), *Journal of Educational Psychology* 112(2):254-270, 41 comparisons: **"measures of cognitive load largely do not support the extraneous cognitive load explanation."**

(Ginns 2006, [REFUTED 1-2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0959475206000806))

**The quantitative version of the same gap.** Noetel et al. (2022) meta-meta-analysis: multimedia design interventions move **learning** (g = 0.38, 95% CI [0.27, 0.49], k = 808) far more than they move **cognitive load** (g = 0.22, 95% CI [0.04, 0.40], k = 68).

> **But do not over-tell even this.** A claim framing signaling's load reduction (g = 0.11 [0.04, 0.18], k = 32; Xie et al. 2017) as "roughly a QUARTER of the same principle's learning effect (g = 0.43 [0.35, 0.50], k = 209)" was **REFUTED 0-3** — because the ratio is manufactured by mixing two different reviews. The *same* review (Schneider et al. 2018) that reports signaling learning g = 0.43 also reports signaling load reduction at **g = 0.25 [0.04, 0.45], k = 27**. The refuting number is inside the claim's own quoted passage. (Noetel et al. 2022, *Multimedia Design for Learning*, Review of Educational Research 92(3), [REFUTED 0-3](https://doi.org/10.31234/osf.io/pynzr))

### The contiguity effect: the numbers that DO hold, and the one word that broke them

**Ginns (2006):** 50 effects, 2,375 students, weighted mean **d = 0.85, 95% CI [0.68, 1.02]**, with severe and significant heterogeneity, **Q = 117.77, df = 49, p < 0.001** — "indicating one or more moderators condition this mean effect." So the pooled number is **not a single transferable effect**. The moderator is **element interactivity**: high-EI (complex, interconnected) material d = 0.78; low-EI material d = 0.28.

**Why this block was still REFUTED (1-2):** the claim described element interactivity as a **"pre-registered"** moderator. That is anachronistic and false — PROSPERO launched in February 2011, five years after publication, and Ginns' own abstract calls it "a **hypothesised** moderator." The moderator *was* stated a priori in the paper's Introduction; it was not *pre-registered*. **The numbers hold; the word does not.** (Ginns 2006, [REFUTED 1-2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0959475206000806))

### What the skill may and may not say about slides and diagrams

**May say (directionally supported):** put the label on the diagram rather than in a legend; speak alongside the visual rather than before/after it; cut decorative material that carries no information; do not read a duplicate of your script off the slide.

**May NOT say:**
- Any of the box-score effect sizes as if they were established magnitudes.
- "Coherence is the most replicated principle."
- "Closer is always better." **This is falsified.** Beege et al. (2019) document a **spatial-contiguity failure** in which *maximum* proximity underperformed *medium* proximity — cramming related and unrelated elements together causes interference.
- "This works because it reduces extraneous cognitive load." The behavioural effect survives; the load story does not.
- Anything unconditional about redundancy: the effect "can be eliminated or even reversed when the learners are experienced, the on-screen text is short, or the material lacks graphics."

**And note the boundary that matters most for a coach:** for **low-element-interactivity** material, Ginns' own d = 0.28 was **not significant (p > .05)**, and he writes that with low intrinsic load "lack of integration of related elements may be **inconsequential**." Contiguity discipline pays off exactly where content is hard and interdependent. (Treat the low-EI null as a *non-detected* effect, not a demonstrated null — n ≈ 10/condition in the primary studies.)

## B4. Federal Plain Language Guidelines — the structural rules an automated checker most wants

These are the rules an LLM-coachable skill most wants to enforce. **All four failed**, and it matters *why* — three of the four failed on citation and inference, not on falsity.

### B4.1 "The guidelines mandate BLUF / answer-first ordering — a legally-backed federal standard"

**What survives.** The FPLG really does recommend answer-first ordering. Verbatim, Section II "Organize", printed p.5:

> "Organization is key. Start by stating the document's purpose and its bottom line. Eliminate filler and unnecessary content. Put the most important information at the beginning and include background information (when necessary) toward the end."

**What does not survive — "mandate", "normative", "legally-backed standard":**
- **The statute expressly disclaims legal force.** Plain Writing Act of 2010, Pub. L. 111-274, §6: "(a) JUDICIAL REVIEW.—There shall be no judicial review of compliance or noncompliance with any provision of this Act. (b) ENFORCEABILITY.—No provision of this Act shall be construed to create any right or benefit, substantive or procedural, enforceable by any administrative or judicial action."
- **The source self-describes as non-binding advice.** PLAIN is "a community of federal employees", and the document says "**We hope** you find this document useful." It uses "we hope", never "must"/"shall", for the ordering rule.
- **The same document prescribes competing orderings**, so "normative ordering rule" is contradicted internally.

(FPLG, [REFUTED 0-3](https://digital.gov/guides/plain-language/principles))

### B4.2 "Section II is a document-level rule, not a sentence-level one"

Even the careful *scoping* of the BLUF claim failed. A claim that main-message-first in FPLG is "a document-organization instruction, not a sentence-level one" and that "the whole of Section II is four sentences long" was **REFUTED 1-2**:
- **Section II is not four sentences.** It spans printed pp.5-16 with four subsections. The four sentences are the section's **divider page**.
- **The sentence-level scope restriction is contradicted by the same document.** Printed p.56, §III.b.4 is literally titled **"Place the main idea before exceptions and conditions"** and reads: "Material is much easier to follow if you start with the main idea and then cover exceptions and conditions."

(FPLG, [REFUTED 1-2](https://wid.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/FederalPLGuidelines.pdf))

### B4.3 "150-word paragraphs, 3-8 sentences, 250-word ceiling, one idea per sentence — directly enforceable by an automated checker"

**What survives — and note the hedge, which must never be stripped.** The quotes are genuine and verbatim in the March 2011 / Rev. 1 May 2011 PDF:

> p.50: "Express only one idea in each sentence."
> p.66: "**Writing experts recommend** paragraphs of no more than 150 words in three to eight sentences. Paragraphs should never be longer than 250 words."
> p.68: "Limit each paragraph or section to one topic... Each paragraph should start with a topic sentence that captures the essence of everything in the paragraph."

**"Writing experts recommend" is part of the rule.** Quoting the 150-word limit without it is a misquote.

**What does not survive:**
- **"Directly enforceable by an automated checker" is false for half the items.** Word and sentence counts are mechanically checkable. **"Express only one idea in each sentence" and "Limit each paragraph to one topic" are not** — the guidelines supply no operational definition of "idea" or "topic", and judging whether a topic sentence "captures the essence" is an irreducibly semantic judgment. A checker can proxy via sentence length or clause count, but that is a *different rule*.
- **The same page contradicts a hard paragraph rule**: "**There is nothing wrong with an occasional one-sentence paragraph**" and "Vary the lengths of your paragraphs." Carry both, or carry neither.
- The cited URL is dead: plainlanguage.gov/guidelines/ 301-redirects to a digital.gov landing page that does **not** contain the quoted text. The live location for this text is digital.gov/guides/plain-language/writing/clear-short.

(FPLG, [REFUTED 0-3](https://digital.gov/guides/plain-language/principles))

### B4.4 "Paraphrase testing with 6-9 participants; the VBA case proves self-reported clarity is invalid"

**What survives — the anecdote and the method, both verbatim.** From the official GSA archive of the paraphrase-testing page: "Conduct between 6 to 9 interviews"; "ask the participant to tell you in his or her own words what that section means"; "**Avoid yes or no questions.** You won't get much usable information from that type of question." And the VBA case:

> "When each reader was asked a general question about understanding the letter, they all said that it was clear. Yet several would have done something other than what VBA wanted because they had a different definition of 'service-connected.' To solve this problem, VBA explained the phrase so that everyone was working from the same definition."

Three readers held three mutually incompatible definitions of "service-connected".

**What does not survive.** The generalization "**Self-reported clarity and yes/no comprehension questions are therefore INVALID proxies for actual understanding**". That absolute is not supported by the quote and is contradicted: metacomprehension accuracy is **weak-but-positive**, not zero — median gamma ≈ .27 (Prinz, Golke & Wittwer 2020, meta-analysis), roughly +.20 to +.30 (Yang, Zhao, Yuan, Luo & Shanks 2023, Review of Educational Research). **"Weak and unreliable" is defensible. "Invalid" is not.** (FPLG paraphrase testing, [REFUTED 0-3](https://digital.gov/guides/plain-language/principles))

### B4.5 The BLUF *negative* result also fails — so BLUF is unsettled, not refuted

It would be convenient to conclude "BLUF is a myth too." **It is not established either way, and the study most often used to kill it does not do the job.**

**The claim:** Kieras (1980) tested exactly the BLUF manipulation and found no benefit — transportation passage 53.9s (conclusion first) vs 55.4s (no conclusion), n.s.; Jutland 90.1s vs 88.6s, "the difference is in the wrong direction, but fails to approach significance (t(30)=.108)".

**What survives.** Those numbers are verbatim. Readers also did not rank the up-front conclusion sentence as the most important sentence.

**What does not survive — decisively: it is not the BLUF manipulation.** Every "bad" version **deletes** the conclusion rather than **moving** it. The claim's own falsifiable prediction was about "putting the conclusion first, **holding content constant**" — which Kieras never did. And the other passages cut the other way: the Watergate passage gave good 47.1s vs bad 57.2s (t(30)=1.376, one-tail p<.1), and a cluster analysis of the Jutland passage found that **only good-version readers produced "the British won"** — i.e. the conclusion-first version was the only one that transmitted the conclusion. (Kieras 1980, Technical Report No. 5, DTIC ADA089910, [REFUTED 0-3](https://apps.dtic.mil/sti/tr/pdf/ADA089910.pdf))

**Language caveat, carried from the source:** English technical prose read by English-speaking US undergraduates. The "initial mention = theme" convention Kieras tests is a convention of **English expository prose**. It must **not** be assumed for rhetorical traditions where the point conventionally lands last (e.g. Japanese *ki-shō-ten-ketsu*, Chinese *qǐ-chéng-zhuǎn-hé*) or for languages with different topic-marking conventions.

### B4.6 "A late frame makes the reader feel they understood without improving retention"

**The claim.** In Bransford & Johnson's Experiment IV ("making and flying a kite", 31 Ss), giving the topic **after** the passage raised subjective comprehension ratings to a level statistically indistinguishable from giving it before (Topic After 3.82 vs Topic Before 4.00; No Topic 2.44) — yet recall was completely unaffected (Topic After 3.18 vs No Topic 3.22, of max 14) and significantly below Topic Before (5.54). Therefore self-reported clarity is not a valid proxy for the benefit of ordering.

**What survives.** The quote and Table 3 are verbatim:

> "Experiment IV. In the analysis of the comprehension ratings, the Topic Before scores were higher than the No Topic scores, d(3, 28) = 2.01, p < .05. However, there was no significant difference between ratings of the Topic Before and Topic After Ss, p > .05. Recall of the Topic Before Ss was superior to that of both No Topic and of Topic After Ss, d(3, 28) = 2.49 and 2.68, p < .05, respectively."

(N = 31: No Topic 9, Topic After 11, Topic Before 11.)

**What does not survive.**
- **The load-bearing half was never tested.** The claim's engine is that a late topic **"raised"** subjective comprehension ratings. **Experiment IV reports no Topic After vs No Topic contrast at all** — only Topic Before vs No Topic, and Topic Before vs Topic After. The claim converts an untested descriptive gap (3.82 vs 2.44, n = 11 and 9) into an established effect.
- **A null is treated as equivalence.** "No significant difference between ratings of Topic Before and Topic After (p > .05)" is not a demonstration that they are the same.
- **The same paper's other experiments cut against it.** Experiment II: Topic After comprehension 2.12 vs No Topic 2.29 — **no rise at all** from a late topic. Experiment III: Topic Before > Topic After, p < .005.

(Bransford & Johnson 1972, JVLVB 11:717-726, [REFUTED 0-3](https://memlab.yale.edu/sites/default/files/files/1972_Bransford_Johnson_JVLVB.pdf))

**What holds regardless, and is sufficient.** Recall for **Topic Before (5.54)** was significantly superior to both **Topic After (3.18)** and **No Topic (3.22)**. **A frame given first improves retention; a frame given late does not.** That is the surviving point — and it needs no claim about felt clarity to be useful.

### The net position on ordering

- **Frame-before-details** (give the topic/schema before the content) has an independent psycholinguistic rationale (Clark & Haviland 1977; Bransford & Johnson 1972), documented in the sibling documents of this knowledge base. It is the supported construct.
- **Conclusion-first (BLUF)** is a **different construct**. This dossier establishes neither that FPLG mandates it nor that Kieras refuted it. **Treat it as a defensible convention, not an evidence-backed rule**, and never cite either source for it.
- The **practices** of answer-first ordering and paraphrase testing are defensible on other grounds (given-new/topic-first psycholinguistics; health-literacy teach-back RCTs). Only these **citations** are not.

## B5. NN/g scanning claims

### B5.1 "Screen readers do not read linearly; they scan"

**This one is not an overreach — it is a category error, and it would encode a false accessibility claim.**

The NN/g quote's subject is **"users"**: "users do not read on the Web; instead they scan the pages, trying to pick out a few sentences or even parts of sentences to get the information they want." The claim rewrote "users" as **"screen readers"** — a term of art for assistive technology (JAWS, NVDA, VoiceOver, TalkBack) — which do exactly the **opposite**: they **linearize** the page. WebAIM: "Audio interfaces present content **linearly** to users, one item at a time... audio interfaces are linearized versions of web content." W3C WCAG SC 1.3.2 (Meaningful Sequence) exists precisely because assistive technology reads content in a programmatically-determined **linear** sequence. Morkes & Nielsen never studied screen readers or blind users. (Morkes & Nielsen 1997, [REFUTED 0-3](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/concise-scannable-and-objective-how-to-write-for-the-web/))

**Never write this sentence.** Even read charitably as "sighted users on screens", the absolute form ("do not read... they scan") is disowned by NN/g's own later research.

### B5.2 "Promotional 'marketese' degrades comprehension and trust"

**What survives.** The **preference** finding: "users detest anything that seems like marketing fluff or overly hyped language ('marketese') and prefer factual information."

**What does not survive.**
- **The comprehension half is refuted by the source's own significance table.** In Morkes & Nielsen's Study 3, for the objective-vs-promotional contrast, **the only statistically significant difference is subjective satisfaction (p < .05)**. Task time, task errors, memory (recognition + recall), and sitemap time all failed to reach significance. The authors' own words: "We did not predict (nor did we find) significant differences between objective users' and control users' measures for task time, task errors, memory, or sitemap time... **THE DIFFERENCES ARE NOT SIGNIFICANT**, but they all point in the same direction."
- **"Trust" was never measured.** The satisfaction index contained no trust construct.
- **The famous "27% better" is not an inferential effect.** It is a geometric mean of *normalized* condition means across five measures, four of which were non-significant. Nielsen labels the cognitive-burden explanation "**Our conjecture** to explain this finding."
- **Independent replications went the other way.** Elgin, Jones, Anders & Farris (2001, HFES, n=80) found "**fewer** idea units were correctly recalled for the **concise** writing style than the scannable, objective, and combined writing styles" — the **opposite** direction for Nielsen's single largest effect. Jones et al. (2004, HFES): writing style did not affect memory for web content.

(Morkes & Nielsen 1997, [REFUTED 0-3](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/concise-scannable-and-objective-how-to-write-for-the-web/))

**Broader source-quality warning, carried from the synthesis pass.** The famous figures — 58% concise, 47% scannable, 27% objective, 124% combined — come from a **5-condition between-subjects study with n=51 total (~10 per cell)**, one fictitious 7-page site, one-tailed t-tests, on a composite "measured usability" geometric mean, in a **self-published Sun Microsystems / useit.com report that was never peer-reviewed**. It is 1997 dial-up-era web with a marketese-heavy control. **Do not project the magnitudes onto modern content.** (Rated *low* confidence, [vote 2-1](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/concise-scannable-and-objective-how-to-write-for-the-web/))

### B5.3 "Readers read only the first sentence of each paragraph — so every paragraph must lead with its point"

**What survives.** The observation is real and the authors' hedge is real: "Several participants, while scanning text, would read only the first sentence of each paragraph. **This suggests that** topic sentences are important, as is the 'one idea per paragraph' rule."

**What does not survive.**
- **Evidentiary weight.** The observation sits in a study the article itself labels exploratory and qualitative ("The first two studies were exploratory and qualitative"), with a handful of participants, and is backed by **one participant's opinion**, not a measurement. Comprehension of a second topic buried inside a paragraph was **never measured**. "Directly validates", "loses the second one entirely", and "**must** state" are all the claim's escalations, not the source's.
- **It was never isolated experimentally.** The only quantitative arm (Study 3) manipulated scannability as a **bundle** — bulleted lists, highlighted keywords, subheads, roughly half the word count. Topic sentences and one-idea-per-paragraph were never independently manipulated.
- **Contradicted by NN/g's own later eye-tracking.** *F-Shaped Pattern of Reading on the Web: Misunderstood, But Still Relevant* (Pernice, 2017) finds users fixate on the first few words on the left of each **LINE** — not each paragraph — and NN/g calls the F-pattern "bad for users."

(Morkes & Nielsen 1997, [REFUTED 0-3](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/concise-scannable-and-objective-how-to-write-for-the-web/))

**What the skill may still do:** leading a paragraph with its point is a reasonable writing convention with independent rationale (frame-before-details). **It may not be sold as "validated by eye-tracking" or "proven by NN/g."**

## B6. Teach-back and comprehension checks — the practice survives, the effect sizes do not

**The practice is on solid ground and lives elsewhere in this knowledge base** (Clark & Brennan on positive evidence of understanding; Griffey et al.'s ED teach-back RCT; Kemp et al. 2008: "Simply asking patients whether they understand or have any questions does not seem to be sufficient... they may not understand the situation well enough to formulate a question"). **What follows are the four numeric claims people attach to it that did not survive.**

### B6.1 "Comprehension is checked only 12% of the time, and checking predicts outcome at OR 15.15 — a bigger effect than health literacy"

**What survives — the base rate, which is the useful half.** Verbatim: "Physicians assessed recall and comprehension of any new concept in 12 (20%) of 61 visits and for **15 (12%) of 124 new concepts**." Comprehension checking is rare in observed practice.

**What does not survive — the effect size and the ranking.**
- **Inverted temporality.** The authors: "Because we used patients' **most recent** HbA1c level as an indicator of their glycemic control **rather than a test value obtained after the observed visit**, we cannot rule out the possibility that physicians' communication was affected by their patients' most recent HbA1c results." **The "outcome" temporally precedes the "exposure."** This is not a predictor of outcome; it is a cross-sectional correlate whose causal arrow may run backward.
- **The authors explicitly disclaim the magnitude:** "the small sample size limited our ability to accurately quantify the strength of this association." The claim does precisely what they say cannot be done — it quantifies *and ranks* (OR 15.15 > OR 3.97). The CIs are 2.07–110.78 vs 1.09–14.47 and **overlap across nearly their whole range**.
- The authors also state "the design of our study calls for caution in making causal inferences."

(Schillinger et al. 2003, *Closing the loop*, Arch Intern Med 163(1):83-90, [REFUTED 0-3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12523921/))

**Language caveat, carried from the source:** 74 English-speaking patients at a single San Francisco public hospital, all with low functional health literacy. **Do not generalize the odds ratio; generalize only the direction.**

### B6.2 "'Does that make sense?' is empirically weak; 'Explain how X works' is strong; and the check must not be multiple-choice"

**What survives — the effect sizes, precisely.**

| Self-explanation prompt type | g | k | N | 95% CI | Statistically detectable? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Conceptualize (explain the concept) | **0.873** | 13 | 1,678 | [0.563, 1.183] | **Yes** |
| Metacognitive (self-explain planning/performance) | 0.192 | **3** | 241 | [−0.073, 0.456] | **No** |
| Multiple-choice format | 0.242 | **2** | 204 | [−0.247, 0.731] | **No** |

Post-hoc: conceptual > metacognitive, **z = 3.284, p = .001**. (Bisra, Liu, Nesbit, Salimi & Winne 2018, *Inducing Self-Explanation: a Meta-Analysis*, Educational Psychology Review 30(3):703-725, [REFUTED 1-2](https://gwern.net/doc/psychology/spaced-repetition/2018-bisra.pdf))

**What does not survive.**
- **"The check must not be multiple-choice" is directly contradicted by the source:** "Although the overall comparison of inducement types [format] returned p = .048, post-hoc tests found **no significant differences among them**." The multiple-choice cell rests on **k = 2**.
- **The prompt examples are mismapped to the paper's coded categories.** The paper defines *metacognitive* as "the inducement asked the participant to self-explain their **planning or performance**" — **not** "Does that make sense?" or "How well do you understand this?", which are confidence/monitoring ratings and are not self-explanation prompts at all. Nothing in the k=3 studies tested them. Conversely *conceptualize* is "define or elaborate the meaning of a **named concept**", which is not the same as "Explain how X works."

**Language caveat, carried from the source:** the metacognitive cell rests on **k = 3 studies (N = 241)** — this is an **absence of evidence** as much as evidence of absence. State it as "**no detectable benefit**", never as "proven useless". English-language literature only.

**The surviving, honest rule:** asking someone to **explain the content** has a large, well-detected effect; asking them to **rate their own understanding** has no detectable effect in a very small evidence base. Do not claim more.

### B6.3 "The protégé effect is carried by the act of explaining — 'you'll have to teach this later' wears off in a week"

**What survives.** The Fiorella & Mayer (2013) numbers are verbatim: teaching vs control d = 0.82 immediate and d = 0.79 at one-week delay; preparation vs control d = 0.59 immediate but d = 0.24 (not significant) at delay.

**What does not survive.** A meta-analysis explicitly names and rejects this contention. Kobayashi (2019), *Learning by Preparing-to-Teach and Teaching: A Meta-Analysis*, Japanese Psychological Research 61(3):192-203 (28 studies): "**contrary to Fiorella and Mayer's (2013, 2014) contention**, the present research found **no evidence** suggesting that preparing-to-teach is effective only in the short term. Rather, preparing-to-teach produced a medium effect on delayed test performance." Preparing-to-teach vs control: **delayed** k=12, g = 0.53 [0.39, 0.68]; **immediate** k=20, g = 0.28 [0.20, 0.35]. **The delayed effect is significant and larger than the immediate one — the exact inverse of the decay story.** (Fiorella & Mayer 2013, [REFUTED 0-3](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0361476X13000209))

**Language/design caveat:** US undergraduates, English, one narrow STEM topic (the Doppler Effect), taught **to a video camera** rather than a live person. The "teaching" was non-interactive monologue — it isolates explaining, but the numbers license **no** claims about interactive dialogue teaching.

### B6.4 "Teach-back works, but there is no pooled effect size to cite"

**What survives.** Talevski et al. (2020) is real and its numbers are verbatim: 20 studies (9 RCTs; **4 rated high quality, 9 moderate, 7 weak**), teach-back "proved effective in **19 of the 20** studies, ranging from learning-related outcomes (e.g. knowledge recall and retention) to objective health-related outcomes (e.g. hospital re-admissions, quality of life)"; and "given the heterogeneity of outcome measures, it was not possible to conduct a meta-analysis."

**What does not survive — the universal negative.** Talevski said **that review** could not pool. The claim silently upgraded this to **the field** has no pooled estimate. It does: a meta-analysis exists (Oh, Choi, Oh & Lee, *Effectiveness of discharge education using teach-back method on readmission among heart failure patients: A systematic review and meta-analysis*, Patient Education and Counseling). **Do not say "there is no pooled effect size to cite."** (Talevski et al. 2020, PLoS ONE 15(4):e0231350, [REFUTED 0-3](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0231350))

**Language note — this one favours generality.** The 20 studies were mostly USA (n=10) and Iran (n=7), plus Australia, China, India. **Teach-back is NOT an English-only finding**, which is a point in its favour. **But** "explain it back in your own words" is a **face-threatening** request whose acceptability is culturally variable, and the review found **zero** studies that adapted or tailored teach-back to the specific patient population ("No studies reported an interpersonal focus by tailoring or adapting the teach-back intervention"). Cross-cultural phrasing is untested.

### B6.5 "People cannot self-assess comprehension — the illusion of explanatory depth proves it"

**What survives.** Rozenblit & Keil (2002) is real and the quote is verbatim: "Nearly all participants showed drops in estimates of what they knew when confronted with having to provide a real explanation, answer a diagnostic question, and compare their understanding to an expert description... A repeated measures ANOVA showed time was significant, F(4, 56) = 16.195, p < .001, η² = .536." Samples confirmed (Yale grads n=16; Yale undergrads n=33; regional non-selective state campus n=16).

**What does not survive.**
- **A hedge was deleted.** The claim asserts understanding "drops **further** after a diagnostic question." The sentences immediately after the quoted ANOVA read: "**The contrasts for Time 2 versus Time 3, and Time 2 versus Time 4 were not significant**", and the authors' own summary: "Attempts to answer the diagnostic questions led to a further (**non-significant**) decrease." The claim took the word the authors put in parentheses and deleted it.
- **The universality is contradicted by the source itself.** Abstract: "The illusion is **far stronger for explanatory knowledge than many other kinds of knowledge**, such as that for facts, procedures or narratives." For procedures: "there was **no drop** in the self-ratings across time." So "People cannot self-assess comprehension" and "**any** check that relies on the listener's confidence signal is invalid" are both overreaches.

(Rozenblit & Keil 2002, Cognitive Science 26(5):521-562, [REFUTED 0-3](https://cogdevlab.yale.edu/sites/default/files/files/rozenblit%20&%20keil%20%202002.pdf))

**Language caveat:** US university students tested in English on Western consumer artifacts (zipper, flush toilet, cylinder lock, helicopter). Do not port the numeric drop to other populations.

**The defensible statement:** self-rated understanding of **how a mechanism works** is inflated and collapses when the person must actually explain it. This does **not** extend to facts, procedures, or narratives, and it does not make every confidence signal invalid — it makes confidence signals **weak** (see B4.4: metacomprehension gamma ≈ .27).

## B7. Kintsch — bottom-up processing, and the concrete-context story

### B7.1 "Comprehension is strictly bottom-up: context does not pre-filter ambiguity"

**What survives.** The quote is verbatim, and it describes **Kintsch's model**: "In contrast to expectation-based, predictive views of discourse comprehension, **a model is developed** in which the initial processing is strictly bottom-up."

**What does not survive.** That is a statement about a **proposed model's architecture**, not an empirical finding about the human parser — and Kintsch framed the bottom-up construction phase as a deliberate simplifying commitment: "The integration phase is **the price the model pays** for the necessary flexibility in the construction process." The claim converts "Kintsch's CI model **posits** X" into "X **is** how comprehension works."

**And it is contradicted by the modern consensus.** Altmann & Kamide (1999, Cognition): listeners launch **anticipatory** eye movements to "cake" at the verb "eat", *before* the noun is heard — context pre-restricts the referential domain ahead of bottom-up input. Kuperberg & Jaeger (2016, *What do we mean by prediction in language comprehension?*, Language, Cognition and Neuroscience 31(1):32-59): prediction/pre-activation is the modern consensus. (Kintsch 1988, Psychological Review 95(2):163-182, [REFUTED 0-3](https://condor.depaul.edu/dallbrit/extra/hon207/readings/kintsch-1988-construction-integration.pdf))

**Note what this does NOT touch.** Kintsch's *other* claim — that comprehension runs in cycles with a small buffer, so verbosity lengthens the distance over which a critical earlier proposition must survive — is a separate finding and is not refuted here. Keep them apart.

### B7.2 "Concrete, familiar framing makes formally identical content dramatically easier — five birds and three worms vs five red and three green marbles"

**What survives.** The quote is verbatim and Kintsch really does write it.

**What does not survive — and the refutation is devastating because the confound is in the primary study Kintsch is summarizing.** Hudson (1983, *Child Development* 54:84-90) held the **birds-and-worms content constant** across both conditions and varied **only the question wording**:

| Question (same birds-and-worms display) | Nursery | Kindergarten | 1st grade |
|---|---|---|---|
| "How many **more** birds than worms are there?" | 17% | 25% | 64% |
| "Suppose the birds all race over and each one tries to get a worm... How many birds **won't get** a worm?" | 83% | 96% | 100% |

**Concreteness was the CONTROL variable, not the manipulation.** It was held fixed while performance swung ~60 points. The active ingredient is the **comparative construction**, not the concreteness of the scenario. The assertion that "world knowledge about birds and worms supplies the whole/part structure" is a misattribution. (Kintsch 1988, [REFUTED 0-3](https://condor.depaul.edu/dallbrit/extra/hon207/readings/kintsch-1988-construction-integration.pdf))

**The right lesson is a better lesson.** The lever is not "make it concrete." The lever is **the linguistic form of the question/relation** — comparative constructions ("how many more X than Y") are genuinely harder than action constructions ("who won't get one"), even with identical content and identical arithmetic. That is a *language* finding, and it is far more actionable than "use a vivid example."

### B7.3 Concreteness fading — the sequence rule is theoretical, not established

**The popular claim:** the concrete-vs-abstract debate is a false dichotomy; the evidence-backed rule is a **sequence** — start concrete, then explicitly and gradually fade to the abstract — and Fyfe et al. name four **mechanisms**.

**What survives.** The quote is verbatim, and the "false dichotomy / combine their advantages" framing is a faithful reading of the abstract.

**What does not survive.**
- **A hedge was stripped.** The abstract's own words are "**Theoretical** benefits of this 'concreteness fading' technique" and "**we argue for**" / "**we recommend**." The claim converts these into "**the evidence-backed** recommendation" and "four **mechanisms**." Fyfe & Nathan (2019) later concede concreteness fading is an **underspecified** theory with "critical gaps."
- **"Neither pole alone wins" is contradicted.** Jaakkola & Veermans (2018, Instructional Science, N=127, electric circuits): "learning with **constantly concrete** representations either took less time or resulted in **better** learning compared to concreteness fading."

(Fyfe, McNeil, Son & Goldstone 2014, Educational Psychology Review 26(1):9-25, [REFUTED 0-3](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ1036777))

**What the skill may say:** ordering a concrete example before the abstract principle, and making the abstraction step explicit, is a **reasonable recommendation put forward by researchers** — not a demonstrated mechanism, and not a universal.

## B8. Bell's audience-design hierarchy

### The popular claim

Style is designed for the audience, graded by role distance (addressee > auditor > overhearer > eavesdropper); non-audience factors (topic, setting) derive their force by association with addressee types; and "who is listening" — not Labov's "attention paid to speech" — is the primary controllable lever.

### What survives

The **attribution** is textually accurate. Bell (1984)'s abstract really does say "Style is essentially speakers' response to their audience. In audience design, speakers accommodate primarily to their addressee. Third persons — auditors and overhearers — affect style to a lesser but regular degree... Nonaudience factors like topic and setting derive their effect by association with addressee types." And §II is literally headed **"A NONSTARTER: ATTENTION PAID TO SPEECH"**, containing "Empirical foundation for the attention variable is **notably lacking**, however."

The natural-experiment core is also real: New Zealand newscasters reading the **same news from the same studios** shifted intervocalic /t/-voicing systematically between a higher-status (YA) and a lower-status (ZB) audience.

### What does not survive

- **Bell himself retracted the topic/setting sub-claim.** In *Back in style: reworking audience design* (2001), revisiting his own point 7, he writes: "The hypothesis that shifts according to topic echo shifts according to audience was one of the '**bolder**' ones put forward in Bell (1984)... **The evidence is, however, not clear**, nor is it obvious [how to test it]."
- **The quoted abstract was cherry-picked by truncation.** The very next sentences read: "These style shifts are mainly **responsive**... Speakers can also use style as **initiative**, to redefine the existing situation. Initiative style is primarily **referee design**: divergence from the addressee and towards an absent reference group." **Bell 1984 is a TWO-dimension theory.** Quoting only the responsive half and then calling audience "the primary lever" misrepresents it.
- **The newscaster evidence is thinner than it sounds:** n = 4 newscasters, 1974 data, no inferential statistics on the key figure; "only the audiences differ" is **Bell's inference**, not an independently validated fact (the stations plausibly differ in format and stylebook policy); and Bell immediately concedes it is "not a case of simple convergence."
- The hierarchy itself is empirically contested (e.g. Sharma 2018, *Style dominance: Attention, audience, and the 'real me'*, Language in Society 47(1):1-31).

(Bell 1984, *Language style as audience design*, Language in Society 13:145-204, [REFUTED 1-2](https://web.stanford.edu/~eckert/PDF/bell1984.pdf))

### Language caveat

**Bell's /t/-voicing variable is New Zealand English-specific.** The *principle* that "who is listening" is a real lever on how one speaks is language-general and usable; **the graded hierarchy and the specific variable are not** to be presented as established.

## B9. Readability — the universal negatives that went too far

**The rule that survives, and must be kept:** readability formulas are **not validated as a measure of real-time processing ease** and must **never** be used as a *target metric* or as *proof of clarity*. That is well supported (Bailin & Grafstein: "the criteria commonly used in readability formulae do not constitute a satisfactory basis for assessing reading difficulty"; the ISO 24495-1 working group declined to endorse readability formulas; the Government of Canada Language Portal: "readability formulas cannot match material to readers at targeted grade levels").

**What follows are the three stronger forms that failed.**

### B9.1 "Every class of readability method — including frontier LLMs — is a poor predictor, therefore readability scores cannot be treated as a valid proxy at all"

**What survives.** The narrow finding, with its own scope restriction intact: the models and formulas evaluated "are all poor predictors of reading ease **in English**." The model list (Flesch, Flesch-Kincaid, Dale-Chall, Gunning Fog, ARI, Coleman-Liau; Coh-Metrix L2, CAREC, CARES, Sentence-BERT; GPT-4o, GPT-5, Gemini 2.0-Flash, Gemini 2.5 Pro, Llama 3.3 70B, Claude Sonnet 4; Lexile, TextEvaluator) checks out.

**What does not survive.**
- **The blanket universal.** "Cannot be treated as a valid proxy" is a strong universal negative sourced to a **single unrefereed arXiv preprint on a single English corpus** (OneStop Eye Movements, 360 English L1 adults, Guardian paragraphs).
- **The "in English" scope was stripped.** The paper's own abstract restricts the finding to English. The claim dropped it.
- **Contradicted by peer-reviewed eye-tracking.** Nahatame (2021, *Language Learning* 71(4):1004-1043): "although traditional formulas showed reliable performance in [predicting eye-movement measures]..." — the opposite conclusion, in a refereed journal.

(Gruteke Klein, Frenkel, Shubi & Berzak, arXiv:2502.11150, [REFUTED 0-3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.11150))

### B9.2 "No reader-independent text score can predict whether a given audience will understand a given text"

**What survives.** Bailin & Grafstein's two named failure points are faithfully quoted: (a) the formulae's notion of linguistic complexity is not supported by linguistic theory, and (b) formulae assume readers' vocabulary and background knowledge are **homogeneous** and collapsible into a single score.

**What does not survive.** The **impossibility result** the claim manufactures. Neither the quote nor Bailin & Grafstein assert a universal negative about predictability — their position is that formulas measure the **wrong text properties**, not that text-side prediction has zero validity (their 2016 book is literally an attempt to build a better theory of readability). And it is empirically contradicted by the strongest anti-formula study available: Cunningham, Hiebert & Mesmer (2018, *Reading and Writing* 31(4)), testing Lexile and Flesch-Kincaid against Bormuth's mean-cloze comprehension criterion over 330 passages, found **Flesch-Kincaid r = −.76 (r² = .57)** and **Lexile r = −.70 (r² = .49)**. That is not zero validity.

**Citation hygiene:** the quote is from the **preface of the authors' 2016 Palgrave monograph**, not from the 2001 *Language & Communication* article the claim cites. (Bailin & Grafstein 2001, [REFUTED 0-3](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ629584))

### B9.3 "Surprisal, word length and word frequency give you concrete rewrite levers"

**What survives — the descriptive half.** "existing methods are poor predictors of reading ease, outperformed by entropy and the 'big three' text properties, word length, frequency and especially surprisal", and "**The best predictor overall tends to be surprisal**."

**What does not survive — the prescriptive half, which is the whole payload.**
- **Measurement → intervention leap.** The paper proposes surprisal as an unsupervised **scoring measure**. It **never tests whether rewriting** text to be shorter / more frequent / lower-surprisal improves anything. Turning a correlational eye-tracking measure into a writing target is **exactly the Goodhart failure that discredited readability formulas in the first place** — the same critique this paper leans on.
- **Construct swap.** Reading time ≠ understanding.
- **The measure itself is unstable.** Oh & Schuler (TACL 2023): larger, lower-perplexity language models yield surprisal that fits human reading times **worse**, monotonically — so an LLM's own logprobs are a moving target.

(Gruteke Klein et al., arXiv:2502.11150, [REFUTED 1-2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.11150))

### B9.4 The two "language-neutral mechanical signal" substitutes also fail

Two attempts to replace readability with something mechanically computable and cross-linguistic were both **REFUTED 0-3**:

**Dependency length minimization.** The finding is real and genuinely cross-linguistic (Futrell, Mahowald & Gibson 2015, PNAS: 37 languages, 10 families, real sentences have shorter dependency lengths than random baselines). **But it is not language-neutral in the way a coach needs.** The paper itself: "the head-final languages such as Japanese, Korean, and Turkish show **much less minimization** than more head initial languages such as Italian, Indonesian, and Irish, which are apparently highly optimized." **Effect size varies ~6× across languages. No fixed cross-language threshold is valid.** Any use must compare against a **per-language corpus baseline**, never an absolute number — and it is corpus evidence about what speakers **produce**, not a comprehension measure. ([REFUTED 0-3](https://sites.socsci.uci.edu/~rfutrell/papers/futrell2015largescale.pdf))

**"Shorten your dependencies" is not even a safe rule within a language.** In German verb-final main clauses, *adding* a preverbal dependent — **lengthening** the subject-verb dependency — makes the final verb **faster** to read (anti-locality). **But do not cite this as a falsifier either:** the source (Levy & Keller 2013) is the canonical case study of a psycholinguistic finding that **failed to replicate** — Vasishth, Mertzen, Jäger & Gelman (2018, JML 103:151-175) ran **seven direct replication attempts (268 participants)** and concluded "the published claims are so noisy that even non-significant results are fully compatible with them." ([REFUTED 1-2](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3928089/))

**Linear surprisal→reading-time linking.** A claim that the linear link "survives cross-linguistically... in every one of the 11 languages" was **REFUTED 0-3**: it is a **null result** (failure to reject nonlinearity on a small corpus), the paper's own hedge notes the linearity may be an artifact of weaker LMs, and it is directly contradicted on overlapping languages by Xu, Chon, Liu & Futrell (2023, Findings of EMNLP), who find evidence for **superlinearity**. The wider literature has concluded sublinear (Brothers & Kuperberg 2021), linear (Shain et al. 2022), and superlinear (Meister et al. 2021; Hoover et al. 2022) — **it is contested.** ([REFUTED 0-3](https://arxiv.org/html/2307.03667v4))

### Operational rule for the skill

- **Never use a readability score as a target metric or as proof of clarity.** This survives.
- **Do not claim readability formulas have zero predictive validity.** They correlate meaningfully with cloze comprehension (r² ≈ .5). They are the *wrong instrument for the job*, not a random number generator.
- **Do not say "LLMs cannot assess readability."** The surviving claim is narrower: LLM readability scores are **not validated against real-time processing ease**.
- **All readability formulas are English artifacts.** The 2025 eye-tracking paper restricts its own finding to English. Do not port grade-level targets across languages.
- **Do not adopt surprisal, dependency length, or any single computable score as a rewrite target.** Each was tried and each failed on the same fault line: a measurement is not an intervention.

## B10. Grounding and the given-new contract — four over-tellings

The foundational findings (grounding criterion; least collaborative effort; positive evidence of understanding; frame-before-details) are strong and live elsewhere. **These four elaborations are not.**

### B10.1 "Speakers spontaneously chunk-then-confirm; customers echoed numbers back over 70% of the time"

**What survives.** The Clark & Brennan quote is genuine ("Dividing a presentation into repeatable installments is based on the tacit recognition that people have limited immediate memory spans... British operators **always** divided numbers of seven or more digits into their conventional groupings").

**What does not survive.**
- **"Over 70%" is fabricated precision. The real number is 67.7%.** Clark & Schaefer (1987), Table 2 (N=502): "display full hearing" = **67.7%**, below 70%. "Over 70%" is only reachable by hand-summing non-equivalent move categories, an aggregation the authors never perform — and the claim's own supporting quote contains no percentage at all.
- **Prevalence overstated:** chunk-then-confirm was the **minority** strategy, reserved for trouble — not the default pattern the claim describes.

(Clark & Brennan 1991, [REFUTED 1-2](https://www.cs.cmu.edu/~illah/CLASSDOCS/Clark91.pdf))

### B10.2 "The eight medium constraints and eleven costs yield a falsifiable prediction confirmed by Cohen (1984)"

**What survives — and it is a lot.** The eight constraints (copresence, visibility, audibility, cotemporality, simultaneity, sequentiality, reviewability, revisability) and eleven costs (formulation, production, reception, understanding, start-up, delay, asynchrony, speaker change, display, fault, repair) are **verbatim exact**. So is "Our prediction is straightforward: People should ground with those techniques available in a medium that lead to the least collaborative effort."

**What does not survive — the epistemic framing.** "A falsifiable prediction **confirmed by** Cohen (1984)" misrepresents the source: Clark & Brennan present it as a **post-hoc account** of a difference Cohen *describes* ("This prediction would **account for** the difference Cohen describes"), not as a prediction they made and then confirmed. Monk notes the framework "has yet to be fleshed out in sufficient detail" to yield mechanical predictions. (Clark & Brennan 1991, [REFUTED 1-2](https://www.cs.cmu.edu/~illah/CLASSDOCS/Clark91.pdf))

**Keep the taxonomy. Drop the "confirmed" verb.**

### B10.3 "Every referring expression must resolve to one and only one antecedent the audience already holds"

**What survives.** The "Maxim of Antecedence" is real and verbatim, and Clark & Haviland do place it under Grice (specifically the maxim of **Manner**). Uniqueness and Computability are genuine stated conditions, and Table 2 does map their violation to "unacceptable" / "unacceptable or incomprehensible in context".

**What does not survive — the mechanically checkable prescription, contradicted by the same paper.** Table 2, section A: "Maxim of antecedence — 1. Violation by speaker: **ALLOWED under special circumstances.** 2. Strategy of listener encountering violation: a. Bridging b. Addition. 3. Consequence of violation: **Listener draws an implicature.**" And in the text: "**the speaker does not have to provide given information with a direct antecedent**"; "suppose the speaker has violated the maxim and the [listener] cannot find a direct antecedent. Then, we assume, the listener can turn to one of three procedures: bridging, addition, or restructuring."

**Violation predicts repair, not incomprehensibility.** A rule saying "every referring expression MUST resolve to exactly one antecedent" inverts the source. (Clark & Haviland 1977, [REFUTED 1-2](http://www.web.stanford.edu/~clark/1970s/Clark,%20H.H.%20_%20Haviland,%20S.E.%20_Comprehension%20and%20the%20given-new%20contract_%201977.pdf))

### B10.4 "Bridging inferences cost a measurable, replicated 140-180 ms per sentence"

**What survives.** The numbers are exact: Haviland & Clark (1974), Exp I — 68 context-target pairs, 16 subjects, **835 ms** (direct antecedent) vs **1016 ms** (indirect), a 181 ms difference, minF'(1,36)=6.47, p<.025. Exp II, controlling for lexical repetition — **1031 ms** vs **1168 ms**, a 137 ms difference, minF'(1,23)=15.7, p<.001.

**What does not survive — the authors themselves disclaim the quantitative reading.** Haviland & Clark (1974), General Discussion, verbatim: "there may have been some instances of 'incomplete' contextual comprehension, and so **the absolute differences between conditions cannot be taken very seriously**." A skill that ships "leaving an inferential gap costs the reader ~150 ms" is quoting a number the authors explicitly said not to take seriously as a magnitude. (Clark & Haviland 1977, [REFUTED 1-2](http://www.web.stanford.edu/~clark/1970s/Clark,%20H.H.%20_%20Haviland,%20S.E.%20_Comprehension%20and%20the%20given-new%20contract_%201977.pdf))

**The direction survives; the magnitude does not.** Say "bridging inferences cost the reader measurable extra processing time." Do not say "≈150 ms per sentence."

## B11. Egocentric anchoring — three over-tellings

### B11.1 "Audience design is not part of initial planning; perspective-taking is an optional second stage that drops out under time pressure"

**What survives.** Horton & Keysar (1996, *Cognition* 59(1):91-117) is real and the abstract is verbatim: "While descriptions under no time constraints appeared to incorporate common ground with the listener, common ground was **not** used when the speakers were under time pressure."

**What does not survive.**
- **The first author abandoned the model.** Horton & Gerrig (2016, *Revisiting the Memory-Based Processing Approach to Common Ground*, TopiCS): speakers "could both strategically **and automatically** gain access to information about others through domain-general memory processes." Automatic access is flatly incompatible with "an optional, resource-dependent stage."
- **Directly contradicted on the exact construct.** Lockridge & Brennan (2002, Psychonomic Bulletin & Review): addressees' needs influence speakers' **early syntactic** choices. Vanlangendonck, Willems, Menenti & Hagoort (2016): "common ground exerts an **early**, but incomplete effect" during speech planning.
- **It is one side of a 30-year live debate** (monitoring-and-adjustment vs constraint-based/memory-based accounts), presented as settled fact.

(Horton & Keysar 1996, [REFUTED 0-3](https://europepmc.org/article/MED/8857472))

### B11.2 "Mutual knowledge is error correction applied after an egocentric first pass"

**What survives — the numbers, exactly.** Keysar, Barr, Balin & Brauner (2000, *Psychological Science* 11(1):32-38, n=20): initial fixation on the shared referent **delayed by 503 ms** in the test condition, t(18)=2.5, p<.05; the decision fixation **delayed by 1,449 ms**, t(20)=4.2, p<.0001; and "the addressee's hand actually reached for the occluded object in **23% of the trials** in the test condition, compared with **none** in the control condition, t(20)=4.4, p<.01."

**What does not survive — the architectural claim.** The paper explicitly refuses that strength: the abstract says people "**occasionally** use an egocentric heuristic", and p.37 states outright: **"Our experiments cannot distinguish between the no-constraint and the partial-constraint theories."** The claim asserts as settled architecture the very thing the authors say their experiments cannot decide. (Keysar et al. 2000, [REFUTED 0-3](https://thegricean.github.io/xprag-ling247/readings/keysar2000.pdf))

**Language caveat:** "Twenty **native English speakers** participated as addressees." A co-present visual referential game with a confederate director. **Do not generalize to other languages, or to non-co-present (written/asynchronous) communication.**

### B11.3 What you may still say

That listeners and speakers both anchor egocentrically and correct **imperfectly and sometimes too late** — with the 503 ms / 1,449 ms / 23% figures as illustration, and the "occasionally" hedge attached. Not: "common ground is never used in initial planning", and not: "mutual knowledge is *only* error correction."

## B12. Repair universality — keep the mechanism, drop the etiology

**What survives (and it is one of the strongest language-neutral findings in the whole knowledge base):** other-initiated repair is universal conversational infrastructure. "Sequences of other-initiated repair have been identified in **every spoken language investigated so far**"; "**no language appears to lack an interjection** for this function." Documented across 10 languages studied in depth (5 continents) plus 21 from published sources, and 12 languages from 8 families in the follow-up, used on average about **once per 1.4 minutes** in every language.

**What does not survive: the causal story about *why*.** A claim that the cross-linguistic similarity "is explained by convergent cultural evolution under interactional selective pressure, **not by innateness**" was **REFUTED 1-2** — because the authors explicitly decline to establish it:
- Their own FAQ: "**Does that mean we've demonstrated it's not innate? No, merely that the evidence militates against it.**"
- The paper's Discussion: "In the absence of historical language data it is **impossible to tell** whether the present-day forms go back to one ancestral form... or whether they arose independently", and they "have to **remain agnostic** as to its ultimate origins."
- Convergent evolution is introduced as "**A MORE PLAUSIBLE MECHANISM**... This **PROPOSAL** sees huh? not as an arbitrary grunt but as a product of cultural evolution" — preferred on grounds of **parsimony**, not demonstrated.

(Dingemanse, Torreira & Enfield 2013, PLoS ONE 8(11):e78273, [REFUTED 1-2](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0078273))

**Also carry these caveats on the surviving finding:** it is an **inductive generalization** over ~10+21 and 12 languages out of ~7,000, and the authors hedge with "likely to be attested" — do not state it as proven-in-all. Universality of the **mechanism** does **not** entail cross-cultural uniformity in **willingness** to initiate repair; face and politeness norms modulate this heavily.

**The usable rule:** "signal trouble → speaker repeats or reformulates" is language-neutral infrastructure a communication skill can rely on. **Combined with the feign-understanding finding: silence is not evidence of understanding, in any language.**

## B13. Grice's maxims are not rules to be maximally obeyed — but the "only flouting generates meaning" version is also wrong

**What survives.** The four-way taxonomy is verbatim: a participant may quietly **VIOLATE** a maxim ("in some cases he will be liable to mislead"); **OPT OUT**; face a **CLASH**; or **FLOUT** a maxim ("blatantly fail to fulfill it"), at which point Grice says the maxim is being **EXPLOITED**. Irony, metaphor ("You are the cream in my coffee"), meiosis, and hyperbole ("Every nice girl loves a sailor") are Grice's own examples of floutings of the first maxim of Quality.

**What does not survive.** "**Only** flouting generates conversational implicature" is false by Grice's own organization. He divides the examples into **three** groups, and implicature arises in all three — **Group A is literally titled "Examples in which no maxim is violated, or at least in which it is not clear that any maxim is violated"** (the out-of-petrol/garage exchange yields an implicature with no maxim broken). (Grice 1975, *Logic and Conversation*, p.49, [REFUTED 1-2](https://lawandlogic.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/grice1975logic-and-conversation.pdf))

**What the skill may say:** the maxims are descriptive expectations, not commandments; a system that treats them as rules to be maximally obeyed loses the mechanism by which non-literal meaning is generated. **What it may not say:** that meaning arises *only* when maxims are broken.

## B14. Jargon — the finding is real, but "a definition does not rescue it" is not what was tested

**What survives — the numbers, exactly.** In a 2 (jargon vs no-jargon) × 2 (definitions vs no-definitions) between-subjects experiment (N=650, US general-population Qualtrics panel):

| Effect | Statistic |
|---|---|
| Main effect of **jargon** on processing fluency | F(1, 636) = 76.03, p < .001, **η² = .11** |
| Jargon condition | M = 4.57 (SD 1.11) |
| No-jargon condition | M = 5.27 (SD 0.90) |
| Main effect of **definitions** | F(1, 636) = 0.37, **p = .543**, η² = .0005 (none) |
| Jargon × definitions **interaction** | F(1, 636) = 0.17, **p = .678**, η² = .0002 (none) |

(Bullock, Colón Amill, Shulman & Dixon 2019, *Jargon as a barrier to effective science communication*, Public Understanding of Science 28(7):845-853, [REFUTED 1-2](https://comm.osu.edu/sites/comm.osu.edu/files/PUS%202019-%20Bullock%20et%20al..pdf))

**What does not survive — the skill rule as stated: "A parenthetical gloss does not buy back the cost."** The definitions condition was **not a parenthetical gloss**. It was **optional mouseover text**: "participants were told they could scroll over underlined [terms]". An unused optional affordance failing to help is not evidence that an inline gloss fails to help. **The study never tested a parenthetical definition.**

**Language caveat — English-specific artifact risk.** Jargon was operationalized as English technical terms plus **acronyms**, and the no-jargon condition replaced acronyms with their full form. Processing fluency was measured with items like "A lot of the terms felt familiar to me." **English scientific vocabulary is largely opaque Greco-Latinate borrowing**, so an English jargon term is phonologically and morphologically alien to a lay reader. In languages whose technical vocabulary is built from native, transparent morphemes, the fluency penalty may be smaller. **Do not generalize the magnitude.**

**The defensible rule:** unfamiliar technical terms measurably reduce how *fluent* a text feels, and that felt difficulty is consequential. **Prefer replacing the term with plain wording.** Do **not** claim that defining a term has been shown not to help — what was shown is that an *optional mouseover* definition did not help.

## What this does NOT license

**Read this section as a blocklist.** Each bullet below opens with a claim you must **not** make, followed by why it fails. Nothing in this section is an endorsement. Every entry is blocked by a refuted block above or by the dossier's binding language caveats.

**On the three famous myths**
- "Words are only 7% of your message." Mehrabian himself: "the verbal part is **nearly 100%** of the message" for a factual message; the general reading is "absurd."
- Replacing 7-38-55 with any other channel-weighting constant. No such constant is supported here.
- "Mehrabian's study proves nonverbal dominance." Its two scope conditions — **feelings/attitudes** *and* **inconsistent channels** — are the author's own, and both must hold.
- "The 7% figure is an artifact of holding words constant." (REFUTED 0-3 — the .07 comes from the *companion* study, which did vary words.)
- "You have N seconds before they tune out." No seconds-valued attention span is supported; the defensible construct operates over **tens of minutes**.
- "Microsoft's report actually debunks the goldfish myth." (REFUTED 0-3 — it is the vector that spread it.)
- "No researcher has ever quantified attention span." (The 2-1 dissent produced a counter-example; the correct claim is that the *average attention span* framing is task-dependent and the 8-second number has no study behind it.)
- "Learning styles don't exist." Preferences exist and are reported; aptitudes differ. The **meshing hypothesis** is what fails.
- Withholding a diagram, or withholding text, because of someone's self-declared style.

**On memory and structure**
- "Working memory holds 4 slots." / "Never more than 4 bullets." / "A live audience can only hold 3 things."
- "Reduce extraneous cognitive load" as an audience-independent rule. It has no definition without fixing *who* is listening.
- "Closer is always better" for labels. **Falsified** — maximum proximity underperformed medium proximity in the documented spatial-contiguity failure.
- Quoting any Mayer box-score (23/23, d=0.86, 22/22, d=1.10) as an established effect size.
- "This works because it reduces cognitive load." The behavioural effects survive; the load mechanism does not.
- Stating redundancy or contiguity unconditionally: both have boundary conditions their own sources print (experienced learners; short on-screen text; no graphics; low element interactivity).

**On writing rules**
- "The Federal Plain Language Guidelines mandate BLUF." No mandate; the statute has **no judicial review and no enforceability**.
- "150-word paragraphs, max 250" as a hard rule. The hedge is "**Writing experts recommend**", and the same page says "**There is nothing wrong with an occasional one-sentence paragraph.**"
- "One idea per sentence is automatically checkable." It is an irreducibly semantic judgment with no operational definition in the source.
- "Kieras proved BLUF doesn't work." He never moved a conclusion; he deleted it — and the passage where the conclusion was deleted was the only one whose readers failed to extract the conclusion.
- "Self-reported clarity is an invalid proxy." It is **weak** (metacomprehension gamma ≈ .27), not invalid.
- "Users don't read, they scan." Disowned by NN/g's own later research; and **never** attribute scanning to "screen readers", which linearize.
- "Marketese degrades comprehension and trust." Only **subjective satisfaction** reached significance; trust was never measured; the concise effect **reversed** in independent replication.
- "Readability formulas have no predictive validity." They correlate with cloze comprehension around r² ≈ .5. They are the wrong instrument, not noise.
- "LLMs cannot assess readability." The surviving claim is only that LLM readability scores are **not validated against real-time processing ease**.
- Using **any** computable score — readability, surprisal, dependency length — as a rewrite **target**. Every attempt in this dossier failed on the same fault line: a measurement is not an intervention.

**On checking understanding**
- "Teach-back raises comprehension by OR 15.15." The authors disclaim the magnitude and the outcome preceded the exposure.
- "There is no pooled effect size for teach-back." One exists.
- "The comprehension check must not be multiple-choice." The source found **no significant differences among formats**.
- "Telling someone they'll teach it later has no lasting benefit." A 28-study meta-analysis found the opposite (delayed g = 0.53 > immediate g = 0.28).
- "People cannot self-assess comprehension." The illusion of explanatory depth is "**far stronger for explanatory knowledge than many other kinds**"; there was **no drop** for procedures.

**On language scope — English-specific artifacts, never to be generalized**
End-of-sentence focal stress; cleft/pseudocleft/passive information-packaging; definite-NP-first tendencies; **all readability formulas** (the 2025 eye-tracking paper restricts its own finding to English); ISO 24495-1's worked examples (ISO itself says the examples are English-only); Bell's /t/-voicing variable; the FBICIAUSA chunking illustration; English backchannel tokens ("uh huh", "yeah"); Mehrabian's tone manipulation ("variations of pitch and stress which are **linguistically unnecessary**" — a description that is simply false for a tone language); English jargon's Greco-Latinate opacity; the "initial mention = theme" convention of English expository prose (contrast *ki-shō-ten-ketsu*, *qǐ-chéng-zhuǎn-hé*); reading-vs-listening operationalizations that depend on English's deep orthography.

**On language scope — established cross-linguistically, and usable**
The curse of knowledge; chunk-relative capacity; grounding and positive-evidence-seeking; **repair infrastructure** (documented across 8+ language families, ~once per 1.4 minutes); audience design **as a principle**; frame-before-details; spatial/temporal contiguity; teach-back (studies across the USA, Iran, Australia, China, India — though its **face-threat** is culturally variable and **no** study has tested a culturally adapted version).

**Finally — the meta-rule this document exists to enforce**
- Never let a rule inherit more confidence than its source. The academic layer here is strong (primary, peer-reviewed, quote-verified against actual PDFs). The **practitioner** layer is much weaker: the two most-cited practitioner sources both wobbled — the NN/g concise/scannable/objective study is a non-peer-reviewed 1997 vendor report with ~10 participants per cell whose largest effect **reversed** in independent replication, and the most-quoted Mayer effect sizes did not clear verification. When you cannot cite it, do not assert it.
